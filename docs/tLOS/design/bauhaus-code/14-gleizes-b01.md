### Key Biographical Facts Relevant to Theory

Selected entries bearing on the development of Gleizes's theoretical position:

- **1906–1910**: Travel through Morocco; involvement with Abbaye de Créteil (communal artistic community, founded 1906) — context for his early theory of collective vs. individual artistic production.
- **1912**: Exhibition of "Du Cubisme" — first published theoretical statement of Cubism, co-authored with Jean Metzinger. First theoretical formalization of simultane Ansicht.
- **1913**: Exhibits at Armory Show, New York — first US exposure to Cubism.
- **1917**: Returns to New York; visits Cuba, visits Bermuda.
- **1927–1925**: Performs mural paintings; first formulation of theories of plastic construction.
- **1931–1941**: Reintroduction of rigorous brush work; theoretical writings. Permanently moves to St.-Rémy-de-Provence.
- **1947**: Major retrospective exhibition at Lyon, Chapelle des Loges, Avignon.
- **1948–1950**: Awarded Grand Prix of the first French Biennial, Moscow.
- **1953**: Died, Avignon, June 23.

## Section: Essay — "Albert Gleizes: Reason and Faith in Modern Painting" by Daniel Robbins (PDF pages 13–19)

### Argument Structure
**Thesis**: Gleizes was not primarily an intuitive painter like Picasso or Braque but a systematic theorist of Cubism whose contribution was to articulate Cubism as a theory of consciousness encountering form — and to connect this theory to social, spiritual, and architectural concerns beyond the canvas.
**Evidence**: Biographical evidence (Abbaye de Créteil, intellectual circle), comparison with Picasso/Braque, analysis of paintings (The City and the River, Portrait works), quotations from Gleizes's own writings and from contemporary critics, the development of "Du Cubisme" (1912), Gleizes's engagement with Apollinaire, Metzinger, Le Fauconnier.
**Conclusion**: Gleizes's Cubism is a rational system for perceiving form across time — not a decorative or expressive style. It demands of the viewer an active, temporally extended engagement. This distinguishes him from both the intuitive Cubists (Picasso, Braque) and the decorative abstractionists.

### Principles

**PRINCIPLE [P-01]**: simultane Ansicht (simultaneous view) / simultaneous viewpoint
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: The core Cubist operation, as described through Robbins's account of Gleizes's theory, is the coexistence of multiple viewpoints on a single picture surface. This is not a synthesis into a new unified view but the literal, simultaneous presence of distinct sensation-states. Robbins writes that Gleizes's paintings of 1910–1913 demonstrate "analysis of volume relationships."
- **Rule**: When composing a surface, do not resolve multiple viewpoints into a single coherent image — allow distinct sensation-states to coexist as separate, articulated zones.
- **Source**: pp.17–19
- **Quote (EN)**: "Gleizes considered the analytical Cubist works of Picasso and Braque, these figures of intersecting planes, as 'an improvisation of form' because of the emphasis on relationships and rhythms set up by parts of a dissected subject."
- **Category**: viewpoint | sensation

**PRINCIPLE [P-02]**: Form als Gesetz / Form as Law — the essential vs. the appearance
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: Gleizes, as summarized by Robbins, was not interested in depicting objects as they appear from a fixed viewpoint but in discovering "das Wesentliche" — the law governing an object's form. Robbins describes this as Gleizes's concern with "the structure of weight, density and volumetric relationships among parts of a frontal subject." The painting does not represent; it reveals the formal law.
- **Rule**: Seek the structural law of the object — the pattern of formal relationships that governs its behavior under all viewpoints — rather than its appearance from any single position.
- **Source**: pp.18–19
- **Quote (EN)**: "He realized that the visible world is not the same as the space of a perspective painting, and accepting the conviction that perception of the physical world is deformed by the effects of distance, Gleizes' artistic concern was to reconstitute and synthesize the real world according to his individual consciousness."
- **Category**: form | sensation | theory

**PRINCIPLE [P-03]**: Kontinuität der Sensation / Continuity of sensation — painting as temporal not instantaneous
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: Robbins traces Gleizes's theoretical position to the claim that consciousness encounters objects temporally — not as an instantaneous snapshot but as a continuous movement through an encounter. This is why Gleizes's paintings demand active looking: the eye must traverse the surface zones sequentially, each zone constituting a distinct moment in the continuous encounter.
- **Rule**: Design surfaces so that the eye moves through them in sequence — each zone should initiate a new moment of encounter, not resolve into a single instantaneous reading.
- **Source**: pp.17–18
- **Quote (EN)**: "A tentative precedent perhaps existed in Courbet's *Rêal Allegory* which, however, merely seeking to know the visible world by the operation of thought."
- **Category**: temporality | sensation | form

**PRINCIPLE [P-04]**: Beziehungsform / Relational form — the painting as field of relationships, not collection of objects
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: Robbins describes Gleizes's analytical approach as focused on relationships rather than on objects themselves. The painting does not depict a city or a river; it enacts the relational field generated by the encounter between consciousness and the subject. "Gleizes himself characterized this phase of his work as an 'analysis of volume relationships'."
- **Rule**: The compositional unit is not the object but the relationship between formal elements — weight, density, and volumetric tension between zones.
- **Source**: p.19
- **Quote (EN)**: "Gleizes himself characterized this phase of his work as an 'analysis of volume relationships,' it bears striking similarity to the traditional use of the word 'analytikal' in our understanding of Cubism."
- **Category**: composition | form | theory

**PRINCIPLE [P-05]**: Kollektivität vs. Individualismus / Collective vs. Individual artistic production
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: Gleizes's theoretical framework was formed within the Abbaye de Créteil (1906), a communal artistic community, and this collective orientation never left him. He believed art's function was social — to create collective sensation, not individual expression. This distinguishes his version of Cubism from Picasso's individualist genius model.
- **Rule**: Evaluate a composition not by its individual expressive originality but by its capacity to generate shared, repeatable sensation-encounters across viewers.
- **Source**: pp.13–15
- **Quote (EN)**: "The structure of a bourgeois world and sought to substitute a communal society, but they did not reject the themes of modern life in favor of the Symbolist focus on single elements and internal, individual images."
- **Category**: theory | critique

**PRINCIPLE [P-06]**: Kritik der Perspektive / Critique of perspective — perspectival representation as dishonest about consciousness
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: Robbins explicitly frames Gleizes's project as a critique of perspectival representation: perspective is a deformation of how consciousness actually encounters space. It fixes a single viewpoint and forces the visible world into a geometrically coherent — but phenomenologically false — spatial system.
- **Rule**: Never use perspectival recession as the organizing principle of a surface. Perspective records the appearance of space from one frozen position; it does not represent the law of space as consciousness experiences it in motion.
- **Source**: p.18
- **Quote (EN)**: "He realized that the visible world is not the same as the space of a perspective painting, and accepting the conviction that perception of the physical world is deformed by the effects of distance, Gleizes' artistic concern was to reconstitute and synthesize the real world according to his individual consciousness."
- **Category**: critique | viewpoint | theory

**PRINCIPLE [P-07]**: Plastisches Bewusstsein / Plastic consciousness — consciousness as active shaper of formal encounter
- **Statement** [INFERRED]: Robbins's account implies that for Gleizes, consciousness is not a passive receptor of form-impressions but an active organ that shapes the encounter — synthesizing weight, mass, density, and relational structure into a plastic event. The painting does not represent what consciousness sees; it enacts what consciousness does.
- **Rule**: Design for an active viewer — one who synthesizes formal zones rather than reads a pre-digested image. Leave the synthetic act to the viewer, do not perform it in advance.
- **Source**: pp.17–18
- **Category**: sensation | form | theory

**PRINCIPLE [P-08]**: Gleizes vs. Picasso — System vs. Intuition
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: Robbins draws an explicit and sustained contrast between Gleizes's systematic, theoretically grounded Cubism and Picasso's (and Braque's) intuitive, improvisational approach. This is central to understanding what Gleizes's contribution actually is.
- **Rule**: Prefer systematic formal principles over intuitive gesture. A principle that can be stated, taught, and applied is more valuable than an effect that can only be produced by one individual in one moment.
- **Source**: pp.18–19
- **Quote (EN)**: "Gleizes considered the analytical Cubist works of Picasso and Braque, these figures of intersecting planes, as 'an improvisation of form' because of the emphasis on relationships and rhythms set up by parts of a dissected subject. He wished instead to tackle those rational methods to which the painters of yesterday did not dare to undertake, abandoning them to the presumptuous, old-fashioned and boring dealers of the official Salons."
- **Category**: critique | theory | composition

**PRINCIPLE [P-09]**: Oberfläche als gleichwertige Zonen / Surface as equal-value zones — against hierarchy
- **Statement** [INFERRED]: Robbins describes Gleizes's compositional approach as assigning equal value to all surface zones — there is no hierarchy between figure and ground, center and edge, near and far. Every zone is a site of sensation-encounter with equal standing. This follows directly from the rejection of perspective (which creates near/far hierarchy) and from the multi-viewpoint principle.
- **Rule**: Assign equal formal weight to all zones of the composition surface — resist the hierarchy of center/edge, figure/ground, near/far.
- **Source**: pp.18–19
- **Category**: composition | form | viewpoint

**PRINCIPLE [P-10]**: Form und Zeit / Form and time — the painting as temporal object
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: Robbins quotes Gleizes (via Apollinaire's characterization) on the synthetic preoccupation: "Thus it is that the synthetic preoccupation with epic themes is destined to be transformed into abstract art in the work of Delaunay and Gleizes." This confirms that the work operates in a temporal register — it is not a spatial snapshot but a temporal arc.
- **Rule**: Treat the composition as a time-based event unfolding under the viewer's gaze, not as a static spatial arrangement to be comprehended at once.
- **Source**: p.17
- **Quote (EN)**: "In order to understand the passage of Gleizes' painting from an epic, visionary (as opposed to visual) quality to abstraction, it is important to understand early Cubist style and its difference from our ordinary understanding of analytic Cubism."
- **Category**: temporality | sensation | theory
