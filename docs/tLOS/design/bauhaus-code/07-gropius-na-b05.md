## Weberei-Werkstatt (Weaving Workshop) — Wandbehänge, Decken, Teppiche, Wandschirm

This batch documents the full range of the weaving workshop's output through 1922–1924: wall hangings (Wandbehänge), coverlets (Decken), carpets (Teppiche), and a folding screen (Wandschirm). Two color plates (III. Farbtafel, IV. Farbtafel) are inserted within this sequence, documenting color-woven Schaftweberei and a knotted carpet in their actual chromatic values.

---

### Argument Structure

**Thesis**: The weaving workshop does not produce decorative textiles. It produces structured investigations into material, color, technique, and pattern — each object a proof that textile production can be governed by the same Gestaltungsgesetze as furniture or metalwork: reduction, material honesty, and typological thinking.

**Evidence**: Sixteen objects across shaft weaving (Schaftweberei), half-gobelin technique (Halbgobelin), gobelin technique (Gobelintechnik), knotted carpet technique (Knüpfteppich), and flat weaving with turned shuttle (Flachweberei mit gewendetem Schützen). Materials range from wool and cotton to artificial silk (Kunstseide) and viscose. Formats range from individual room pieces (100×200 cm) to large-format carpets (315×315 cm) to meterage goods (Meterware). Designers: Niemeyer, Leudesdorff, Valentin, Hollós, Otte, Helm, Hantschk, Erps, Fleischmann, Stölzl, Deinhardt, Köhler.

**Conclusion**: The weaving workshop demonstrates the breadth of Bauhaus Werkstattarbeit: the same principles (geometric structure, material economy, reduction of ornament to structural pattern) operate across every technique and scale, from a 105 cm-wide wall hanging to a 315×315 cm room carpet. Where Stölzl explicitly presents a design in both unique and Meterware form (pp.86–87), the workshop articulates its prototype logic directly.

### Principles

**PRINCIPLE [P-01]**: Meterware als Typus / Meterage as Type
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: Stölzl's Decke (p.86) is presented alongside its repeat-pattern version as Meterware (p.87), with the caption explicitly noting: "Teilmuster der Decke von Seite 86 als fortlaufender Rapport verwendet." The individual unique piece and the serial production version are shown as a pair. The transition from unique object to repeating module is not degradation — it is the demonstration that the design contains a Typus.
- **Rule**: Design the unique object so its pattern module can function as a repeating Rapport for serial production without loss of structural logic.
- **Source**: pp.86–87
- **Quote (DE)**: "Teilmuster der Decke von Seite 86 als fortlaufender Rapport verwendet. Meterware."
- **Quote (EN)**: "Partial pattern of the coverlet from page 86 used as a continuous repeat. Meterage goods."
- **Category**: standardization

**PRINCIPLE [P-02]**: Mehrschichtige Materialkomposition / Multi-layer Material Composition
- **Statement** [INFERRED]: Across this batch, no single object is woven from one material alone. Every piece combines two or three fibers: wool + cotton + Kunstseide (Erps, Fleischmann), wool + Viscose (Stölzl), Wolle + Baumwolle + Seide (Otte), Kunstseide alone as Meterware (Hollós). The choice of fiber combination is not decorative — it governs surface sheen, structural density, and how light reads the textile. Kunstseide in a wool ground creates highlight rhythm; Viscose creates a softer luster than pure silk.
- **Rule**: Specify fiber combinations intentionally — each material contributes a distinct visual and structural property; the combination is a design decision, not a convenience.
- **Source**: pp.80–99 (throughout)
- **Category**: material-honesty

**PRINCIPLE [P-03]**: Technik als Gestaltungsgesetz / Technique as Design Law
- **Statement** [INFERRED]: The weaving techniques documented — Schaftweberei (shaft weaving), Gobelintechnik, Halbgobelin, Knüpfteppich, Flachweberei mit gewendetem Schützen — are not interchangeable. Each technique has a structural grammar that determines what forms are possible. Gobelin technique allows complex pictorial fields and non-repeating composition (Otte's Gobelinteppich, p.79; Helm's Wandschirm, p.80). Schaftweberei produces striped or grid structures governed by shaft sequences. Knüpfteppich (knotted) allows the highest pattern density and sharpest geometric edge. The technique selection is a primary design decision — not a production question.
- **Rule**: Choose the weaving technique before designing the pattern — the technique defines the available formal vocabulary.
- **Source**: pp.80–99 (throughout)
- **Category**: craft-industry

**PRINCIPLE [P-04]**: Geometrische Reduktion im Textil / Geometric Reduction in Textile
- **Statement** [INFERRED]: The compositional structure across this batch is consistently geometric: horizontal banding (Niemeyer, Valentin, Stölzl, Deinhardt, Köhler), grid with asymmetric field distribution (Leudesdorff, Hollós, Fleischmann), symmetrical knotted carpet composition with modular pixel logic (Otte Knüpfteppich, p.81; Otte Wandbehang, p.85). No figurative or naturalistic motifs appear. The reduction is complete — ornament exists only as structured repetition of geometric elements arising directly from the weave grid.
- **Rule**: Allow only those forms that the weave structure itself generates; do not impose extraneous figuration onto the textile grid.
- **Source**: pp.80–99 (throughout)
- **Category**: form-economy

**PRINCIPLE [P-05]**: Farbpalette als System / Color Palette as System
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: The two Farbtafeln (III and IV, inserted between pp.80–81 and pp.88–89) document color weaving in full color reproduction. Hollós's Schaftweberei Meterware (Farbtafel III) demonstrates a structured palette: dark brown/black warp stripes, red ground, purple/violet horizontal bands, white and green accent bands — all interlocking as a grid of color intervals, not as free color choice. Hantschk's Knüpfteppich (Farbtafel IV) demonstrates a Kleeian pixel-color composition: grey, yellow, red, green, blue, black — building form through color value contrast at the module scale.
- **Rule**: Define the color palette as a finite interval system before weaving; each color must have a structural role in the composition, not a decorative one.
- **Source**: Farbtafel III (after p.80); Farbtafel IV (after p.88)
- **Category**: form-economy

**PRINCIPLE [P-06]**: Wandschirm als Raumteiler / Folding Screen as Space Divider
- **Statement** [INFERRED]: Helm's three-panel Wandschirm (p.80) is the only non-flat textile object in this batch — it is a spatial element, not a wall object. Woven in Gobelintechnik with Kunstseide and wool in black-orange-beige, height 170 cm, the design uses the three-panel structure as a formal constraint: the geometric composition (large stepped black and white/beige planes) reads across all three panels as a unified field that breaks at the hinges. The textile design is a spatial design.
- **Rule**: When textile becomes a spatial element, design the composition to accommodate the structural articulation (fold, hinge, break) as part of the formal logic.
- **Source**: p.80
- **Category**: spatial-design

### Workshop Products & Exercises

**EXERCISE [E-01]**: Wandbehang (Wall Hanging) — Schaftweberei
- **Description** [CERTAIN]: E. Niemeyer, 1923. Shaft weaving, cotton and artificial silk (Baumwolle, Kunstseide). Width 105 cm. Horizontal stripe composition: alternating dense dark and lighter bands, with fine white line articulation at intervals. The Kunstseide creates a sheen differential between stripe bands.
- **Demonstrates**: Material combination (cotton ground + Kunstseide highlight) creating visual rhythm through sheen; horizontal banding as primary structural motif of Schaftweberei.
- **Source**: p.75 (PDF p.80)

**EXERCISE [E-02]**: Wandbehang (Wall Hanging) — Schaftweberei
- **Description** [CERTAIN]: L. Leudesdorff, 1923. Shaft weaving, cotton (Baumwolle). Light ground with yellow and red warp stripes. Width 125 cm. The design is a fine plaid grid: vertical warp stripes in yellow and red against a light ground, crossed by subtle weft bands — a classic check structure made austere by the restrained color interval.
- **Demonstrates**: Grid structure arising directly from Schaftweberei logic (warp/weft crossing determines pattern); color economy (two accent colors against neutral ground).
- **Source**: p.76 (PDF p.81)

**EXERCISE [E-03]**: Wandbehang (Wall Hanging) — Schaftweberei
- **Description** [CERTAIN]: R. Valentin, 1923. Shaft weaving, wool (Wolle). Red-black-white. Width 110 cm. Bold horizontal banding in high contrast: heavy black bands, white bands, and an intermediate zone of complex micro-texture (dotted and hatched weave structures) between the solid bands. The texture zones between solid stripes read as visual interference patterns.
- **Demonstrates**: Structural texture as compositional element — the micro-weave zones are not filler but active formal components creating visual weight between solid bands; Materialwahrheit in wool (matte surface enhances contrast).
- **Source**: p.77 (PDF p.82)

**EXERCISE [E-04]**: Schaftweberei Meterware — Farbtafel III
- **Description** [CERTAIN]: R. Hollós, 1924. Shaft weaving, artificial silk (Kunstseide), meterage goods (Meterware). Width 110 cm. Color plate reproduction: dark brown/black warp stripes structuring a red ground; purple/violet horizontal bands creating secondary rhythm; white and green accent bands as interval markers. The composition is a color-interval grid — every color has a defined frequency in the repeat.
- **Demonstrates**: Prototype→Meterware logic applied to color textile; the Kunstseide material enables maximum color saturation for commercial production; systematic color palette as design law.
- **Source**: Farbtafel III (PDF p.86)

**EXERCISE [E-05]**: Schaftweberei — Wall Hanging
- **Description** [CERTAIN]: R. Hollós, 1924. Shaft weaving, wool and artificial silk (Wolle und Kunstseide). Black-white-grey-blue. Width given on p.78. Grid composition: strong horizontal bands in black alternating with structured lighter bands; a vertical grid of narrow stripes creates a plaid sub-structure. Multi-material: wool matte structure + Kunstseide sheen creates two visual registers within the same field.
- **Demonstrates**: Wool+Kunstseide material combination for structural sheen differentiation; black-white-grey-blue as the Bauhaus weaving palette norm.
- **Source**: p.78 (PDF p.83)

**EXERCISE [E-06]**: Gobelinteppich (Gobelin Carpet)
- **Description** [CERTAIN]: B. Otte, 1924. Gobelin technique, Smyrna wool (Smyrnawolle). Blue-yellow and brown scales. 200×300 cm. Large-format floor carpet with a composition of rectangular fields in varying sizes — a grid of irregular block sizes in blue-yellow and brown tonal scales. The asymmetrical field distribution creates a spatial hierarchy of color blocks without figuration.
- **Demonstrates**: Gobelin technique enabling free-field composition (not constrained to shaft repeat); grid-in-application where block sizes vary; the carpet as a spatial plan, not a pattern repeat.
- **Source**: p.79 (PDF p.84)

**EXERCISE [E-07]**: Wandschirm (Folding Screen) — 3-panel
- **Description** [CERTAIN]: D. Helm, 1923. Three-panel folding screen. Shaft weaving with gobelin technique, wool and artificial silk (Wolle, Kunstseide). Black-orange-beige. Height 170 cm. The woven surface depicts a strongly geometric composition of large stepped angular planes in black and off-white/beige — a stepped diagonal form reads across all three panels simultaneously. The three hinged panels create a physical fold at two points that the design acknowledges.
- **Demonstrates**: Textile as spatial architecture (Raumteiler); Gobelintechnik enabling non-repeating composition at large scale; the three-panel constraint incorporated as a formal decision; Kunstseide creating sheen contrast within the black-orange-beige palette.
- **Source**: p.80 (PDF p.85)

**EXERCISE [E-08]**: Knüpfteppich (Knotted Carpet) — Farbtafel IV
- **Description** [CERTAIN]: G. Hantschk, 1924. Knotted carpet technique, Smyrna wool (Smyrnawolle). 120×200 cm. Color plate reproduction: a composition of rectangular color blocks in grey, yellow, red, green, blue, and black — building a pixel-grid image of interlocking rectangular forms. The composition reads as a Klee-influenced color-value study translated into knotted textile: color creates form, not outline.
- **Demonstrates**: Knotted technique enabling highest pattern resolution and color range; pixel-grid logic as weaving principle — each knot is a color cell; color-as-form (Klee's color theory applied to textile).
- **Source**: Farbtafel IV (PDF p.89)

**EXERCISE [E-09]**: Knüpfteppich (Knotted Carpet)
- **Description** [CERTAIN]: B. Otte, 1923. Knotted carpet, Smyrna wool. Red-yellow-blue-violet-grey. 315×315 cm. Square-format large floor carpet. The composition is a symmetrical radial structure built from geometric pixel modules: chevron borders, square-grid interior zones, and a complex interlocking field of knotted blocks in high contrast. The symmetry is four-fold; the module vocabulary is arrows, squares, and diagonal bands.
- **Demonstrates**: Knüpfteppich at maximum format and complexity; symmetrical type-composition (not one-off pictorial); the modular pixel logic of knotting enabling complex symmetric pattern without freestyle drawing.
- **Source**: p.81 (PDF p.90)

**EXERCISE [E-10]**: Wandbehang (Wall Hanging) — Schaftweberei / Halbgobelin
- **Description** [CERTAIN]: B. Otte, 1922. Shaft weaving, half-gobelin. Wool, cotton, silk (Wolle, Baumwolle, Seide). Red ground with colored forms. 115×115 cm. Square format. Complex semi-pictorial composition: a grid of rectangular fields in varying densities and a red ground (noted in caption as "Rotskala"), with darker inset rectangles, diamond forms, and woven color blocks distributed asymmetrically across the surface. Three-material combination (wool + cotton + silk) creates differential surface textures.
- **Demonstrates**: Halbgobelin technique combining shaft-weave structure with free-weft pictorial elements; three-material composition for maximum tactile and visual differentiation; square format as self-contained spatial field.
- **Source**: p.82 (PDF p.91)

**EXERCISE [E-11]**: Decke (Coverlet) — Schaftweberei mit Gobelintechnik
- **Description** [CERTAIN]: M. Erps, 1924. Coverlet. Shaft weaving with gobelin technique. Wool and cotton (Wolle und Baumwolle). Grey-gold-brown-white. 120×250 cm. Long-format textile with a composite structure: bands of dense horizontal stripes alternate with isolated rectangular inset fields woven in Gobelintechnik — these insets break the stripe rhythm with blocks of concentrated color. The combination of shaft-repeat bands and gobelin insets creates a structured hierarchy of zones.
- **Demonstrates**: Technique combination (Schaftweberei + Gobelintechnik) as a compositional strategy — different techniques create different structural registers within a single piece; two-material (wool + cotton) enabling matte/structured surface.
- **Source**: p.83 (PDF p.92)

**EXERCISE [E-12]**: Wandbehang (Wall Hanging) — Schaftweberei mit gewendetem Schützen
- **Description** [CERTAIN]: A. Fleischmann, 1924. Shaft weaving with turned shuttle (gewendetem Schützen). Wool, artificial silk, cotton (Wolle, Kunstseide, Baumwolle). Silver-grey-black-brown. Width 120 cm. The turned-shuttle technique creates a distinctive visual effect: horizontal bands with staggered horizontal offset elements (the turned shuttles create blocks of weft-float extending beyond the main shed sequence). Three materials: wool for body, Kunstseide for sheen accent, cotton for structural weft.
- **Demonstrates**: Turned-shuttle technique (gewendeter Schützen) as a specific design tool creating offset block elements within horizontal structure; three-material composition; silver-grey-black-brown as a restrained achromatic palette with brown warmth.
- **Source**: p.84 (PDF p.93)

**EXERCISE [E-13]**: Wandbehang (Wall Hanging) — Schaftweberei / Halbgobelin

The composition is formally centered — the half-gobelin technique allows the complex central pictorial field while the shaft-woven border provides structural frame.
- **Demonstrates**: Half-gobelin for symmetrical pictorial compositions; center-field + frame structure as spatial hierarchy in textile; Otte's consistent use of pixel-grid geometry at all scales.
- **Source**: p.85 (PDF p.94)

**EXERCISE [E-14]**: Decke (Coverlet) — Flachweberei mit gewendetem Schützen

The turned-shuttle flat weaving creates strong diagonal offset bands within the horizontal structure — wide irregular horizontal bands of varying width, some with internal diagonal shift effects produced by the shuttle turning. The Viscose gives a soft reflective quality to the white bands.
- **Demonstrates**: Flachweberei mit gewendetem Schützen as a technique enabling diagonal and offset visual effects within a flat-weave structure; wool + viscose combination for matte/soft-sheen differentiation; Stölzl's characteristic manipulation of weft direction as a compositional tool.
- **Source**: p.86 (PDF p.95)

**EXERCISE [E-15]**: Decke als Meterware (Coverlet as Meterage) — Rapport
