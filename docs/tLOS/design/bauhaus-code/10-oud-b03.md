## DIE ENTWICKLUNG DER MODERNEN BAUKUNST IN HOLLAND (continued) — pp. 39–53

### Berlage — Late Assessment (p. 39–41)

**Argument Structure**
**Thesis**: Berlage's late work (the London Kontorgebäude, Abb. 9) represents a nearly complete solution to the problem he spent his life approaching — but only in that one building did he achieve a form-giving that corresponds both to his architectural theory and to the character of new materials.
**Evidence**: His general tendency was hindered by the Stil problem and by his reliance on Viollet-le-Duc's indirect method — studying old forms to derive constructive principles, rather than arriving at construction directly from the inside out.
**Conclusion**: Berlage's significance lies not in his completed works but in the perspective of endless developmental possibilities he opened for Dutch architecture. Even negative assessment of his output as a whole must acknowledge this.

### Principles

**PRINCIPLE [P-28]**: Formgebung von innen nach außen / Form-giving from inside outward
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: The new principle of organic architecture demands that form always be generated from inner necessity, not from outer formal sources. The new free art (freie Kunst) of the present generates form always less from outside inward, always more from inside outward — always less image (Abbild), always more form-whole (Gebilde).
- **Rule**: Generate architectural form from constructive and functional necessity working outward — not from external formal models applied inward. The building's form must be a direct expression of its inner organization.
- **Source**: p. 53
- **Quote (DE)**: "Die Grundlage einer neuen, einer organischen Baukunst aber — es muß immer wieder hervorgehoben werden — kann sie nur von einer inneren Notwendigkeit gebildet werden."
- **Quote (EN)**: "The foundation of a new, organic architecture — it must always be emphasized again — can only be formed from an inner necessity."
- **Category**: construction

**PRINCIPLE [P-29]**: Amsterdamer Schule — Subjektivismus als Reaktion / Amsterdam School — subjectivism as reaction
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: The Amsterdam School (De Klerk, Kramer, Van der Mey) carried the liberation from Berlage's excessive self-restraint (Zurückhaltung) to the extreme of subjectivism — a reaction to his rationalism so extreme that it passed through the outer extreme of Expressionism toward the utmost of Subjectivism. Their work represents the full anti-rational pole: individually motivated, not collectively directed.
- **Rule**: Do not resolve the tension between rationalist restraint and expressive freedom by abandoning rationalism entirely. The Amsterdam School's extreme subjectivism is a dead end — it cannot lead to the new Stil.
- **Source**: p. 42–43
- **Quote (DE)**: "Es ist die sogenannte Amsterdamer Schule, neben deren begabtestem Vertreter De Klerk noch Kramer und Van der Mey zu nennen sind, welche die Baukunst völlig ins Romantische und Phantastische schleppt, ohne sich um das Stilproblem noch im mindesten zu kümmern."
- **Quote (EN)**: "It is the so-called Amsterdam School, beside whose most gifted representative De Klerk, Kramer and Van der Mey are to be named, which drags architecture entirely into the Romantic and Fantastical, without concerning itself with the Style problem in the least."
- **Category**: form

**PRINCIPLE [P-30]**: Rationalismus als Gegengewicht / Rationalism as counterweight
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: Rationalism in every form is the complete opposite of subjectivism — a counterweight that, at the highest level through the instrument of balancing inner tensions, comes into sharp focus. The new, improved principle of organic architecture displaces the accent of architecture from outside to inside. Not the practical demands of the time, the demands of life, cause the outer appearance to develop — at the moment of creation the outer appearance is at first the main moment of architectural consciousness; the reality is secondary; the street image, the facade, is subordinated to the main matter of baukünstlerischen Gestaltens.
- **Rule**: In design, do not begin from the street facade as primary fact. Begin from the inner organizational logic; let the exterior emerge from that.
- **Source**: p. 43
- **Quote (DE)**: "Der Rationalismus ist in jeder Form vollkommen organischen Wesens und sein Gegensatz zum Subjektivismus — ein Gegensatz, nur auf hohem Niveau mittels des Ausgleiches innerer Spannungen zu überbrücken — tritt in aller Schärfe zutage."
- **Quote (EN)**: "Rationalism in every form is of completely organic nature, and its opposition to subjectivism — an opposition bridgeable only at a high level through the balancing of inner tensions — appears in full sharpness."
- **Category**: form

**PRINCIPLE [P-31]**: Amsterdamer Schule als Sackgasse / Amsterdam School as dead end
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: The Amsterdam School's reaction against Berlage's formal rationalism — by triggering a powerful reaction toward sachlich direction — opened the path of Dutch architectural development further. But by denying all architectural logic in their rejection of Berlage's formal language, by their production of results of extraordinarily low baukünstlerischen rank, the Amsterdam School has in its denial of all constructive logic provoked a reactionary counter-force — increasingly toward the Stil problem again.
- **Rule**: Extreme expressionism, by refusing constructive logic, ultimately rebounds into historicism — it generates a counter-reaction back toward Stil-Architektur. Only rationalism escapes this cycle.
- **Source**: p. 48
- **Quote (DE)**: "Überdies aber hat ihre Verweigerung jeder architektonischen Logik eine so kräftige Reaktion in entgegengesetzter, also in sachlicher Richtung, hervorgerufen, daß diese sich — wie von selbst — den Bestrebungen einer neu aufkommenden Bewegung anschließt, welche sich mehr und mehr dem Stilproblem wieder zuzuwenden scheint."
- **Quote (EN)**: "Moreover, their denial of every architectural logic has provoked such a powerful reaction in the opposite, that is the sachlich direction, that this — as of itself — connects to the efforts of a newly emerging movement that seems more and more to be turning back to the Style problem."
- **Category**: form

**PRINCIPLE [P-32]**: Zwei Strömungen / Two currents
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: The new movement in its beginnings has two currents that in their lines seem to intersect: the force of the straight-lined and cubic tendencies bringing both externally together while they are internally of fundamentally different natures. The first current takes Wright as model — not understanding his principles, only admiring his works. The second current leads toward new organic architecture through inner necessity alone.
- **Rule**: Distinguish between formal imitation of new architecture (Wright-influenced surface cubism) and genuine understanding of organic architectural principle. The former is still Stil-Architektur with a modernist vocabulary.
- **Source**: p. 53–54
- **Quote (DE)**: "Diese neue Bewegung, welche erst in ihren Anfängen ist, ist vorläufig noch ziemlich komplizierter Art, weil sich zwei Strömungen in ihr abzeichnen, die kraft der geradlinigen und kubischen Tendenzen beider äußerlich scheinbar zusammengehen, während sie innerlich grundverschiedener Natur sind."
- **Quote (EN)**: "This new movement, which is only in its beginnings, is for now still fairly complicated in nature, because two currents are distinguishable in it, which by virtue of their straight-lined and cubic tendencies seem externally to converge, while inwardly they are fundamentally different in nature."
- **Category**: form

**PRINCIPLE [P-33]**: Wright-Einfluss — organische Grundlage / Wright influence — organic foundation
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: The first current takes Wright's work as model (more admiring his works than grasping his principles). The foundation of a new organic architecture cannot be generated from copying any outer form — however new. Every reinterpretation of old or new formal inheritance degrades architecture from aesthetic experience to Geschmackssache (matter of taste) — that is, to Stil-Architektur.
- **Rule**: Do not adopt a new formal vocabulary (even Wright's) as a new style. Understand the generating principle — organic necessity — and derive form from that, each time afresh.
- **Source**: p. 53
- **Quote (DE)**: "Jede Hineininterpretierung alter oder neuer Formüberlieferung erniedrigt die Baukunst vom Erlebnis zur Geschmackssache, d.h. von ästhetischer Gestaltung zur Stil-Architektur."
- **Quote (EN)**: "Every reinterpretation of old or new formal inheritance degrades architecture from aesthetic experience to a matter of taste — that is, from aesthetic form-giving to style architecture."
- **Category**: form

**PRINCIPLE [P-34]**: Neuer Stil aus Sachlichkeit / New style from objectivity
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: The middle force — the new style — emerges from the collective striving after elimination of all inorganic elements, from the drive to achieve purely organic means, from the practical and aesthetic and material and spiritual tendencies of the time. Its middle force, which shows ever more as the consequence of collective tendencies, forms the guideline for the new style.
- **Rule**: The new style cannot be willed or designed. It emerges from the convergence of collective forces — practical, material, aesthetic, spiritual. The architect's task is to align with these forces, not to impose form upon them.
- **Source**: p. 53–54
- **Quote (DE)**: "In diesem Streben nach Beseitigung aller unorganischen und zur Erzielung nur organischer Mittel, in diesem Triebe also von der Nebensächlichkeit zur Sachlichkeit, finden sich die praktischen und ästhetischen, die materiellen und geistigen Tendenzen der Zeit. Ihre Mittelkraft, die sich immer mehr als Folge kollektiver Strömungen zeigt, bildet damit die Richtlinie zum neuen Stil."
- **Quote (EN)**: "In this striving after the elimination of all inorganic and the achievement of only organic means, in this drive from the secondary to objectivity, the practical and aesthetic, material and spiritual tendencies of the time find themselves. Their resultant force, showing ever more as the consequence of collective currents, thereby forms the guideline toward the new style."
- **Category**: form

### Visual Evidence

**FIGURE [Fig-11]**: M. de Klerk, Etagenhäuser von Herrn Hille in Amsterdam, 1911 (Abb. 10, p. 40)
- **Shows**: Photograph of a corner residential apartment building — brick construction, complex and varied facade with elaborately detailed window surrounds, rounded corner tower with conical cap, dramatically varied roofline, deliberately irregular brick detailing. Very high ornamental complexity.
- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: Early Amsterdam School — the extreme opposite of Berlage's rationalism. Oud uses this as evidence of the Subjektivismus he critiques. The building deliberately avoids any rationalist uniformity — every element is individually varied.
- **Text reference**: "Wohnungsblock für Herrn Hille (Abb. 10) und in gewissem Sinne auch das Schiffahrtsgebäude von Van der Mey (Abb. 11) in Amsterdam — sich letzten Endes vielmehr auf die Stil-Architektur, wie auf Berlage zurückführen läßt."
- **Source**: p. 40

**FIGURE [Fig-12]**: J.M. van der Mey, Schiffahrtsgebäude in Amsterdam, 1912 (Abb. 11, p. 43)
- **Shows**: Photograph of a large corner commercial building — extremely elaborate brick facade with turrets, ornamental stone banding, carved figural decoration at corner, complex roofline, Gothic and Art Nouveau influences combined. Highly individualized detail at every level.
- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: The fullest Amsterdam School expression — Oud identifies this as the extreme of Subjektivismus. The corner treatment here is the opposite of De Stijl corner resolution: the corner is dramatized as the building's focal point through accumulated ornament.
- **Text reference**: Referenced alongside Abb. 10 as endpoint of the Stil-Architektur direction through the Amsterdam School route.
- **Source**: p. 43

**FIGURE [Fig-13]**: P. Kramer, Seeleuteheim in Den Helder, 1914 (Abb. 12, p. 44)
- **Shows**: Photograph of a sailors' home — brick construction with a prominent square tower, relatively simplified compared to Van der Mey; windows grouped; surface less ornamental; but tower still expressive and non-rational in proportion.
- **Demonstrates** [INFERRED]: A less extreme example of the Amsterdam School — some rationalist influence but still primarily expressive. Shows the range within the school.
- **Text reference**: Referenced in group of Amsterdam School examples.
- **Source**: p. 44

**FIGURE [Fig-14]**: M. de Klerk, Entwurf für Etagenhäuser an der Peripherie einer Kleinstadt, 1916 (Abb. 13, p. 45)
- **Shows**: Drawing (landscape orientation) of proposed apartment housing at a small town periphery — elongated horizontal building with varied roofline, towers at intervals, elaborately modeled facade surface. The drawing shows the expressive approach applied to mass housing.
- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: De Klerk's Amsterdam School approach extended to housing estates — the element Oud most critiques. Individualistic expressionism applied to standardized housing produces results of "extraordinarily low baukünstlerischen rank" per Oud's text.
- **Text reference**: Referenced among Abb. 14–19 as examples of Amsterdam School housing that Oud specifically critiques.
- **Source**: p. 45

**FIGURE [Fig-15]**: M. de Klerk, Etagenhäuser Spaarndammerplantsoen in Amsterdam, 1913 (Abb. 14, p. 46)
- **Shows**: Photograph of apartment housing block — long horizontal facade, regular window rhythm, but with expressive brick detailing at entries and roofline. Less extreme than the Hille building but still distinctly Amsterdam School in character.
- **Demonstrates** [INFERRED]: The Amsterdam School approach to standardized housing — where individual expressive detail competes with the repetitive housing unit rhythm.
- **Text reference**: Part of the Abb. 14–19 sequence discussed in text (p. 48).
- **Source**: p. 46

**FIGURE [Fig-16]**: M. de Klerk, Etagenhäuser Spaarndammerplantsoen in Amsterdam, 1914 (Abb. 15, p. 48)
- **Shows**: Photograph rotated 90° — apartment block with highly elaborated brick surface, irregular window placement, curved bay projections, prominent chimney elements. The facade is deliberately anti-regular.
- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: Peak Amsterdam School expressionism in housing. Oud states that this direction, despite its "merkwürdige Begabheit" (remarkable giftedness), generates results of the lowest baukünstlerischen rank.
- **Text reference**: "schiedenheit so kräftig zusammenzuhalten wissen, daß in ihrer Einigung die Bauten zu großen, farbig, flächig und massal gespannten Hüllen werden... trotz der Vielförmigkeit, oft großzügiger Art hervorgeht. (Abb. 14–19.)"
- **Source**: p. 48

**FIGURE [Fig-17]**: M. de Klerk, Etagenhäuser Spaarndammerplantsoen in Amsterdam, 1917 (Abb. 16, p. 49)
- **Shows**: Photograph rotated 90° — another view of the Spaarndammerplantsoen development, showing the curved corner treatment and expressive roofline elements. The brick surface is varied by window size, shape, and placement creating a deliberately pictorial effect.
- **Demonstrates** [INFERRED]: Continuation of the expressionist housing sequence. The corner here is handled expressively — a curved tower form. Compare to De Stijl corner resolution by continuation or dissolution.
- **Source**: p. 49

**FIGURE [Fig-18]**: M. de Klerk, Etagenhäuser Spaarndammerplantsoen in Amsterdam, 1917 (Abb. 17, p. 50)
- **Shows**: Photograph rotated 90° — view showing the spike tower element (a narrow pointed brick spire rising from the housing block), curved roof forms, irregular window patterns. Emphatically expressionist and non-structural.
- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: The spike tower is the most extreme element of Amsterdam School expressionism — a purely expressive gesture with no constructive justification. Oud explicitly critiques this type of element as baukünstlerische Gesetzlosigkeit (architectural lawlessness).
- **Text reference**: "Eine auf die Spitze getriebene Gesetzlosigkeit: eine Fülle endlos variierter Motive für Fenster, Türen, Balkone, Erker, Giebel... eine schwungvolle, dennoch irrationale und nur ästhetisch motivierte Massenkonzeption."
- **Source**: p. 50

**FIGURE [Fig-19]**: M. de Klerk, Etagenhäuser Amstelaan in Amsterdam, 1920 (Abb. 18, p. 51)
- **Shows**: Photograph (slightly rotated) of a long apartment block — very long horizontal facade, more restrained than earlier de Klerk works; uniform window rhythm; reduced ornamental detail; but still with elaborated entry elements and varied surface treatment.
- **Demonstrates** [INFERRED]: Later de Klerk — a degree of rationalization in the housing facade, though still Amsterdam School in character. Shows the pressure of housing standardization on the expressionist approach.
- **Source**: p. 51

**FIGURE [Fig-20]**: M. de Klerk, Entwurf für ein Auktionsgebäude für Blumenverkauf in Aalsmeer, 1922 (Abb. 19, p. 52)
- **Shows**: Drawing (landscape) of a flower auction building — very long low horizontal building, strongly horizontal profile, curved ends, minimal superstructure. Approached a more rationalized mass than earlier de Klerk work, though still expressionist in the surface modulations.
- **Demonstrates** [INFERRED]: De Klerk's late work moving toward larger-scale formal simplicity — though still outside the rationalist line. This is the kind of early Amsterdam School work Oud finds more bearable: "mehr wesentlich; Abb. 22–23–24 mehr äußerlich."
- **Source**: p. 52

**FIGURE [Fig-21]**: J.J.P. Oud, Entwurf für eine Häuserreihe am Meeresstrande, 1917 (Abb. 20, p. 54)
- **Shows**: Drawing of a row of houses at the seashore — very simple, rectangular facade grid; uniform window openings in rows; flat roofline; no ornament; strong horizontal and vertical grid reading. Extremely plain by comparison with Amsterdam School examples.
- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: Oud's own early work in the rationalist direction — his first fully realized alternative to the Amsterdam School approach. The drawing shows the Masse und Öffnung logic applied directly: the window grid as the primary design element of the facade.
- **Text reference**: Referenced as starting point of Oud's own development toward organic rationalism.
- **Source**: p. 54

**FIGURE [Fig-22]**: J.J.P. Oud, Entwurf für ein Fabrikgebäude in Purmerend, 1919 (Abb. 21, p. 55)
- **Shows**: Drawing (landscape, dark background) of factory building design — horizontal composition, flat roof, large regular window openings, white rendered facade, minimal ornament. Strong horizontal emphasis at ground level with recessed upper level. A De Stijl color application appears indicated.
- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: Early Oud — the rationalist factory design. The dark/light contrast in the rendering itself demonstrates color as spatial structure: the dark ground plane against the light facade creates spatial depth without physical projection.
- **Text reference**: Referenced as part of Oud's developmental sequence.
- **Source**: p. 55

**FIGURE [Fig-23]**: J.B. van Loghem, Saalbau Wohnungsgruppe "Roschaghe" in Haarlem, 1920 (Abb. 22, p. 56)
- **Shows**: Photograph of housing group — brick construction, relatively simple volumes, flat rooflines, minimal ornament but still heavy in masonry character. Vertical tower element marks the entry.
- **Demonstrates** [INFERRED]: The "first current" of the new movement — Wright-influenced rationalism that has not yet achieved genuine De Stijl composition. Oud groups this with Abb. 22–23–24 as "mehr äußerlich" (more externally influenced).
- **Text reference**: "In dem Strom verdienstvollen Bemühungen und kubischen Tendenzen führt die zweite Strömung in der neuen Baukunstbewegung Hollands mehr oder weniger anschließt, lehrreich (Abb. 20–21–25–26–27–28–29 mehr wesentlich; Abb. 22–23–24 mehr äußerlich)."
- **Source**: p. 56

**FIGURE [Fig-24]**: J.B. van Loghem, Wohnungsgruppe "Rosehaghe" in Haarlem, 1920 (Abb. 23, p. 57)
- **Shows**: Photograph — different view of the same housing group; shows individual dwelling units with large windows, flat roofline, simple masonry volumes. More domestic scale visible.
- **Demonstrates** [INFERRED]: Second view of the Wright-influenced rationalist current — showing how the domestic scale is resolved. Compare to Oud's own approach to housing at human scale.
- **Source**: p. 57

**FIGURE [Fig-25]**: W.M. Dudok, Schulgebäude in Hilversum, 1921 (Abb. 24, p. 58)
- **Shows**: Photograph of school building — low horizontal brick building with strong horizontal banding in windows, flat roofline, prominent vertical tower element set asymmetrically, plain brick surfaces. Strongly Wright-influenced in the horizontal massing and asymmetric composition.
- **Demonstrates** [INFERRED]: Dudok's early Hilversum school shows the Wright-influenced rationalist current most clearly — horizontal extension, asymmetric massing, tower accent. Oud would classify this as the "first current" that remains formal imitation rather than genuine organic derivation. Dudok's work is referenced among Abb. 22–24 as "mehr äußerlich."
- **Source**: p. 58
