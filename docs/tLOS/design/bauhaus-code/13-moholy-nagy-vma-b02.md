## II. Das Material

### Argument Structure
**Thesis**: The encounter with material must begin not through the eye, but through the hand. Visual culture has atrophied the tactile sense; before any student can understand material, the Tastsinn must be rehabilitated through deliberate exercise.
**Evidence**: A systematic program of Tasttafeln (tactile boards) constructed by Bauhaus students in the 2nd semester (1926–1928), ranging from simple thread compositions to rotating drums with contrasting textures, combined with graphic diagrams translating tactile sensation values into measurable form.
**Conclusion**: Tactile education is not a peripheral warm-up — it is an epistemological correction. Only after the hand has learned to know material independently of the eye can the student begin the deeper work of understanding Faktur, Textur, and Struktur as distinct phenomena.

## Tastübungen

### Argument Structure
**Thesis**: The Bauhaus preliminary course took up tactile education early. The Tastorgan (organ of touch) is simultaneously the most reliable and most neglected of the senses; rehabilitating it opens a sixth orientation — the Vibrations- und Druckempfindungen (vibration and pressure sensations).
**Evidence**: The course director described the Tastorgan as simultaneously the oldest and least cultivated sense. During lectures on this theme, students were connected to the topic by handling submitted material samples (Stoffe, Metalle, Broteste, Leder, Papier, Porzellan, Schwamm, etc.) and determining their qualities by touch alone.
**Conclusion**: The human hand, educated by deliberate exercise, can register material qualities with a precision that sight alone cannot achieve. The footnote to tactilism ("manifest des taktilismus," Milan 1921) confirms this is a broader cultural project — Marinetti proposed it, but the Bauhaus gave it a systematic pedagogical form.

### Principles

**PRINCIPLE [P-01]**: Tastsinn (tactile sensation) — rehabilitation of the neglected sense
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: The Tastsinn is the oldest of the senses and simultaneously the most neglected. Modern visual culture has produced a one-sided dominance of the eye. By deliberately exercising tactile perception — through material boards, blindfolded exploration, and graphical measurement of sensation values — the full perceptual apparatus of the designer is restored.
- **Rule**: Before any student works with material visually, require them to know it tactilely — blindfolded, by hand, without the mediation of sight.
- **Source**: p.21 (book p.21, PDF p.026)
- **Quote (DE)**: "er holt sich die verschiedensten materialien zusammen, um mit ihrer hilfe möglichst viele verschiedene empfindungen registrieren zu können"
- **Quote (EN)**: "he assembles the most varied materials, in order to be able to register as many different sensations as possible with their help"
- **Category**: tactile | pedagogy

**PRINCIPLE [P-02]**: Tasttafel (tactile board) — systematic mapping of material sensation
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: A Tasttafel is a systematic arrangement of materials with contrasting tactile values, designed to make the Empfindungswerte (sensation values) legible and measurable. The most rigorous versions pair the physical board with a graphic diagram that translates tactile impression into visual notation — not to replace touch, but to make its results available for comparative analysis.
- **Rule**: Construct tactile boards as scientific instruments: each element must contrast with its neighbor in a single defined property (hardness, roughness, temperature response, vibration character).
- **Source**: p.22 (book), p.23 (book)
- **Quote (DE)**: "versuch, die empfindungswerte in diagrammen festzulegen"
- **Quote (EN)**: "attempt to fix the sensation values in diagrams"
- **Category**: tactile | pedagogy

**PRINCIPLE [P-03]**: Vibrations- und Druckempfindungen — the tactile sixth sense
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: Beyond the standard inventory of tactile qualities (rough/smooth, hard/soft, warm/cold), the hand perceives vibration and pressure as a distinct register. This is not a metaphor — physical vibration boards with spiral spring constructions and lamellar arrangements of different materials produce genuinely differentiated pressure-vibration sensations. These sensations cannot be mediated by sight and constitute their own perceptual domain.
- **Rule**: Include vibration and pressure exercises alongside standard texture exercises — the hand's ability to register Schwingung (oscillation) and Druck (pressure) is a distinct skill requiring distinct training.
- **Source**: p.24 (book), p.29 (book)
- **Category**: tactile | perception

**PRINCIPLE [P-04]**: Gemeinschaftlichkeit der Tastübungen — collective calibration
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: Tactile exercises should be conducted collectively, not in isolation. When many students handle the same materials simultaneously and register their sensations independently, the results can be compared and deviations in individual perception identified. The Tastdiagramm (tactile diagram) makes this collective calibration possible.
- **Rule**: Conduct tactile exercises as group events where multiple observers register the same object, then compare and discuss divergences — these divergences are data, not errors.
- **Source**: p.26 (book), p.27 (book) [PDF pp.031–032]
- **Quote (DE)**: "da die tastwerte ganz und gar subjektiv registriert werden, schien es wünschenswert, eine art kontrolle der eigenen empfindungen in 'tastdiagrammen' einzuholen"
- **Quote (EN)**: "since tactile values are registered entirely subjectively, it seemed desirable to obtain a kind of check on one's own sensations in 'tactile diagrams'"
- **Category**: tactile | pedagogy

**PRINCIPLE [P-05]**: Das Erlebnis des Materials — intensive engagement with material
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: Intensive engagement with material increases the certainty of perception in later, technically demanding tasks. Results from the tactile exercises were not merely pleasant — they showed that intuitive care and precision emerged spontaneously from students who had been educated through their hands. A Japanese student with a more active relationship to materials than his European peers showed this most clearly: stroking and feeling alone was not enough for him — he required direct physical contact.
- **Rule**: Require intensive material engagement before any formal design task — a student who does not know a material by hand will not make sound decisions about it by eye.
- **Source**: p.28 (book), p.31 (book), p.32 (book) [PDF pp.033–037]
- **Quote (DE)**: "die intensive beschäftigung mit dem material stärkt die sicherheit im gefühlsmäßigen, auch auf der seite des wissensmäßigen"
- **Quote (EN)**: "intensive engagement with material increases certainty in the intuitive domain, as well as on the side of knowledge"
- **Category**: material | pedagogy

**PRINCIPLE [P-06]**: Tastkultur (tactile culture) — social and medical extension
- **Statement** [INFERRED]: The pedagogy of tactile education is not limited to art and design training. A training director of an institution for socially oriented healthcare education (for women) recognized in the Bauhaus tactile exercises a direct stimulus for massage training — a domain that likewise depends on educated hand-perception. Tastkultur has applications wherever the hand must act with precision on the physical world.
- **Rule**: Recognize tactile education as a fundamental human skill with professional applications beyond art: healthcare, craft, manufacture, teaching.
- **Source**: p.28 (book) [PDF p.033]
- **Category**: tactile | pedagogy

## Terminologie

### Argument Structure
**Thesis**: Current terminology for material phenomena is imprecise: the words Struktur, Textur, and Faktur are used interchangeably in everyday speech and even in technical literature. This must be corrected. Each word names a distinct phenomenon, and confusing them leads to design errors.
**Evidence**: Four terms are defined with precision: Struktur (the unalterable constructive character of a material), Textur (the organic surface closure of the material), Faktur (the external action upon the material's surface from outside), and Häufung (accumulation). The definitions are illustrated with examples from metalwork, natural materials, and industrial processes.
**Conclusion**: Precise terminology is not pedantry — it is the prerequisite for systematic work. A designer who cannot distinguish Struktur from Faktur will not know whether to work with or against a material's character.

### Principles

**PRINCIPLE [P-07]**: Struktur — die unveränderbare Aufbauart des Materialgefüges
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: Struktur is the unalterable constructive character of the material substance — the way it is built up at the molecular or crystalline level. Every material has a Struktur that cannot be removed or overwritten: the crystal lattice of metal, the fibrous layering of wood, the cellular arrangement of bone. Struktur is given, not made.
- **Rule**: Never design in ignorance of a material's Struktur — to work against Struktur is to court material failure; to reveal it is to achieve formal truth.
- **Source**: p.33 (book) [PDF p.038]
- **Quote (DE)**: "die unveränderbare aufbauart des materialgefüges nennt man struktur. jedes material besitzt also struktur; metalle z. b.: kristallinische, papier: faserige struktur (abb. 14–21) usw."
- **Quote (EN)**: "the unalterable constructive character of the material substance is called structure. every material thus possesses structure; metals e.g.: crystalline, paper: fibrous structure (figs. 14–21) etc."
- **Category**: material | surface

**PRINCIPLE [P-08]**: Textur — organisch entstandene Abschlussfläche jeder Struktur
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: Textur is the organically formed surface closure of every Struktur — the skin the material develops as its natural interface with the world. It is not applied from outside; it grows from within. Examples: Epidermis (skin), organisch (organic surface of biological material). Textur is the Struktur's self-presentation at the boundary.
- **Rule**: Distinguish Textur from applied surface treatment at every stage of design — Textur is to be read and respected, not overwritten without reason.
- **Source**: p.33 (book) [PDF p.038]
- **Quote (DE)**: "die organisch entstandene abschlußfläche jeder struktur nach außen heißt textur (epidermis, organisch). (abb. 22–25)"
- **Quote (EN)**: "the organically formed surface closure of every structure toward the outside is called texture (epidermis, organic). (figs. 22–25)"
- **Category**: material | surface

**PRINCIPLE [P-09]**: Faktur — der sinnlich wahrnehmbare Niederschlag des Werkprozesses
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: Faktur is the sensorially perceptible imprint of the working process — the trace that production leaves on the surface of a material. It shows itself as the external effect of the work process on the material's surface from outside. Faktur can be natural (the beetle's channel in elm bark — abb. 26) or mechanical (the grinding mark of a machine). It can also be the trace of an organism's biological process, which is itself a kind of working. Faktur types include: Musterungen (hammer marks), vollkommene Glätte (polished, pressed smooth), Lichterscheinung (specular light effect, reflection, refraction), and all variations of these as function of material and applied force.
- **Rule**: When you process a surface, you create a Faktur whether you intend to or not — make it deliberate: know which Faktur type you are producing and why.
- **Source**: p.33 (book) [PDF p.038]
- **Quote (DE)**: "faktur ist die art und erscheinung, der sinnlich wahrnehmbare niederschlag des werkprozesses, der sich bei jeder bearbeitung von material zeigt, also die äußere einwirkung kann sowohl elementar (durch natureinfluß), als auch mechanisch, z. b. durch maschine usw. erfolgen."
- **Quote (EN)**: "Faktur is the nature and appearance, the sensorially perceptible imprint of the working process, which manifests itself in every treatment of material — thus the external action can be both elementary (through natural influence) and mechanical, e.g. through machine, etc."
- **Category**: material | surface

### Exercises & Pedagogy

**EXERCISE [E-01]**: Tasttafel aus Fäden (tactile board from threads)
- **Task** [CERTAIN]: Students compose a flat board using threads of different materials and weave structures arranged in adjacent zones. The board presents a systematic series of tactile contrasts — differing in softness, rigidity, surface friction, and fiber direction. The exercise is completed without colour or visual decoration — the only criterion is the quality of tactile differentiation.
- **Purpose**: Trains the hand to distinguish surface texture and material character through touch alone. Demonstrates PRINCIPLE P-01 and P-02. Establishes the vocabulary of tactile contrast before any visual design work begins.
- **Source**: p.22 (book) [PDF p.027] — abb. 1, Otti Berger, Bauhaus 2. Semester 1926

**EXERCISE [E-02]**: Tasttafel mit grafischer Darstellung (tactile board with graphic translation)
- **Task** [CERTAIN]: Students construct a two-row tactile board and simultaneously produce a graphic diagram that translates the sensation values of each material zone into a line curve. The curve rises and falls in response to the student's perception of tactile intensity, roughness, or some other dimension. The diagram is an attempt to fix subjective sensation in measurable, communicable form.
- **Purpose**: Forces the student to quantify tactile experience and thus make it available for comparison and critique. Demonstrates the connection between hand-knowledge and analytical representation. Reference: PRINCIPLE P-02, P-04.
- **Source**: p.22 (book) [PDF p.027] — abb. 2, Hinrich Bredendieck, Bauhaus 2. Semester 1927

**EXERCISE [E-03]**: Kombinierte Tasttafel aus Schrauben (combined board from screws)
- **Task** [CERTAIN]: A long flat board constructed entirely from screws of varying sizes and densities, arranged to produce zones of contrasting Druck- und Vibrationsempfindungen (pressure and vibration sensations). The screw heads create a regular but varied topography across which the finger can be drawn to experience rhythmic punctual pressure.
- **Purpose**: Isolates pressure and vibration as a distinct tactile register, separate from surface texture or temperature. Demonstrates PRINCIPLE P-03.
- **Source**: p.23 (book) [PDF p.028] — abb. 3, Werner Zimmermann, Bauhaus 2. Semester 1927

**EXERCISE [E-04]**: Tasttafel aus Nägeln (tactile board from nails)
- **Task** [CERTAIN]: A flat board with nails of varying densities, head sizes, and arrangements, constructed to register Stichempfindungen (puncture sensations). The hand moves across the surface and experiences a spectrum from dense-fine to sparse-coarse nail fields.
- **Purpose**: Trains sensitivity to the Stich (puncture/prick) register of tactile perception — a sensation type irreducible to either roughness or pressure. Demonstrates PRINCIPLE P-03.
- **Source**: p.23 (book) [PDF p.028] — abb. 4, Siegfried Giesenschlag, Bauhaus 2. Semester 1927

**EXERCISE [E-05]**: Drehbare Tasttrommel (rotating tactile drum)
- **Task** [CERTAIN]: A cylinder with several contrasting materials arranged in rows along its length; the drum is mounted on an axle so it can be rotated. The rotating drum presents different material zones sequentially to the fingers as the hand holds still and the drum turns. The single criterion: each row must present a clear contrast to its neighbours. Crucially, no set form was prescribed — the students arrived at widely divergent solutions, none of which was an individual invention, since the only criterion was the contrast principle.
- **Purpose**: Demonstrates that formal diversity emerges from a shared governing principle when no stylistic norm is imposed. The exercise proves that Konstruktion (governing structural law) produces variety, not uniformity. Demonstrates PRINCIPLE P-02.
- **Source**: p.25 (book) [PDF p.030] — abb. 5, Rudolf Marwitz, Bauhaus 2. Semester 1926

**EXERCISE [E-06]**: Zweizeilige Tasttafel mit Umkehrung — rhythmisch gegliedert (two-row board, rhythmically articulated with reversal)
- **Task** [CERTAIN]: A board arranged in two rows running in opposite directions, so the tactile gradient of one row is reversed in the other. The student works with the sensation of reversal — what was rough-to-smooth in the upper row becomes smooth-to-rough in the lower. This introduces rhythm and directional tension into the tactile sequence.
- **Purpose**: Introduces Spannung (tension) and Bewegung (movement) into tactile composition — the first step toward understanding that material can carry directional energy. Demonstrates PRINCIPLE P-02.
- **Source**: p.31 (book) [PDF p.036] — abb. 12, Sven Hansen, Bauhaus 2. Semester 1926

**EXERCISE [E-07]**: Vierzeilige Tasttafel aus Papier mit Rauhstufen (four-row board from paper with rough gradations)
- **Task** [CERTAIN]: A board constructed from paper only, but with paper treated to produce a rich scale of tactile values: glatt (smooth), hart (hard), weich (soft), geribt (ribbed), gerauht (roughened), gewandt (turned/folded). The board demonstrates how a single material category (paper) contains an enormous internal variety of Faktur values.
- **Purpose**: Proves that material variety is not required for perceptual richness — Faktur differentiation within a single material can generate as wide a tactile spectrum as any multi-material board. Directly supports PRINCIPLE P-09.
- **Source**: p.29 (book) [PDF p.034] — abb. 9, Gerda Marx, Bauhaus 2. Semester 1926

**EXERCISE [E-08]**: Vibrations- und Druckbrücke — Spiralfederkonstruktion mit Lamellen (vibration-and-pressure bridge — spiral spring construction with lamellae)
- **Task** [CERTAIN]: A structural object — a bridge-like construction made from spiral springs with lamellae of different materials attached along its length. The object can be plucked, pressed, and vibrated; below it lies a graphic notation of the vibration-pressure response. The student registers both the physical behavior of the object and the sensations it produces in the hand.
- **Purpose**: Extends tactile education into the domain of mechanical vibration and spring-force — materials as dynamic, not static, tactile phenomena. Demonstrates PRINCIPLE P-03.
- **Source**: p.30 (book) [PDF p.035] — abb. 10 and 11, Gustav Hassenpflug, Bauhaus 2. Semester 1927
