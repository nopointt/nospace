## Section: Fotomontage, Fotoplastik, and the New Visual Synthesis

### Argument Structure

**Thesis**: The photographic image is not bound to single-lens reality. Montage, reflection, distortion, and combination — these are the tools by which photography becomes Produktion. The photomontage, the Fotoplastik, and the Typofoto are all evidence that the photographer-constructor can assemble new visual realities that could not exist without photographic technique.

**Evidence**: A sequence of plates demonstrating: mirror and reflection phenomena (Spiegel und Spiegelungen, Konvexspiegelaufnahme); humorous photographic tricks using multiple-exposure and composite (das fixierte Lachkabinett, der Übermensch); photomontages by Hannah Höch and Citroen/Bauhaus; Fotoplastiken by Moholy-Nagy; a Typofoto magazine cover; and a film-apparatus photograph with critical text.

**Conclusion**: Photography's power lies in its ability to construct images that are impossible or non-existent as physical reality. Every plate in this section is either a proof of Produktion or a demonstration of what Simultanität can look like when assembled from photographic fragments.

## Part I — Optische Täuschung und Spiegelung (Optical Illusion and Reflection)

### Principles

**PRINCIPLE [P-01]**: Spiegelung als Konstruktionsmittel / Reflection as constructive tool
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: The mirror and reflective surface are not merely devices for depicting reality — they multiply, distort, and recompose the visual field. A convex mirror does not reproduce: it constructs a new spatial proposition, wrapping an entire room into a single curved image. I show this with the spherical convex mirror photograph by Muche/Bauhaus: the room, the photographer, the apparatus, all compressed into one gleaming sphere.
- **Rule**: Use reflection and distortion not to record but to reconstitute spatial relationships — the curved or mirrored surface is a compositional tool, not a documentary device.
- **Source**: p.99, p.101
- **Quote (DE)**: "Spiegel und Spiegelungen" / "Konvexspiegelaufnahme"
- **Quote (EN)**: "Mirrors and Reflections" / "Convex mirror photograph"
- **Category**: neue-sehen

**PRINCIPLE [P-02]**: Mehrfachbelichtung als optischer Witz und Erkenntnis / Multiple exposure as optical humor and insight
- **Statement** [INFERRED]: The photograph of the laughing cabinet (Das fixierte Lachkabinett) — three simultaneous versions of the same face caught in a single image — demonstrates that the camera can do what the eye cannot: it can fix the same subject at multiple moments of expression on a single surface. This is not falsification; it is a revelation of temporal depth that the single instant cannot contain.
- **Rule**: Multiple exposure and sequential composite should be used to compress time and expression into a single frame — the Neue Sehen of simultaneity applied to portraiture.
- **Source**: p.100
- **Quote (DE)**: "Das fixierte Lachkabinett"
- **Quote (EN)**: "The captured laughing cabinet"
- **Category**: neue-sehen

**PRINCIPLE [P-03]**: Der Übermensch — Simultanität des Blicks / The Superman — simultaneity of vision
- **Statement** [INFERRED]: The composite portrait titled "Der Übermensch" oder der "Augenbaum" — a face multiplied with six or more eyes stacked vertically — is not grotesque for its own sake. It is an argument about the compound eye: an organism that sees in all directions simultaneously. The camera can construct this seeing that no human eye performs. This is Neue Sehen as biological fantasy made photographically real.
- **Rule**: Composite photography may construct perceptual organs that do not exist in nature — the result is not surrealism but optical logic pushed to its limit.
- **Source**: p.103
- **Quote (DE)**: "Der »Übermensch« oder der »Augenbaum«"
- **Quote (EN)**: "The 'Superman' or the 'eye-tree'"
- **Category**: neue-sehen

## Part II — Fotomontage

### Principles

**PRINCIPLE [P-04]**: Fotomontage als Simultanität / Photomontage as simultaneity
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: Hannah Höch's "Der Milliardär" assembles fragments — industrial machinery, architectural scale, human figures at conflicting scales, political symbols — into a single image that could never be photographed as a unified scene. This is Simultanität made material: many times, many places, many scales of power, collapsed into one constructed surface. The caption reads: "Das zweifache Gesicht des Herrschers" — the double face of the ruler. Montage is political precisely because it can show contradictions simultaneously.
- **Rule**: Photomontage achieves what no single exposure can — the simultaneous presentation of contradictory realities. Use scale conflict, spatial impossibility, and temporal overlay to reveal what sequential narration conceals.
- **Source**: p.104
- **Quote (DE)**: "»Der Milliardär« — Das zweifache Gesicht des Herrschers."
- **Quote (EN)**: "'The Billionaire' — The double face of the ruler."
- **Category**: simultaneity

**PRINCIPLE [P-05]**: Die Stadt als Fotomontage — Erlebnis des Gigantischen / The City as photomontage — experience of the gigantic
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: Citroen's "Die Stadt" (Fotomontage, Bauhaus) takes the experience of the metropolis — the Steinmeer, the sea of stone — and intensifies it to the gigantic. No single photograph of a city can convey what it is to be inside it: the vertical compression, the simultaneity of scales from street-level to aerial, the overlay of movement and mass. The montage does what the single photograph cannot. Caption: "Das Erlebnis des Steinmeeres wird hier ins Gigantische gesteigert."
- **Rule**: When documenting an experience that is too large or too simultaneous for any single viewpoint, use montage to assemble the totality — the city, the crowd, the machine — from its photographic fragments.
- **Source**: p.105
- **Quote (DE)**: "»Die Stadt« — Das Erlebnis des Steinmeeres wird hier ins Gigantische gesteigert."
- **Quote (EN)**: "'The City' — The experience of the sea of stone is here intensified to the gigantic."
- **Category**: simultaneity

## Part III — Fotoplastik

### Principles

**PRINCIPLE [P-06]**: Fotoplastik — Die Kombinationen des Möglichen / Fotoplastik — the combinations of the possible
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: The Fotoplastik differs from the Fotomontage in that it stages its combination rather than assembling pre-existing fragments. In "Zirkus- und Varietéplakat" (Moholy-Nagy), acrobatic figures, geometric arcs, wire lines, and a standing observer are combined into a spatial construction that could not be staged as a single photograph — yet feels spatially coherent. The caption states the principle directly: "Die Kombinationen des Möglichen ergeben reiche Spannung." The combination of the possible yields rich tension.
- **Rule**: The Fotoplastik constructs a spatial logic from photographically real elements — not collage as disorder, but construction as the assembly of possible spatial relationships into a new visual proposition.
- **Source**: p.106
- **Quote (DE)**: "Zirkus- und Varietéplakat — Die Kombinationen des Möglichen ergeben reiche Spannung."
- **Quote (EN)**: "Circus and variety poster — The combinations of the possible yield rich tension."
- **Category**: production-reproduction

**PRINCIPLE [P-07]**: Fotoplastik als Propagandainstrument / Fotoplastik as propaganda instrument
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: "Militarismus" (Fotoplastik: Moholy-Nagy) — designated explicitly as "Propagandaplakat." The image assembles military vehicles, armed figures, and wounded bodies into a spatial argument against war. The Fotoplastik here is not decorative but rhetorical. It demonstrates that photographic construction has persuasive force: the assembly of fragments into a new spatial whole makes an argument that neither a single photograph nor a painting could make with equal directness.
- **Rule**: Fotoplastik is not limited to aesthetic construction — its capacity to assemble spatial contradiction makes it a rhetorical medium. The designer must understand the political force of spatial assembly.
- **Source**: p.107
- **Quote (DE)**: "»Militarismus« — Propagandaplakat."
- **Quote (EN)**: "'Militarism' — Propaganda poster."
- **Category**: production-reproduction

**PRINCIPLE [P-08]**: Reklame-Fotoplastik — Bewegung als Suggestion / Advertising Fotoplastik — movement as suggestion
- **Statement** [INFERRED]: The "Neumatik" Reklameplakat (Fotoplastik: Moholy-Nagy) combines a racing car with sweeping curved forms and typographic text set at a diagonal. The word "NEUMATIK" (tires/pneumatics) is made to feel as if it is being thrown by the vehicle's speed. This is Fotoplastik in service of advertising: the spatial construction creates a sensation of movement and velocity that neither the photograph of the car nor the text alone could produce.
- **Rule**: In advertising Fotoplastik, compose the spatial elements so that the product's essential quality — speed, power, precision — is felt as a spatial experience, not merely illustrated.
- **Source**: p.108
- **Quote (DE)**: "Reklameplakat — Fotoplastik: MOHOLY-NAGY"
- **Quote (EN)**: "Advertising poster — Fotoplastik: Moholy-Nagy"
- **Category**: production-reproduction

**PRINCIPLE [P-09]**: Amerikanische Pneuwerbung — Fragment als Vollbild / American tire advertisement — fragment as complete image
- **Statement** [INFERRED]: The Vanity Fair "Amerikanische Pneuwerbung" (Fotoplastik: VANITY FAIR, p.109) shows only the arc of a large pneumatic tire — cropped so that the tire fills and overflows the frame, with a cityscape visible through its opening. The fragment of an object, at sufficient scale and cropping, becomes a new spatial proposition. The tire arch frames the city like a portal. This is the close-up logic — Neue Sehen — applied to advertising: extreme cropping transforms a product into an architectural or landscape element.
- **Rule**: Extreme cropping and fragment-as-frame: when a portion of an object is enlarged to dominate the picture field, it becomes a spatial container for other realities. This is Produktion through selective framing.
- **Source**: p.109
- **Quote (DE)**: "Amerikanische Pneuwerbung — Inserat."
- **Quote (EN)**: "American tire advertisement — Insert."
- **Category**: neue-sehen

## Part IV — Typofoto

### Principles

**PRINCIPLE [P-10]**: Typofoto — Text als visuelles Element / Typofoto — text as visual element
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: The cover design for the magazine "Broom" (New York, 1922) — "Titelblattentwurf für die Zeitschrift »Broom«" — is labeled explicitly: "Typofoto: MOHOLY-NAGY." Letters are not set in a line; they are distributed as spatial elements. "BR," "OO," "M" are staggered vertically and at different scales. A three-dimensional cylindrical object occupies the center, and "MARCH" is set in horizontal caps at the bottom. The year "04" appears large and geometric. Typography here is a visual medium as much as photography: letters have weight, scale, spatial position, and three-dimensional context.
- **Rule**: In Typofoto, treat every letterform as a visual element with mass, position, and spatial relationship — not as a phonetic unit that happens to be set in type.
- **Source**: p.110
- **Quote (DE)**: "Titelblattentwurf für die Zeitschrift »Broom« NEW YORK. 1922 — Typofoto MOHOLY-NAGY"
- **Quote (EN)**: "Cover design for the magazine 'Broom' New York, 1922 — Typofoto Moholy-Nagy"
- **Category**: typophoto

## Part V — Fotoplastik und Mythos

### Principles

**PRINCIPLE [P-11]**: Der umgekehrte Mythos / The inverted myth
- **Statement** [INFERRED]: "Leda und der Schwan — Der umgekehrte Mythos" (Fotoplastik: Moholy-Nagy) shows floating figures, a radial burst of lines (perhaps an eye or explosion), and a swan-like form — assembled in a spatial field that is neither earthly nor mythological but photographically constructed. The subtitle "Der umgekehrte Mythos" announces that this is not an illustration of the classical myth but its inversion through photographic construction. The Fotoplastik does not narrate; it deconstructs the narrative and reassembles its elements into a new spatial proposition.
- **Rule**: Fotoplastik may take mythological or literary subject matter and invert it — not by illustrating the story but by using its elements as raw visual material for a new construction.
- **Source**: p.111
- **Quote (DE)**: "Leda und der Schwan — Der umgekehrte Mythos."
- **Quote (EN)**: "Leda and the Swan — The inverted myth."
- **Category**: production-reproduction

## Part VI — Film: Technik, Trick, und optischer Rhythmus

### Argument Structure

**Thesis**: Film is the supreme synthetic medium — but its potential is almost entirely unexploited. The enormous technical apparatus of Hollywood cinema (vast sums spent on feature film production) exists alongside what I regard as a malerische Technik (painterly technique) that remains at an "unendlich primitiven Stufe" — an infinitely primitive level. The techniques available — Filmtrick, slow motion, animation, reversal, abstract film — are barely used.

**Evidence**: A Paramount production photograph of a film set apparatus (p.112); three sequential frames showing a face emerging from blackness via film-reversal technique (p.113); trick-table animation photography demonstrating stop-motion methods (p.114); two film strips from a Lotte Reiniger Tricktischfilm showing animated cutout figures in sequence (p.115); film strips from Arnold Fanck/Hannes Schneider's skiing film "Wunder des Schneeschuhs" demonstrating optical rhythm through sequential action frames (p.116); and an abstract film strip (Viking Eggeling: Diagonalsinfonie, p.117).

**Conclusion**: The contrast is stark: the Filmapparat is technically sublime and economically enormous, yet it serves the most primitive narrative and painterly purposes. The Filmtrick, abstract film, and animation demonstrate what film can do when liberated from the obligation to narrate realist stories. The Diagonalsinfonie (Eggeling) is the proof that film can be pure optischer Rhythmus.

### Principles

**PRINCIPLE [P-12]**: Filmapparat vs. malerische Technik / Film apparatus versus painterly technique
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: "Für die Herstellung eines Spielfilms werden oft Riesensummen verwendet. Gegenüber der Technik und Instrumentation dieser Filme ist die heutige malerische Technik noch auf einer unendlich primitiven Stufe." The resources poured into the feature film are extraordinary; the artistic (malerische) imagination that uses them has barely begun.
- **Rule**: Do not mistake technical elaboration for artistic advancement. The primitive state of film's visual language is hidden by the sophistication of its apparatus. The designer's task is to develop the visual language, not to perfect the apparatus.
- **Source**: p.112
- **Quote (DE)**: "Für die Herstellung eines Spielfilms werden oft Riesensummen verwendet. Gegenüber der Technik und Instrumentation dieser Filme ist die heutige malerische Technik noch auf einer unendlich primitiven Stufe."
- **Quote (EN)**: "For the production of a feature film enormous sums are often spent. Compared to the technology and instrumentation of these films, the current painterly technique is still at an infinitely primitive level."
- **Category**: film

**PRINCIPLE [P-13]**: Filmtrick — Entstehung aus dem Nichts / Film trick — emergence from nothing
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: The Filmtrick plate (p.113) is titled "Ein Gesicht entsteht aus dem Nichts" — a face emerges from nothing. The method is described: "Aufnahme des Kopfes. Erwärmen des Bildstreifens, Abschmelzen der Gelatineschicht und später in Reihenfolge umgekehrte Vorführung." The three sequential frames show a face materializing out of complete blackness, becoming visible through a reversal technique. This is Produktion: the image was created by a process unique to film — the melting and reversal of the gelatin emulsion creates a transformation impossible in any other medium.
- **Rule**: Film reversal and emulsion manipulation are constructive techniques, not accidents. The designer of film must understand that the filmstrip's material properties — its gelatin, its sensitivity, its sequential structure — are instruments for Lichtgestaltung in time.
- **Source**: p.113
- **Quote (DE)**: "Filmtrick: Ein Gesicht entsteht aus dem Nichts. Der Weg dazu: Aufnahme des Kopfes. Erwärmen des Bildstreifens, Abschmelzen der Gelatineschicht und später in Reihenfolge umgekehrte Vorführung."
- **Quote (EN)**: "Film trick: A face emerges from nothing. The method: photographing the head. Warming the filmstrip, melting the gelatin layer, and later showing the sequence in reverse."
- **Category**: film

**PRINCIPLE [P-14]**: Tricktischfilm — Animation als konstruktive Bewegung / Trick-table film — animation as constructive movement
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: The trick-table photograph and its description (p.114) explain stop-motion animation: "Man heftet die ausgeschnittenen Figuren an, und nimmt das Bild eine halbe Umdrehung lang auf, sodann werden die Glieder der Figuren um eine Kleinigkeit verändert, wieder aufgenommen usw., bis eine Bewegung ausgeführt ist." Each frame is a separately constructed position. The two film strips from Lotte Reiniger's Tricktischfilm (p.115) show this as a sequence: small cutout dancing figures caught in incremental positions of movement across the filmstrip.
- **Rule**: Stop-motion and trick-table animation decompose movement into its constructed elements — each frame is a discrete spatial decision. This reveals what movement actually is: a series of positional relationships, not a continuous flow. Design the sequence, not just the individual frame.
- **Source**: p.114–115
- **Quote (DE)**: "Wie Tricktischaufnahmen gemacht werden: Man heftet die ausgeschnittenen Figuren an, und nimmt das Bild eine halbe Umdrehung lang auf, sodann werden die Glieder der Figuren um eine Kleinigkeit verändert, wieder aufgenommen usw., bis eine Bewegung ausgeführt ist."
- **Quote (EN)**: "How trick-table shots are made: The cutout figures are attached, and the image is recorded for half a rotation, then the limbs of the figures are changed by a small amount, re-recorded, etc., until a movement has been executed."
- **Category**: film

**PRINCIPLE [P-15]**: Sportfilm als optischer Rhythmus / Sports film as optical rhythm
- **Statement** [INFERRED]: The film strips from "Wunder des Schneeschuhs" (Arnold Fanck and Hannes Schneider, p.116) show a skier in ten sequential positions across two film strips. The visual rhythm is evident: the figure, small against white snow, traces a curve that develops across the strip. The shadow of the skier creates a secondary rhythm. This is optischer Rhythmus made visible in the filmstrip format: the movement of a body in space, serialized into a sequence of frames, creates a temporal composition with clear visual tempo.
- **Rule**: When presenting sequential film frames, the strip format itself reveals the optical rhythm — the interval between positions, the development of a trajectory. Read the filmstrip as a temporal score.
- **Source**: p.116
- **Quote (DE)**: "Aus dem kinematografisch illustrierten Sportlehrbuch: »Wunder des Schneeschuhs« von Arnold Fanck und Hannes Schneider"
- **Quote (EN)**: "From the cinematographically illustrated sports manual: 'Wonder of the Snow Shoe' by Arnold Fanck and Hannes Schneider"
- **Category**: optical-rhythm

**PRINCIPLE [P-16]**: Diagonalsinfonie — Reiner optischer Rhythmus / Diagonal Symphony — pure optical rhythm
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: The film strips from Viking Eggeling's "Diagonalsinfonie" (p.117) show two strips of abstract forms — on the left strip, linear cursive or calligraphic marks developing incrementally frame by frame; on the right strip, repeated geometric figures (small symmetrical forms, possibly abstract human or mechanical shapes) developing similarly. This is abstract film: no subject matter, no narrative, only the development of visual form in time. The film is pure optischer Rhythmus — the tempo of visual transformation is the content.
- **Rule**: Abstract film operates only through optical rhythm: the rate of transformation of form from frame to frame is the sole compositional parameter. Every design decision must be made in terms of temporal pace, not spatial arrangement alone.
- **Source**: p.117
- **Quote (DE)**: "Tricktischfilm. Viking Eggeling: Diagonalsinfonie"
- **Quote (EN)**: "Trick-table film. Viking Eggeling: Diagonal Symphony"
- **Category**: optical-rhythm

**PRINCIPLE [P-17]**: Telegrafiertes Kino — Übertragung als neues Medium / Telegraphed cinema — transmission as new medium
- **Statement** [INFERRED]: The two blurred, transmitted photographs attributed to "Prof. A. Korn: Telegrafiertes Kino" (Foto: WELTSPIEGEL, p.118) show what appears to be a camera or film apparatus, transmitted photographically by telegraph technology. The images are soft, degraded, yet legible. This is significant: if the photograph can be telegraphed — transmitted over distance as signal — then the spatial and temporal conditions of cinema change. Kino is no longer bound to physical transport of film reels. This is a different mode of Simultanität: simultaneous presence across distance.
- **Rule**: The transmission of images — by telegraph, wire, or signal — is not degradation but a new medium property. The distributed image, degraded by transmission, is its own visual form; its softness and grain are not defects but characteristics of a new Neue Sehen: the image-at-distance.
- **Source**: p.118
- **Quote (DE)**: "Prof. A. Korn: Telegrafiertes Kino"
- **Quote (EN)**: "Prof. A. Korn: Telegraphed cinema"
- **Category**: neue-sehen

The composition is dominated by diagonal motion and the letter-as-object.
- **Type**: Produktion — the sensation of velocity is constructed through the combination of photographic car image, typographic letter-objects, and abstract curved forms; the poster could not be photographed as a unified scene.
- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: P-08 — advertising Fotoplastik; movement as spatial suggestion; also Typofoto principle (letters as visual/spatial elements).

**FIGURE [Fig-11]**: Amerikanische Pneuwerbung / Vanity Fair (p.109) — Fotoplastik: VANITY FAIR
- **Shows**: A cropped close-up of a large pneumatic tire (the "Pennsylvania Vacuum Co." tire, text readable on the tire sidewall), photographed from below so that the arc of the tire frames the top half of the image. The image is dominated by the tire's texture (knobbled tread visible in the upper portion) and its curved form.
- **Type**: Produktion — the fragment-as-frame device, the extreme close-up and cropping that turns the tire into an architectural portal, is a photographic compositional choice that re-contextualizes the industrial object.
- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: P-09 — fragment-as-frame; Neue Sehen through radical cropping; also advertising use of close-up for product as spatial proposition.
- **Text reference**: Caption: "Amerikanische Pneuwerbung — Inserat."
- **Source**: p.109

**FIGURE [Fig-12]**: Titelblattentwurf "Broom" — Moholy-Nagy (p.110) — Typofoto: MOHOLY-NAGY
- **Shows**: A Typofoto cover design for the American magazine "Broom" (New York, 1922). The layout distributes letterforms spatially: large "BR" at upper left; "OO" in the center-right with the two Os forming a vertical column (the second O is rendered as a three-dimensional cylindrical lens or tube object); "4" to the right; a vertical column of letters "V," "N" down the left; "MARCH" in horizontal caps at the bottom. The background is dark, the letters are white/light, creating strong contrast. The composition is non-linear — it must be read spatially, not left-to-right.
- **Type**: Produktion — the Typofoto is a photographically constructed object; the three-dimensional letter-forms and their spatial distribution are photographic, not typeset.
- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: P-10 — Typofoto as spatial design; letters as visual/spatial elements rather than phonetic sequences; the designed page as simultaneous visual field.
- **Text reference**: Caption: "Titelblattentwurf für die Zeitschrift »Broom« NEW YORK. 1922 — Typofoto MOHOLY-NAGY"
- **Source**: p.110

**FIGURE [Fig-13]**: Leda und der Schwan — Der umgekehrte Mythos (p.111) — Fotoplastik: MOHOLY-NAGY
- **Shows**: A Fotoplastik with a white/light background. In the upper-left area, a radial burst of lines emanates from a dark organic center (possibly an eye or a flower-burst form), with a small dark bird-like figure nearby. In the lower-center, a crouched small human figure (possibly a nude). The spatial field is airy and open. The combination is fragmentary and evocative rather than narratively complete.
- **Type**: Produktion — the spatial field and its elements are photographically constructed; the myth is deconstructed, not illustrated.
- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: P-11 — the inverted myth; Fotoplastik as mythological deconstruction rather than illustration.
- **Text reference**: Caption: "Leda und der Schwan — Der umgekehrte Mythos."
- **Source**: p.111

**FIGURE [Fig-14]**: Apparatur der Filmaufnahme — Paramount (p.112) — Foto: PARAMOUNT / Aus dem Lubitschfilm: "Ehe im Kreise"
- **Shows**: A black-and-white documentary photograph of a film studio set during production. A large interior set (a room with painted walls, furniture, a bed) is surrounded by the apparatus of production: multiple film lights on stands, cameras, crew members. The contrast between the illusionistic interior set and the surrounding mechanical apparatus is total. The set is a stage; the apparatus surrounds it completely.
- **Type**: Reproduktion — this is a documentary photograph of an existing scene, recording the film production apparatus. However, it functions in the argument as evidence: the technical apparatus is vast, the visual ambition remains primitive.
- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: P-12 — the film apparatus as context for the argument that malerische Technik lags behind technical investment.
- **Text reference**: Full text block: "Für die Herstellung eines Spielfilms werden oft Riesensummen verwendet. Gegenüber der Technik und Instrumentation dieser Filme ist die heutige malerische Technik noch auf einer unendlich primitiven Stufe."
- **Source**: p.112

**FIGURE [Fig-15]**: Filmtrick — Ein Gesicht entsteht aus dem Nichts (p.113) — Foto: BERLINER ILLUSTRIERTE ZEITUNG
- **Shows**: Three sequential film frames (small, low-contrast) showing a face in progressive stages of emergence from complete blackness: frame 1 — barely a suggestion of form in darkness; frame 2 — a shadowy but recognizable face; frame 3 — a fully visible, bearded elderly face. The sequence demonstrates the film reversal technique: original recording of a face, warming of the filmstrip to melt the emulsion, reversal of the sequence in projection.
- **Type**: Produktion — the emergence of the face from nothing is a film-specific technique; the image could not exist in any other medium.
- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: P-13 — Filmtrick as constructive technique; the gelatin emulsion as a material to be manipulated, not merely exposed.
- **Text reference**: Caption and technical description: "Filmtrick: Ein Gesicht entsteht aus dem Nichts. Der Weg dazu: Aufnahme des Kopfes. Erwärmen des Bildstreifens, Abschmelzen der Gelatineschicht und später in Reihenfolge umgekehrte Vorführung."
- **Source**: p.113

**FIGURE [Fig-16]**: Tricktischaufnahme — Animation method (p.114) — Foto: MAGAZIN / DRESDEN
- **Shows**: A close-up photograph of hands manipulating small cutout animated figures (two figures visible: a rotund character in a suit and a dancer in a skirt) on a flat surface. The hands are adjusting the figures' limb positions. The photograph illustrates the stop-motion animation method described in the caption.
- **Type**: Reproduktion (of an animation process) — but the process it depicts is constructive Produktion; the photograph documents the making of a film-Produktion.
- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: P-14 — stop-motion animation as constructive movement; the decomposition of motion into discrete positional states.
- **Text reference**: Full caption: "Wie Tricktischaufnahmen gemacht werden: Man heftet die ausgeschnittenen Figuren an, und nimmt das Bild eine halbe Umdrehung lang auf, sodann werden die Glieder der Figuren um eine Kleinigkeit verändert, wieder aufgenommen usw., bis eine Bewegung ausgeführt ist."
- **Source**: p.114

**FIGURE [Fig-17]**: Tricktischfilm — Lotte Reiniger film strips (p.115) — Aus einem Tricktischfilm von Lotte Reiniger
- **Shows**: Two vertical film strips side by side, each showing approximately ten frames of animation. Left strip: a figure appears to be in motion (a dancer or character), with each frame showing a slightly different body position — the sequential development of movement visible as a temporal score. Right strip: similar cutout figures in a related sequence. The frames are small but the incremental positional change is legible.
- **Type**: Produktion — animated film; each frame is a constructed image. Lotte Reiniger's silhouette animation is entirely camera-constructed.
- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: P-14 — the filmstrip as temporal score; each frame as a discrete spatial decision in the construction of movement.
- **Text reference**: Caption: "Aus einem Tricktischfilm von Lotte Reiniger"
- **Source**: p.115

**FIGURE [Fig-18]**: Sportfilm — Wunder des Schneeschuhs (p.116) — Aus dem kinematografisch illustrierten Sportlehrbuch
- **Shows**: Two vertical film strips (left: 5 frames, right: 5 frames) showing a skier in sequential positions — descending, turning, stopping — against white snow. The skier is a small dark figure against a light field; a diagonal shadow runs across the snow. Each frame shows a stage in the arc of a skiing movement. The comparison of the two strips (two different sequences or two different moments in the same sequence) creates a visual rhythm.
- **Type**: Reproduktion — standard cinematic photography of an athletic event. However, the serial presentation in filmstrip format reveals optischer Rhythmus as a principle.
- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: P-15 — optischer Rhythmus as revealed by the filmstrip format; the serialization of movement as temporal composition.
- **Text reference**: Caption: "Aus dem kinematografisch illustrierten Sportlehrbuch: »Wunder des Schneeschuhs« von Arnold Fanck und Hannes Schneider im Verlag Gebrüder Enoch, Hamburg"
- **Source**: p.116

**FIGURE [Fig-19]**: Diagonalsinfonie — Viking Eggeling (p.117) — Tricktischfilm
- **Shows**: Two vertical film strips of abstract animation. Left strip: cursive or calligraphic abstract marks (linear, diagonal in orientation) developing incrementally across approximately 12 frames — the marks transform in a flowing, rhythmic progression. Right strip: more regular, symmetrical abstract forms (possibly small human or mechanical silhouette-like shapes) repeating with incremental variations across approximately 12 frames. No representational content. The strips read as visual music notation.
- **Type**: Produktion — pure abstract film; no subject matter exists outside the film medium. The Diagonalsinfonie is exclusively a film-medium construction.
- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: P-16 — pure optischer Rhythmus; abstract film as the decomposition of the visual field into temporal rhythm alone; film as the medium of Lichtspiel in its most constructive form.
- **Text reference**: Caption: "Tricktischfilm. Viking Eggeling: Diagonalsinfonie"
- **Source**: p.117

**FIGURE [Fig-20]**: Telegrafiertes Kino — Prof. A. Korn (p.118) — Foto: WELTSPIEGEL
- **Shows**: Two side-by-side photographs (small format) showing what appears to be a film camera or cinema projection apparatus, transmitted photographically by telegraph (wire photo). Both images are soft and somewhat degraded — the grain and diffusion of the wire-transmission process is visible. The objects are recognizable but the images have the characteristic soft-focus quality of transmitted photographs.
- **Type**: Produktion — the transmission process alters the photographic image; the degraded, transmitted image is not a failed reproduction but a new image-form specific to the medium of wire transmission.
- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: P-17 — telegraphed cinema as Neue Sehen; the distributed image as a new visual form; Simultanität through transmission across distance.
- **Text reference**: Caption: "Prof. A. Korn: Telegrafiertes Kino" / "Foto: WELTSPIEGEL"
- **Source**: p.118
