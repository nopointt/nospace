**Conclusion**: Formal laws of point and line are not merely described — they must be seen operating. The plates are the proof.

## Tab. 5 — Punkt: Der schwarze und weiße Punkt als elementar-farbige Werte

**Caption page**: p.150 (back of plate) — "Der schwarze und weiße Punkt als elementar-farbige Werte" / "The black and white point as elementary color values"

### Principles

**PRINCIPLE [P-01]**: Schwarzer und weißer Punkt als elementar-farbige Werte / Black and white point as elementary color values
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: The point is not only a formal element but also a color element at its most elementary level. Black and white are themselves colors — the extreme poles of the tonal scale — and the point, as the smallest possible form, acts as a pure carrier of this color value. A black circle on a white Grundfläche is not merely a geometric figure; it is a condensed elemental color tension.
- **Rule**: Treat the black point on white and the white point on black as structurally equivalent but tonally inverted — both are valid elemental color carriers; the choice between them must arise from the overall compositional temperature, not from convention.
- **Source**: p.150 (caption), plate on facing page
- **Quote (DE)**: "Der schwarze und weiße Punkt als elementar-farbige Werte"
- **Quote (EN)**: "The black and white point as elementary color values"
- **Category**: point | color

### Visual Evidence

**FIGURE [Fig-01]**: Tab.5 — Black circle on white / White circle on black (two panels)
- **Shows**: Two square frames side by side. Left: white ground with a large black filled circle centered. Right: black ground with a large white filled circle centered. Both circles are identical in size and position within their respective frames — only the ground/figure polarity is reversed.
- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: The inversion demonstrates that the Punkt's formal identity (size, position, tension relative to Grundfläche borders) remains structurally constant while its color value — and therefore its compositional temperature and weight — reverses completely. The black point on white is heavy, advancing, concentrated. The white point on black is light, receding, yet radiating against the dark ground. These are not aesthetic preferences; they are measurable differences in Spannung.
- **Text reference**: Caption states "elementar-farbige Werte" — this is the precise theoretical designation.
- **Source**: p.151 (plate recto) / p.150 (caption verso)

## Tab. 6 — Linie: Dasselbe in Linienform

**Caption page**: p.152 — "Dasselbe in Linienform" / "The same in line form"

### Principles

**PRINCIPLE [P-02]**: Übersetzung vom Punkt zur Linie / Translation from point to line
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: What was demonstrated with the Punkt in Tab.5 — the polarity of black on white vs. white on black — can be translated directly into line form. The same tonal polarity operates for the Linie. This is not a mere stylistic variation; it demonstrates that the color-value principle is independent of which formal element (Punkt or Linie) carries it. The law belongs to the color system, not to any single form.
- **Rule**: When testing any formal law established for the Punkt, immediately verify it in Linienform — the law will either confirm itself as universal or reveal a form-specific exception. Both results are theoretically valuable.
- **Source**: p.152 (caption)
- **Quote (DE)**: "Dasselbe in Linienform"
- **Quote (EN)**: "The same in line form"
- **Category**: line | color

### Visual Evidence

**FIGURE [Fig-02]**: Tab.6 — Black X-cross on white / White X-cross on black (two panels)
- **Shows**: Two square frames side by side. Left: white ground with a large black X formed by two thick crossing diagonal lines (approximately 45° and 135°), creating a bold cross shape. Right: black ground with the identical X rendered in white lines. The X lines are of substantial width — they read more as thick bands than fine lines.
- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: The plate completes the comparison begun in Tab.5. Where Tab.5 used the Punkt as its carrier, Tab.6 uses the Linie (here: two crossing straight lines forming an X). The tonal polarity law holds: black-on-white X is heavy, active, advancing; white-on-black X has equal structural tension but opposite tonal temperature. The crossing of the lines introduces an additional complexity — the intersection point becomes a secondary Punkt by implication.
- **Text reference**: Caption explicitly marks this as "Dasselbe in Linienform" — a direct continuation of Tab.5's argument.

## Tab. 7 — Linie: Mit Punkt an der Grenze der Fläche

**Caption page**: p.154 — "Mit Punkt an der Grenze der Fläche" / "With point at the boundary of the plane"

### Principles

**PRINCIPLE [P-03]**: Punkt an der Grenze der Fläche / Point at the boundary of the plane
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: The position of the Punkt relative to the boundary (Grenze) of the Grundfläche fundamentally alters its compositional character. A point placed at or near the border of the plane is no longer in free compositional space — it enters into direct tension with the boundary itself. The Spannung shifts: instead of radiating freely toward all four borders, the point now pulls strongly toward the nearest edge, creating an asymmetric, directional force.
- **Rule**: Never place a point at the boundary of the plane accidentally — the boundary relationship is one of the strongest compositional forces available. Use it deliberately, or avoid it entirely.
- **Source**: p.154 (caption)
- **Quote (DE)**: "Mit Punkt an der Grenze der Fläche"
- **Quote (EN)**: "With point at the boundary of the plane"
- **Category**: point | tension | plane

### Visual Evidence

**FIGURE [Fig-03]**: Tab.7 — Large black circle with diagonal lines; point at boundary composition
- **Shows**: A full-page (unframed) composition on white ground. Upper-center-left: a very large, heavy black filled circle — the dominant Punkt-mass of the image. From approximately the center, two long diagonal straight lines extend — one sweeping broadly downward-right (the longest element, nearly spanning the full page width), another shorter diagonal crossing it at an angle. Additional thin lines radiate at varying angles. The overall composition is asymmetric and directional; the heavy circle sits near the upper edge, placing it close to the boundary of the implied Fläche.
- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: This is the formal demonstration of a Punkt at the Grenze — the large circle's proximity to the upper-left region of the Grundfläche creates a strong pull toward that corner/boundary. The diagonal lines extending from the compositional center act as counterforces, but they cannot neutralize the boundary pull of the heavy Punkt. The tension between the circle's mass and the boundary is the subject.
- **Text reference**: Caption names the principle directly.
- **Source**: p.155 (plate recto) / p.154 (caption verso)

## Tab. 8 — Linie: Betonte Gewichte in schwarz-weiß

**Caption page**: p.156 — "Betonte Gewichte in schwarz-weiß" / "Emphasized weights in black-white"

### Principles

**PRINCIPLE [P-04]**: Betonte Gewichte / Emphasized weights
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: Within a complex composition, individual elements carry different visual weights. Black areas (filled forms, thick lines) carry maximum weight; white areas (open space, thin lines) carry minimum weight. The emphasis (Betonung) of weight is a compositional tool: by deliberately massing heavy elements at certain positions and contrasting them with light elements, the composer controls the direction and intensity of Spannung throughout the entire Fläche.
- **Rule**: In any composition, identify the weight hierarchy before placing elements — heaviest elements should be placed according to compositional intention, not chance. Their position determines where the viewer's Spannung is anchored.
- **Source**: p.156 (caption)
- **Quote (DE)**: "Betonte Gewichte in schwarz-weiß"
- **Quote (EN)**: "Emphasized weights in black-white"
- **Category**: composition | tension | line | plane

### Visual Evidence

**FIGURE [Fig-04]**: Tab.8 — Complex multi-element composition with circle, arcs, rectangles, diagonals, horizontal bands
- **Shows**: A large-format unframed composition on white ground containing: (1) upper-left: an open circle (ring, not filled) — the lightest large element; (2) extending rightward from near the circle: two long thick diagonal lines, one angled steeply, creating strong directional energy; (3) center: a large thick arc (curved line, heavily weighted); (4) upper-center: thin rectangular outline; (5) lower-center: a second large arc curving downward; (6) lower portion: a band of closely-spaced horizontal parallel lines (thick/thin alternating), creating a dense weight mass; (7) additional thin rectangular outlines scattered across the composition.
- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: The composition systematically deploys weight contrast. The open ring (lightest), the thick diagonals (heavy), the dense horizontal band (heaviest concentration), and the thin outlines (lightest) create a weight map across the Fläche. The Spannung follows the weight gradient — maximum tension at the heavy clusters, minimum at the thin outlines. This is the "betonte Gewichte" principle made visible.
- **Text reference**: Caption identifies the principle as "betonte Gewichte in schwarz-weiß."
- **Source**: p.157 (plate recto) / p.156 (caption verso)

## Tab. 9 — Linie: Die dünnen Linien halten Stand vor dem schweren Punkt

**Caption page**: p.158 — "Die dünnen Linien halten Stand vor dem schweren Punkt" / "The thin lines hold their ground before the heavy point"

### Principles

**PRINCIPLE [P-05]**: Widerstand dünner Linien gegen schweren Punkt / Resistance of thin lines against heavy point
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: Thinness alone does not make a line weak. A multiplicity of thin lines, organized with formal clarity, can maintain compositional equilibrium against a single heavy Punkt of far greater tonal mass. The formal law here is that quantity and structural organization can counterbalance individual weight. This is not a paradox — it is a specific case of compositional Gleichgewicht (equilibrium) achieved through means other than tonal equivalence.
- **Rule**: Do not assume that heavy forms automatically dominate thin forms. A precisely structured network of thin lines can hold its compositional position against a massive Punkt — provided the thin lines are organized, not scattered.
- **Source**: p.158 (caption)
- **Quote (DE)**: "Die dünnen Linien halten Stand vor dem schweren Punkt"
- **Quote (EN)**: "The thin lines hold their ground before the heavy point"
- **Category**: line | tension | composition | Gleichgewicht

### Visual Evidence

**FIGURE [Fig-05]**: Tab.9 — Large black circle (upper-left) vs. thin line construction (center-right and lower)
- **Shows**: Unframed composition on white ground. Upper-left: a very large, very heavy (densely filled, slightly textured) black circle — the dominant Punkt-mass. Lower-right quadrant: two distinct thin-line constructions — (1) a small horizontal axis with short vertical lines crossing it (like a schematic cross-section, lower-left of center); (2) a taller triangular/pyramidal structure built of thin lines — a central vertical with horizontal crossbars at varying intervals, and diagonal lines framing it into a triangular silhouette. The heavy circle and the thin constructions occupy opposite zones of the composition.
- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: The plate demonstrates P-05 directly. The massive black circle on the upper-left creates overwhelming tonal weight, yet the thin-line triangular construction on the lower-right "holds its ground" — it does not dissolve or become invisible before the heavy Punkt. The structural organization of the thin lines (convergent/triangular form, clear hierarchy of parts) gives them compositional presence equal to their formal opposite.
- **Text reference**: Caption states the principle exactly: "Die dünnen Linien halten Stand vor dem schweren Punkt."
- **Source**: p.159 (plate recto) / p.158 (caption verso)

## Tab. 10 — Linie: Zeichnerischer Aufbau eines Teils der "Komposition 4" (1911)

**Caption page**: p.160 — "Zeichnerischer Aufbau eines Teils der 'Komposition 4' (1911)" / "Linear-graphic construction of a part of 'Composition 4' (1911)"

### Principles

**PRINCIPLE [P-06]**: Analytische Zeichnung als Methode / Analytical drawing as method
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: An abstract painting can be subjected to analytical decomposition by isolating its linear skeleton. By extracting only the lines from a complex painting and discarding color, tonal weight, and textural Faktur, one reveals the underlying compositional structure — the pure line-logic of the work. This is not merely an academic exercise; it is the method by which one confirms or questions whether a composition is governed by formal law (Innere Notwendigkeit) or by arbitrary arrangement.
- **Rule**: To understand any composition — your own or another's — isolate its linear structure first. Redraw only the lines. What remains must be coherent by its own formal logic. If it is not, the original composition has a defect of structure.
- **Source**: p.160 (caption)
- **Quote (DE)**: "Zeichnerischer Aufbau eines Teils der 'Komposition 4' (1911)"
- **Quote (EN)**: "Linear-graphic construction of a part of 'Composition 4' (1911)"
- **Category**: line | composition | pedagogy | inner-necessity

### Visual Evidence

- **Shows**: A medium-format unframed drawing on white ground, signed "Kandinsky" lower-left, page number "11" lower-right. The drawing consists entirely of black lines — no fills, no tonal areas. The lines are varied: some are broad brush-strokes, some are angular/broken, some are curved. The overall impression is gestural but not chaotic. Identifiable motifs: (1) a vertical shape with angular top (upper-center) suggesting a figure or tower; (2) a large sweeping diagonal from upper to lower; (3) shorter diagonal lines crossing; (4) irregular angular shapes suggesting landscape or figure fragments. The lines have varying width, suggesting different weights/pressures.
- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: This is the analytical linear extraction from Komposition 4 (1911). The drawing strips away color and fills, leaving only the line skeleton. Applied to an actual major painting (not a hypothetical example), this demonstrates that the method of P-06 is operational on real works — and that a 1911 painting, made before the systematic theory was fully formalized, nonetheless contains an analyzable linear structure.

- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: The linear analysis of Komposition 4 reveals that its dominant structural force is a vertical-diagonal ascent — a movement that combines the warmth-activity of the vertical with the ambiguity and dynamism of the diagonal. This is not a description of what the painting looks like; it is an identification of the formal law governing the placement of all its major line elements. The ascent is not random — it is the Innere Notwendigkeit of that particular composition.
- **Rule**: When analyzing any composition, identify the dominant directional law — is the primary movement horizontal (cold), vertical (warm), or diagonal (transitional)? The answer defines the emotional temperature of the entire work. In Komposition 4: vertical-diagonal = warm-ascending, dynamically unresolved.

## Tab. 12 — Linie: Exzentrischer Aufbau, wobei das Exzentrische durch entstehende Fläche betont ist

**Caption page**: p.164 — "Exzentrischer Aufbau, wobei das Exzentrische durch entstehende Fläche betont ist" / "Eccentric construction, where the eccentric is emphasized by the emerging plane"

### Principles

**PRINCIPLE [P-08]**: Exzentrische Spannung durch entstehende Fläche / Eccentric tension through the emerging plane
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: Eccentric (exzentrisch) compositional tension — tension directed outward, away from the center toward the boundaries — can be intensified when line configurations enclose or imply a secondary Fläche (plane). The enclosed or implied plane becomes a visual concentration of the eccentric force: it makes the outward-directed movement visible not as mere line movement but as a formed area pressing toward the boundary. The Fläche here is not drawn directly — it emerges (entsteht) from the relationships between the lines.
- **Rule**: To maximize eccentric tension in a composition, arrange lines so that their relationships imply enclosed planes. The emerging (entstehende) plane amplifies the eccentric force beyond what the lines alone could achieve.
- **Source**: p.164 (caption)
- **Quote (DE)**: "Exzentrischer Aufbau, wobei das Exzentrische durch entstehende Fläche betont ist"
- **Quote (EN)**: "Eccentric construction, where the eccentric is emphasized by the emerging plane"
- **Category**: line | plane | tension | composition

### Visual Evidence

**FIGURE [Fig-08]**: Tab.12 — Eccentric composition: arc, diagonal curve, horizontal parallel lines, diagonal + rectangle
- **Shows**: A large unframed composition on white ground. Elements: (1) upper-left: a large open arc curving to the right and downward — a curved line of substantial weight; (2) center: a bold diagonal curved stroke sweeping from upper-center downward to lower-left — this is the dominant weight of the composition; (3) upper-right to middle-right: two thick horizontal parallel lines (double-bar form); (4) lower section: a short diagonal straight line (thin-medium weight) and a small vertical rectangle (filled black). The elements are widely spaced; they do not form a closed figure but their spatial relationships imply an enclosed area — particularly between the large arc and the diagonal curve.
- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: The composition demonstrates P-08: the eccentric structure. The large arc (upper-left) and the diagonal stroke (center) together imply an enclosed Fläche between them — a triangular or lens-shaped area that is never drawn but is strongly implied. This "entstehende Fläche" (emerging plane) becomes the site of maximum eccentric tension: the eye feels the outward pull of both bounding lines as if there is something between them pressing to escape the Grundfläche's center. The horizontal parallels (right) and the isolated rectangle (lower) counterpoint the movement without canceling it.
- **Text reference**: Caption explicitly identifies both the eccentric structure and the role of the emerging plane.
- **Source**: p.165 (plate recto) / p.164 (caption verso)

## Tab. 13 — Linie: Zwei Gebogene zu einer Geraden

**Caption page**: p.166 — "Zwei Gebogene zu einer Geraden" / "Two curves [relating] to a straight line"

### Principles

**PRINCIPLE [P-09]**: Verhältnis gebogener Linien zur Geraden / Relationship of curved lines to the straight line
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: The straight line (Gerade) and the curved line (Gebogene) are formally opposed: the Gerade is produced by a single constant force; the Gebogene is produced by two forces in continuously shifting relationship. When two curved lines are placed in relationship to a straight line, the three elements create a formal tension that none could produce alone. The curves introduce alternating-force dynamics; the straight line introduces single-force stability. Their coexistence is a study in the dialogue between these two fundamental line types.
- **Rule**: When composing with curved and straight lines together, understand the force-logic behind each: the Gerade is stable, cold, directed; the Gebogene is dynamic, warm, continuously modulated. Their relationship must be intentional — place them to create productive tension, not to cancel each other.
- **Source**: p.166 (caption)
- **Quote (DE)**: "Zwei Gebogene zu einer Geraden"
- **Quote (EN)**: "Two curves [relating] to a straight line"
- **Category**: line | tension | composition

### Visual Evidence

**FIGURE [Fig-09]**: Tab.13 — Two curved arcs meeting/relating to implied or actual straight; large sweep with small crescent
- **Shows**: Large unframed composition on white ground. The dominant element is a very large, sweeping curved arc that begins in the upper portion of the page and curves broadly downward and leftward, terminating at the base where it meets a short horizontal rectangle (a flat base element — the implied Gerade or stabilizing element). Near the top of the large arc, a small crescent-shaped curve (a second, tightly curled arc) sits against the large curve — it is the second "Gebogene" of the title. The contrast in scale between the large arc (spanning most of the page height) and the small crescent is extreme.
- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: The plate demonstrates the relationship of two curved lines (of vastly different scale) to a straight/stable element (the base rectangle). The large arc's force is produced by a gradually shifting relationship of two forces — it is neither tight nor abrupt. The small crescent at the top has a much tighter radius — two forces more rapidly alternating. The flat rectangle at the base is the Gerade element, anchoring the curves with a stable, single-force line. The compositional question: can a large and a small curve coexist with a straight element without formal incoherence? The answer given here: yes, when the curves share a spatial relationship (the small crescent nesting at the apex of the large arc) and both relate to the same Gerade at the base.
- **Text reference**: Caption names the formal relationship: "Zwei Gebogene zu einer Geraden."
- **Source**: p.167 (plate recto) / p.166 (caption verso)

## Tab. 14 — Linie: Das Langformat begünstigt die Gesamtspannung der wenig gespannten Einzelformen

**Caption page**: p.168 — "Das Langformat begünstigt die Gesamtspannung der wenig gespannten Einzelformen" / "The long format favors the overall tension of the weakly tensioned individual forms"

### Principles

**PRINCIPLE [P-10]**: Grundflächen-Format als Spannungsverstärker / The Grundfläche's format as tension amplifier
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: The format (proportions) of the Grundfläche is not a neutral container — it is itself a compositional force. A long format (Langformat) — a strongly horizontal or strongly vertical rectangle — augments the Gesamtspannung (total tension) of individual forms that would themselves be "wenig gespannt" (weakly tensioned, low-tension) if placed on a more neutral (square-like) ground. This is a specific case of the general law that the Grundfläche actively participates in the tension relationships of any composition.
- **Rule**: When individual compositional elements are themselves low in tension (simple, calm, few forces), choose a long-format Grundfläche to amplify the total compositional tension. A neutral or square format will leave the composition inert; the Langformat activates it.
- **Source**: p.168 (caption)
- **Quote (DE)**: "Das Langformat begünstigt die Gesamtspannung der wenig gespannten Einzelformen"
- **Quote (EN)**: "The long format favors the overall tension of the weakly tensioned individual forms"
- **Category**: plane | tension | composition | Grundfläche

### Visual Evidence

**FIGURE [Fig-10]**: Tab.14 — Abstract composition on tall/long format (caption page only visible; plate implied from facing recto)
- **Shows**: The caption page for Tab.14 is p.168. The facing plate (recto, p.169 in book terms, which falls in the next batch) contains the actual plate. The caption page itself shows a faint ghost image from the plate visible through the thin paper — a large sinuous/S-curved shape is dimly visible, suggesting the plate contains one or more low-tension curved forms on a long-format ground.
- **Demonstrates** [INFERRED]: Based on the caption's principle (P-10) and the visible ghost: the plate shows weakly-tensioned individual forms (low-energy curves, few and simple elements) placed on a Langformat Grundfläche, demonstrating that the format itself generates the Gesamtspannung that the elements alone could not provide.
- **Text reference**: Caption states the formal principle directly: "Das Langformat begünstigt die Gesamtspannung der wenig gespannten Einzelformen."
- **Source**: p.168 (caption verso)
