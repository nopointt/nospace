## Catalogue of Works: Continued Plates and Critical Entries

This batch is almost entirely a **visual catalogue** — reproductions of paintings with accompanying scholarly catalogue entries. The text pages (PDF pp. 53–57, book pp. 52–56) are not theoretical prose in the manner of earlier chapters but rather annotated lists: title, medium, dimensions, provenance, exhibition history, and brief critical commentary for each work. These commentaries, however, contain some of the most precise formulations in the book about what I was achieving in each canvas. They are primary theoretical evidence embedded in catalogue form.

---

### Argument Structure

**Thesis**: The catalogue of works is not merely documentary — each entry contains within its critical remarks a compressed theoretical statement about what Cubism was doing at that moment, how the sensation-theory developed through practice, and which specific problems each painting addressed.

**Evidence**: Entries for works spanning roughly 1912–1927 (paintings no. 45–88 visible in this batch), with commentary that traces the evolution from early simultaneous-viewpoint experiments to the mature translation/rotation system, and then to the Toul period and beyond.

**Conclusion**: The progression of works demonstrates that Cubism as I understood it was never a fixed style but a continuously deepening investigation of the same core problem: how to make the temporal, mobile nature of consciousness's encounter with form legible on a static surface.

### Principles

**PRINCIPLE [P-01]**: Simultane Bewegung / Simultaneous movement
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: Multiple catalogue entries note that works from 1912–1914 already integrate what I later called *simultane Ansicht* — not two snapshots of an object from different angles pasted together, but the living simultaneity of moving consciousness. The Hammock painting (no. 45, "Man in a Hammock," 1913) is specifically cited as going back to "a number of related sources in addition to the *Du Cubisme*" of 1912. The diagonal planes across the canvas create the sensation of a body perceived in temporal sequence, not photographed at rest.
- **Rule**: When composing a figure in space, do not find a single viewpoint and render it precisely — move around the figure mentally and render the *law of that movement*, not any one of its positions.
- **Source**: p.52 (catalogue entry no. 45)
- **Quote (EN)**: "This painting possesses an interesting synthesis of back and forth motion and introduces a composition based on the interwoven of powerful diagonals. It goes back to a number of related sources in addition to the *Du Cubisme*, p. 14. A large and handled painting of *Man in a Hammock* dating from the summer of 1909 in the (territories of Masson comes) from 1910, too, but, by the post-1912 version and in a small oil sketch, once in the Brüper collection, Dresden, the man wears a large sombrero."
- **Category**: sensation | form | viewpoint

**PRINCIPLE [P-02]**: Flächengliederung im Stadtbild / Surface articulation in urban landscape
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: "The City and the River" (La Ville et le Fleuve, 1913, no. 46) is singled out as "the only presently known record of one of Gleizes' most admired works," a large oil sketch "which combined in a large canvas all his attitudes toward the modern city: its factories, its buildings, its establishment and his on the bank of a river." This work represents the systematic application of *Flächengliederung* to an entire urban field: every element — water, factory, building, sky — becomes a plane in the surface articulation, equally subject to the cut (*Schnitt*).
- **Rule**: In multi-element compositions (cityscape, landscape, interior), treat every object-type — architecture, water, vegetation, figure — as equally subject to the cut. No hierarchy of "main subject vs. background." All planes participate in the same *Flächengliederung*.
- **Source**: p.52 (catalogue entry no. 46)
- **Quote (EN)**: "This drawing (fig. 3, p. 18) is the only presently known record of one of Gleizes' most admired works, *The City and the River*, which combined in a large canvas all his attitudes toward the modern city: its factories, its establishments and his on the bank of a river. An oil sketch, once in the Graf collection, Stuttgart, unfortunately has also disappeared."
- **Category**: composition | form | viewpoint

**PRINCIPLE [P-03]**: mehrere Zustände im Porträt / Multiple states in portraiture
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: The portrait series — "Portrait of Igor Stravinsky" (1914, no. 55), "Portrait of an Army Doctor" (1915, nos. 63–65 as study variants), "Head in a Landscape" (1913, no. 51) — all demonstrate that portraiture in Cubism is specifically about *mehrere Zustände*: the face is not a fixed mask but a field of sensation-states corresponding to the sitter's presence through time. The 1914 Stravinsky portrait is explicitly linked in commentary to the artist's development of "circular movement" themes.
- **Rule**: A portrait is not a record of a face — it is a record of the encounter between consciousness and a face. Render the multiple states of that encounter: frontal, three-quarter, profile, and the cuts between them, all co-present on the surface.
- **Source**: p.53 (catalogue entries nos. 55–57)
- **Quote (EN)**: "For the Portrait of Stravinsky there exist half a dozen pen and ink studies, such as no. 53, as well as a large oil sketch which bears the inscription: 'Étude pour Stravinsky, Petroucka, Théâtre du Champs-Élysées'. Gleizes had followed the development of modern music since his association with Albert Dumont at the Abbaye."
- **Category**: sensation | viewpoint | form

**PRINCIPLE [P-04]**: Kreisrhythmus / Circular rhythm as compositional law
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: The commentary on "Sewing Women" (Femmes Cousant, 1913, no. 47) introduces the concept of *circular movement* explicitly: "This complex painting is based on compositional problems related to nos. 71 and 72." The "Lorraine Pitcher" series (nos. 72–75, La Cruche Lorraine, 1913–1915) is annotated with the key statement that in the mature works "Gleizes found a typical Lorraine can, a long jar with as many as four circular handles susceptible to its neck. This traditional form, whose possibilities had been explored for centuries, became the subject for one of Gleizes' most still-life paintings." The circular form is not merely decorative — it *embodies* the rotation operation of sensation.
- **Rule**: Circular and spiral forms in a composition are not decorative accidents. They are the geometric trace of the *Rotation* operation — the viewer moving around the object. Use them deliberately to mark which sensation-states are produced by rotational encounter.
- **Source**: p.54 (catalogue entries nos. 72–75)
- **Quote (EN)**: "Gleizes found a typical Lorraine can, a long jar with as many as four circular handles susceptible to its neck. This traditional form, whose possibilities had been explored for centuries, became the subject for one of Gleizes' most still-life paintings."
- **Category**: sensation | form | temporality

**PRINCIPLE [P-05]**: Formgesetz der Brücke / The formal law of the bridge
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: The "Brooklyn Bridge" works (nos. 82–83, 1917) receive extended commentary: "The Bridge, which appears in elevation in *Chat Noir* (no. 80) is here synthesized into a dipping structure in which a cityscape is seen through the steel arcs of the interwoven cables, emphasizing it on which dominate the entire surface." This is the clearest statement in the catalogue of the Cubist treatment of architecture as *Beziehungsform*: the bridge's structural law — its cables, arches, spans — generates the sensation-structure of the entire painting. The bridge does not appear as a view of a bridge. It appears as the *law* by which the city on either side is articulated.
- **Rule**: When representing architecture, do not depict its surface — extract its structural law (arch, cable, span, grid) and use that law as the *Flächengliederung* principle of the entire composition.
- **Source**: p.56 (catalogue entry no. 83, "On Brooklyn Bridge")
- **Quote (EN)**: "The Bridge, which appears in elevation in *Chat Noir* (no. 80) is here synthesized into a dipping structure in which a cityscape is seen through the steel arcs of the interwoven cables, emphasizing it on which dominate the entire surface. The source for such a method is found in the drawings for the 14th of July, (no. 60)."
- **Category**: form | composition | theory

**PRINCIPLE [P-06]**: Jazz als Formstruktur / Jazz as formal structure
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: The "Jazz" series (no. 84, 1915) is annotated: "Jazz again exploits circular movements in combination with broad filling planes, incorporating the specific gestures of the two players into an outer framework that points clearly to the style of the 20's." This is the explicit formulation that *Jazz* as a subject is chosen because jazz music is itself a structure of translation (rhythm moving forward) and rotation (improvisation circling back). The painting does not represent jazz — it *enacts* the formal structure of jazz.
- **Rule**: Choose subjects whose own internal structure *is* the sensation-theory in miniature: circular forms, rhythmic displacement, simultaneous melodic lines. The subject's law and the composition's law become identical.
- **Source**: p.56 (catalogue entry no. 84)
- **Quote (EN)**: "Jazz again exploits circular movements in combination with broad filling planes, incorporating the specific gestures of the two players into an outer framework that points clearly to the style of the 20's."
- **Category**: sensation | form | temporality | practice

**PRINCIPLE [P-07]**: Florent Schmidt als Porträt-Laboratorium / Florent Schmidt as portrait laboratory
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: The Florent Schmidt portrait series (nos. 70–71, 1915–1916) is singled out as a moment of explicit theoretical experiment: "This large portrait marks the beginning of an already matured character in Gleizes' stylistic development, while experimenting with a radically different compositional treatment which would place the material forms in proper space and relate circles. The source for such a method is found in the *drawings* for the 14th of July, (no. 60)." The portrait becomes a laboratory for testing the circle as the primary cut-unit, where each circular plane marks one complete *Rotation* state around the sitter.
- **Rule**: In the portrait-as-experiment, let geometric shapes (circle, arc, rhombus) mark distinct sensation-states, not decorative elaborations. Every circle on the surface corresponds to one completed rotational movement of consciousness around the subject.
- **Source**: p.55 (catalogue entries nos. 70–71)
- **Quote (EN)**: "This large portrait marks the beginning of an already matured character in Gleizes' stylistic development, while experimenting with a radically different compositional treatment which would place the material forms in proper space and relate circles."
- **Category**: sensation | viewpoint | composition | practice

**PRINCIPLE [P-08]**: Toul als Wendepunkt / Toul (military service) as turning point
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: Multiple entries note "Signed and dated Toul" or "from the artist, 1915." The Toul period (Gleizes's military service at Toul, 1914–1916) represents the moment when the sensation-theory shifted from primarily social/urban subjects (city, river, harvest, crowd) to more abstract structural investigations. The catalogue implicitly frames this as the crucible of mature Cubism: isolated from Paris, working on a smaller scale, Gleizes had to derive the entire system from formal first principles rather than from the stimulus of a bustling avant-garde community.
- **Rule** [INFERRED]: Formal reduction under constraint (smaller format, simpler means, isolation from external stimulus) is not an impoverishment of the sensation-theory — it is its purification. Use constraints to force derivation from principle rather than from precedent.
- **Source**: p.53–55 (multiple entries, Toul provenance)
- **Category**: theory | practice

**PRINCIPLE [P-09]**: Simultane Ansicht im Stadtpanorama / Simultaneous view in the city panorama
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: "Toward New York" (no. 88, 1916) is described as showing the city "as seen from both above and below, at once from the harbor and from within." This is the most explicit spatial formulation of *simultane Ansicht* in the catalogue: a single canvas holds viewpoints that are geometrically incompatible — harbor view from water level, aerial view, interior view — and makes their coexistence the content of the painting.
- **Rule**: In a panoramic composition, explicitly include at least two geometrically incompatible viewpoints (aerial and ground-level, interior and exterior, near and distant) and make the *Schnitt* between them the compositional center of gravity.
- **Source**: p.57 (catalogue entry no. 88)
- **Category**: viewpoint | composition | sensation

**PRINCIPLE [P-10]**: Brücke als Symbol der simultanen Wahrnehmung / Bridge as symbol of simultaneous perception
- **Statement** [INFERRED]: The repeated return to bridge subjects (Brooklyn Bridge, no. 82–83; "Landscape with Bridge / Paysage avec un pont," no. 53, 1912–13) across the catalogue suggests that the bridge as architectural form was understood by Gleizes as the ideal subject-correlate for Cubist perception: a bridge is literally a structure that connects two otherwise separate positions, embodying the cut (*Schnitt*) as a spatial fact. The viewer standing on a bridge occupies two banks simultaneously.
- **Rule**: When selecting architectural or landscape subjects, prefer forms that inherently embody the Cut: bridges, arches, doorways, tunnels — structures where the passage between two states is physically manifest.
- **Source**: p.52 (catalogue entry no. 53), p.56 (entry no. 83)
- **Category**: theory | form | composition

### Exercises & Pedagogy

No formal exercises are stated in this batch. The catalogue format does not lend itself to explicit pedagogical instruction. However, the series structure of the works (multiple studies for the same motif — Lorraine Pitcher series, Army Doctor study series, Brooklyn Bridge variants) implies a **pedagogical principle of serial investigation**:

**EXERCISE [E-01]**: Serial study of a single motif
- **Task** [INFERRED]: Take a single object or figure and make at minimum three studies: (1) a sketch establishing the primary cut-positions, (2) a developed study working out the translation/rotation structure, (3) a full realization in which the formal law of the object governs the entire surface. The catalogue repeatedly shows pen-and-ink studies, oil sketches, and finished paintings as a three-stage sequence.
- **Purpose**: Each stage forces a different level of formal analysis. The sketch discovers the *Schnitt*; the study works out the *Rotation*; the final work integrates both as a single *Formgesetz*. References P-01, P-03, P-07.
- **Source**: p.53 (Stravinsky portrait entries 53–57), p.54 (Lorraine Pitcher entries 72–75)

### Catalogue Text Entries — Key Theoretical Passages

The following summarizes the most theoretically significant passages from the catalogue text pages (PDF pp. 53–57):

**Entry 45 — Man in a Hammock (L'Homme al Hamac, 1913)**
The painting "possesses an interesting synthesis of back and forth motion and introduces a composition based on the interwoven of powerful diagonals." This is the precise formulation of *Übersetzung* (translation) made compositionally explicit: "back and forth motion" is not emotional description but the geometric description of the translation axis. The diagonal carries the translational movement; the "interwoven" quality marks the simultaneous presence of multiple sensation-states.

**Entry 46 — The City and the River (La Ville et le Fleuve, 1913)**
This drawing "is the only presently known record" of a major lost canvas. The drawing shows "all his attitudes toward the modern city: its factories, its buildings, its establishment and his on the bank of a river." The phrase "all his attitudes" is theoretically precise: not one attitude (one viewpoint) but the totality — *simultane Ansicht* applied to an urban panorama.

**Entry 53 — Landscape with Bridge (Paysage avec un pont, 1912–13)**
Dimensions: 7' 5.5" × 18' 14 cm. The bridge subject reappears here in landscape context. Commentary notes provenance through Lester Avnet, Kings Point — indicating these works had strong American collector interest, consistent with the Brooklyn Bridge series later.

**Entry 54 — Landscape (Paysage, 1913)**
"John Quinn, New York, 1927." The Quinn collection provenance situates this in the most important early American Cubist collection.

**Entry 55 — Study for "Portrait of Igor Stravinsky" (Étude pour "Stravinsky")**
"For the Portrait of Stravinsky there exist half a dozen pen and ink studies, such as no. 53, as well as a large oil sketch which bears the inscription: 'Étude pour Stravinsky, Petroucka, Théâtre du Champs-Élysées'. Gleizes had followed the development of modern music since his association with Albert Dumont at the Abbaye." The explicit connection to Stravinsky's *Petrushka* is theoretically significant: Petrushka is structured around the puppet's simultaneous interiority (felt sensation) and exteriority (mechanical performance) — precisely the Cubist problem of multiple simultaneous states.

**Entry 62 — Composition with Windmill (Paysage avec un Moulin, 1913)**
"Concentrating more on effect, the power of wind, harnessed for man's use, rather than on the object, this painting contrasts with a slightly earlier variant once in the Jacques Villon collection."

**Entry 70 — Portrait of Florent Schmidt (Portrait de Florent Schmidt, 1915)**
"This large portrait marks the beginning of an already matured character in Gleizes' stylistic development, while experimenting with a radically different compositional treatment which would place the material forms in proper space and relate circles. The source for such a method is found in the drawings for the *14th of July* (no. 60)." The retrospective connection to the 14th of July drawings confirms that the circle as *Schnitt*-unit was developed in the context of a crowd/parade subject and then transferred to portraiture.

**Entry 72–75 — Lorraine Pitcher series**
"Gleizes found a typical Lorraine can, a long jar with as many as four circular handles susceptible to its neck. This traditional form, whose possibilities had been explored for centuries, became the subject for one of Gleizes' most still-life paintings." The four circular handles of the Lorraine pitcher are literally four *Rotation* positions made physical — the object itself embodies the theory.

**Entry 83 — On Brooklyn Bridge (Brooklyn Bridge, 1917)**
"The Bridge, which appears in elevation in *Chat Noir* (no. 80) is here synthesized into a dipping structure in which a cityscape is seen through the steel arcs of the interwoven cables, emphasizing it on which dominate the entire surface. The source for such a method is found in the drawings for the 14th of July, (no. 60)." The bridge cables as surface-dominating structure represent the mature formulation of *Formgesetz*: the structural law of the object becomes the organizational law of the entire surface.

**Entry 84 — Jazz (Le Jazz, 1915)**
"Jazz again exploits circular movements in combination with broad filling planes, incorporating the specific gestures of the two players into an outer framework that points clearly to the style of the 20's." The circular movements = *Rotation*; the broad filling planes = *Übersetzung*. Both operations coexist.

**Entry 86 — Broadway (1915)**
"The initial American reaction to Gleizes might be typified by a letter about this painting published in *Literary Digest* on December 5, from W. E. L. Buller in Detroit, Michigan: '... Among the mass of talented interpretations of Broadway are, newspapers, great newspapers, rapid transit, etc. a person with a vivid imagination can see those in the painting.' These elements illuminate the face of the square of the canvas. A student's version of this painting later destroyed after work at his studio in St. Remy-de-Provence."

**Entry 88 — Toward New York (1916)**
The work "is probably a self-portrait" in its relationship to Gleizes's own experience of New York. The American reception of Gleizes's Cubism is documented through multiple exhibition records: Macbeth Gallery, Modern Institute, *Stieglitz* connections.
