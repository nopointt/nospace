## Plate Pages: Visual Evidence

### Argument Structure (Plates Section)
**Thesis**: [INFERRED] The selection and sequencing of reproductions constitutes an argument through visual demonstration — the plates show the progression from early Cubist fragmentation (multiple simultaneous viewpoints still tied to recognizable subjects) toward mature simultaneous movement (Übersetzung + Rotation fully integrated, surface as its own law).
**Evidence**: Color and B&W reproductions spanning approximately 1910–1924, including figure paintings, portraits, city scenes, still lifes, and near-abstract heads.
**Conclusion**: [INFERRED] The catalogue demonstrates that the Cubist operation is consistent across subject matter — the same principle of simultane Ansicht and la coupure (Schnitt) governs figures, landscapes, still lifes, and architectural subjects equally.

### Principles (Extracted from Catalogue Critical Notes)

**PRINCIPLE [P-01]**: Simultane Ansicht / Simultaneous viewpoint — development through the circus and music-hall subject
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: The catalogue entries for works from the 1916–1922 period (nos. 90–100 range) repeatedly note that the circus theme — clowns, acrobats, performers — appeared over several years in Gleizes's work, with a second version sometimes noted. The circus/music-hall subject provided the ideal demonstrating ground because performance subjects are inherently temporal: the performer is never at rest, the audience encounters them across time.
- **Rule**: Choose subjects that are inherently temporal — where the subject exists only in movement — to demonstrate that perception is always a temporal encounter, never a snapshot.
- **Source**: p.75
- **Quote (DE/FR)**: "Gleizes rarely painted still lifes, his epic interests usually finding sympathetic echos in more inclusive themes."
- **Quote (EN)**: [Same — text on these pages is in English, being the English-language catalogue]
- **Category**: theory | sensation | temporality

**PRINCIPLE [P-02]**: Composition as abstract equivalent of earlier figural forms
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: The catalogue entry for no. 111 (STUDY FOR "ON A VAUDEVILLE THEME") states explicitly: "Although not the last painting of the circus theme, this is in many respects the culmination of Gleizes' treatment of the dynamic figure. Derived from a 1914 painting in the collection of Madame Gleizes whose treatment of the two elements are already clearly identifiable, this work is abstract, employing tilting planes and circular movement to express the essence of dramatic rhythm."
- **Rule**: The abstract composition is not a departure from subject but an advance into das Wesentliche — when the formal law is fully understood, the subject can be dropped without losing anything that matters.
- **Source**: p.75 (entry for no. 111)

**PRINCIPLE [P-03]**: Übersetzung und Rotation / Translation and rotation as complementary operations in a single work
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: The catalogue entry for no. 121 (WOMAN WITH BLACK GLOVE) states: "This is an example of the principles of translation and rotation at work. **PRINCIPLE [P-04]**: La coupure at architectural scale / The Schnitt applied to building and space
- **Statement** [INFERRED]: The series of works from the Barcelona period (1916–1917), including the "Bermuda Studies" and "Barcelona" compositions (nos. 100–109 range), show Gleizes applying simultane Ansicht to architectural and landscape subjects. The catalogue notes the "Bermuda period" as producing works organized around "circular movements" and "volumetric construction of tilting planes."
- **Rule**: The Cubist operation applies identically to architectural space as to the human figure — the cut between sensation-states of a building or landscape is as compositionally fundamental as the cut between views of a face.
- **Source**: p.75–76
- **Quote (DE/FR)**: "Returning to America early in 1917, the Gleizes left almost immediately for Bermuda, where they stayed about two months. The effect of Bermuda's mild weather, lush foliage and pastel colors directly influenced Gleizes' style, which became smoother or more placid. These paintings emphasized a design for simple formal and intellectual concerns which Gleizes had already begun to deal with. The parallel brushstrokes indicate also a temporary return to a Cézannesque technique."

**PRINCIPLE [P-05]**: Cézannesque technique as Cubism's preparatory gesture
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: The catalogue entry explicitly references a "temporary return to a Cézannesque technique" in the Bermuda paintings, linking parallel brushstrokes to the broader Cubist project. This confirms the genealogical argument: Cézanne's method of building form through sensation-response (the parallel stroke registering the sensation of surface-encounter) is the immediate precursor to the Cubist Schnitt.
- **Rule**: Parallel brushstroke builds sensation of surface before the cut is introduced; the cut is the next logical operation after Cézanne — not a break from him but an advance along the same axis.
- **Source**: p.75–76
- **Quote (DE/FR)**: "The parallel brushstrokes indicate also a temporary return to a Cézannesque technique."

**PRINCIPLE [P-07]**: The "Iberian period" — concentrated circular compositions as theory in practice
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: The catalogue entry for works from the Barcelona period (nos. 103–110 range) notes that "the brief Iberian period produced a host of similar paintings in which specific patterns and motifs were exploited in an effort to place the painting in the context of a precise environment" with "circular motions" as the organizing principle. **PRINCIPLE [P-09]**: "Imaginary Still Life" as Nature Morte Imaginaire — reality of sensation over reality of things
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: Multiple catalogue entries distinguish between "still life" (nature morte) and "imaginary still life" (nature morte imaginaire, VERDÄTRE). These are explicitly different categories. The "imaginary" category signals works where the objects are not observed but generated by the sensation-logic of the composition itself. The entries note: "By the time Gleizes painted the two Imaginary Still Lifes of 1924, his conceptual formulation of surface was almost wholly formed and still life — as a subject in itself — was almost insignificant for he felt that every day objects provided a 'support of sensation.' An 'imaginary' still life, however, was more complex in subject matter; it could reflect ideal relations, pure and non-tentative forms."
- **Rule**: The "imaginary" subject — forms generated by the internal logic of sensation rather than observed from nature — is the highest form of Cubist composition. The subject serves the sensation; it does not dictate it.
- **Source**: p.79 (entries for nos. 129–131)
- **Quote (DE/FR)**: "every day objects provided a 'support of sensation.' An 'imaginary' still life, however, was more complex in subject matter; it could reflect ideal relations, pure and non-tentative forms."

**PRINCIPLE [P-12]**: "For a Painting on a Familiar Theme" — the familiar subject as pedagogy
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: The catalogue entry for no. 127 (FOR A PAINTING ON A FAMILIAR THEME / POUR UNE PEINTURE FAMILIÈRE, 1923) identifies this as a gouache and watercolor study. The "familiar theme" designation is significant — Gleizes uses the ordinary, the everyday, the subject that does not demand special knowledge, as the pedagogical vehicle for the Cubist operation. The familiar subject removes the distraction of content and makes the formal operation visible to the viewer.
- **Rule**: Use the familiar subject as a pedagogical tool — when the viewer already knows what the thing is, they can attend to how the consciousness encounters it rather than what it is. The familiar subject is the most transparent vehicle for sensation-theory.

### Exercises & Pedagogy

**EXERCISE [E-01]**: Study→Series method (from catalogue description of Equestrian works)
- **Task** [CERTAIN]: Make a study drawing that successfully articulates a single sensation-structure (e.g., translation-dominant upward thrust, or rotation-dominant circular sweep). Use that drawing as a "departure point" (as the catalogue states for no. 119) for a series of paintings, each exploring a different resolution of the same formal problem.
- **Purpose**: Trains sustained investigation of a single formal law — the series form is the natural unit of Cubist research. References P-08.
- **Source**: p.77

---

**EXERCISE [E-02]**: Abstract-from-figure method (from catalogue description of Vaudeville Theme)
- **Task** [CERTAIN]: Begin with a figural subject (a dancer, a musician, a circus performer — temporal, kinetic subjects are optimal). Work the composition until it is "abstract, employing tilting planes and circular movement to express the essence of dramatic rhythm" (catalogue text). The test: remove all subject-identifying features — does the sensation-structure remain coherent?
- **Purpose**: Trains access to das Wesentliche — the formal law that generates the subject's appearances. References P-02.
- **Source**: p.75

---

**EXERCISE [E-03]**: Translation/Rotation isolation and combination (from catalogue description of no. 121)
- **Task** [CERTAIN]: Make three compositions of the same subject: (1) translation-dominant (the consciousness moves toward/through the subject — use strong directional lines, vertical or horizontal thrust); (2) rotation-dominant (the consciousness circles the subject — use arcs, circular structures, concentric or eccentric curves); (3) combined simultane Bewegung (both operations present simultaneously). Compare the three.
- **Purpose**: Trains explicit awareness of the two fundamental perceptual operations and their combination. References P-03, P-01.
- **Source**: p.78

---

**EXERCISE [E-04]**: "Imaginary" subject generation
- **Task** [INFERRED]: Instead of painting a subject from observation, generate a composition from "pure and non-tentative forms" — forms that obey only the internal logic of sensation-structure. The test of success: the composition should "reflect ideal relations" rather than depict observed appearances. The familiar-subject method (P-12) can be a bridge: begin with a known subject, progressively subordinate it to the sensation-logic until it becomes imaginary.
- **Purpose**: Trains Form als Gesetz — the ability to work from the law of form rather than from the appearance of things. References P-09, P-12.
- **Source**: p.79

### Notes on Provenance and Historical Context

The catalogue entries confirm several historical facts relevant to understanding the theoretical development:

1. **New York 1915–1917 period**: Gleizes was in New York and Tarrytown (NY) during 1915–1916, producing works directly responding to the New York urban environment. The city-scene compositions with text fragments (nos. 80, 87) belong to this period. The American city was experienced as a maximally intense environment of simultaneous sensation-states — signage, movement, architectural layering — and Gleizes read it through the Cubist operation immediately.

2. **Barcelona 1916–1917 period**: The "Iberian period" produced concentrated circular compositions. The architecture of Barcelona — Gaudí's curved surfaces, the Mediterranean light, the harbor views — may have contributed to the Rotation-dominant compositional tendency of this period.

3. **Bermuda 1917**: The "Bermuda period" produced a temporary return to "Cézannesque technique" — parallel brushstrokes, atmospheric color, less aggressive Schnitt-articulation. This is not a retreat from Cubism but a re-grounding in sensation-response before advancing further.

4. **Paris 1920s**: The mature works (nos. 119–138) show the theory fully developed — the distinction between "imaginary" and observed still life, the systematic exploration of Übersetzung and Rotation as named formal categories, the use of specific compositions as illustrations for theoretical texts (*Le Peintre et ses Lois*).

5. **Institutional presence**: Works in this batch are documented in collections including: The Solomon R. Guggenheim Museum (New York), Musée National d'Art Moderne (Paris), Galerie Louis Carré (Paris), Galerie Lucien Blanc (Aix-en-Provence), Museum of Western Art (Moscow), and private collections in France, UK, and USA. This confirms the theoretical seriousness with which Gleizes's Cubism was received — it was not marginal but institutionally central.
