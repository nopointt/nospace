## Section: Plate Pages — Color Reproductions

### Argument Structure
**Thesis**: [INFERRED] The color plates in this section demonstrate the full range of Gleizes's application of simultaneous movement — from early analytical Cubism through the post-1930 synthetic and devotional works.
**Evidence**: Plates numbered approximately 90–121 in the book's figure sequence, including large color reproductions and dense b/w multi-image pages.
**Conclusion**: [INFERRED] The progression of the plates enacts the argument of the text: sensation-states multiply, the Cut becomes more decisive, translation and rotation are increasingly separated and then reunited as the mature style consolidates.

### Principles

**PRINCIPLE [P-01]**: mehrere Zustände / multiple states
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: The color plate on p.80 (book fig. approx. 90) shows a fully developed Cubist composition in which no single reading position dominates. Faceted planes in yellow, red, orange, blue, dark brown, and grey-white coexist without recession into a unified spatial illusion. Each color zone constitutes a distinct sensation-state — the eye cannot resolve them into a single viewpoint.
- **Rule**: Allow each color zone to stand as a complete sensation-state; do not resolve the surface into a single spatial reading.
- **Source**: p.80
- **Quote (DE)**: [No text on this page]
- **Quote (EN)**: [No text on this page]
- **Category**: sensation

**PRINCIPLE [P-02]**: Flächengliederung / surface articulation
- **Statement** [INFERRED]: The composition on p.80 demonstrates Flächengliederung at its most explicit: the surface is organized as a mosaic of angled planes, each with its own color and tonal identity, articulated against each other through sharp edges (la coupure). No plane bleeds into another without a defined boundary.
- **Rule**: Every plane must have a defined boundary against its neighbor — the Cut is not a transition but a declaration of difference between sensation-states.
- **Source**: p.80
- **Quote (DE)**: [No text on this page]
- **Quote (EN)**: [No text on this page]
- **Category**: composition

**PRINCIPLE [P-03]**: Rotation / rotation (movement around)
- **Statement** [INFERRED]: The large color composition on p.89 (book fig. XXIX) shows a work organized primarily around rotational movement. A dominant circular arc sweeps from upper-left to lower-right, enclosing a central column of stacked vertical elements. The outer planes echo this arc. The entire surface is structured as a movement around a central axis — the viewer's sensation is of circumambulating the form, not translating across it.
- **Rule**: To encode rotation, organize surface planes as arcs or fans that reference a common center, compelling the eye to move around rather than across.
- **Source**: p.89
- **Quote (DE)**: [No text on this page]
- **Quote (EN)**: [No text on this page]
- **Category**: temporality

**PRINCIPLE [P-04]**: Übersetzung und Rotation / translation and rotation combined
- **Statement** [INFERRED]: The large color composition on p.90 (book fig. XXI) combines both fundamental movements. A strong diagonal from lower-left to upper-right enacts translation — the eye is carried across the surface. Simultaneously, large curved forms (a blue arc, a red wedge, a circular dotted element) impose rotation. The two movements are not reconciled — they coexist in tension, which is the mature Cubist operation.
- **Rule**: In a fully resolved Cubist composition, translation and rotation must be simultaneously present and unreduced — neither subordinates the other.
- **Source**: p.90
- **Quote (DE)**: [No text on this page]
- **Quote (EN)**: [No text on this page]
- **Category**: composition

## Section: Catalogue Text — Works 1924–1933 (pp.95–96)

### Argument Structure
**Thesis**: [INFERRED] Gleizes's works of the 1920s–early 1930s continue to develop the vocabulary of simultaneous movement, but also begin to incorporate lyrical and expressive elements — color used for its own sensation-value, not merely as plane-differentiator.
**Evidence**: Catalogue entries 139–158, with dimensions, media, exhibition history, and critical commentary.
**Conclusion**: [INFERRED] The critical text of the catalogue confirms that Gleizes's teaching practice and theoretical work directly inform the paintings — the paintings are not illustrations of theory but active experiments in it.

### Principles

**PRINCIPLE [P-05]**: das Wesentliche / the essential (law governing form)
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: Gleizes taught his students "to paint the inner principles rather than the appearance of nature." By the early thirties he believed that God was discernible in any aspect of nature — and that painting should access this essential law, not the surface appearance. The reference: "In this work and in the related gouaches (no. 142), the painter reveals an essential identity between flowers and divine love."
- **Rule**: Paint the law governing the form, not its appearance from any single viewpoint — das Wesentliche, not the surface.
- **Source**: p.95 (catalogue entry for Tonus Adoration, no. 140, 1930)
- **Quote (EN)**: "In this work and in the related gouaches (no. 142), the painter reveals an essential identity between flowers and divine love."
- **Category**: theory

**PRINCIPLE [P-06]**: Kontinuität / continuity — temporal extension of sensation
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: The catalogue text for Symphony in Violet (no. 141, 1930-31) notes that Gleizes "wished to infuse his large compositions with many elements drawn from circular movement" and that by the early thirties "he was more and more interested in what he called 'le roulement', dark emphasized to equalize the movement of his forms." The term *le roulement* (rolling, turning) is his late version of Rotation — not a single rotational act but a continuous rolling movement through the form.
- **Rule**: Circular and rotating elements must not be isolated incidents — they must generate a continuous rolling (le roulement) through the entire surface, equalizing the movement.
- **Source**: p.95–96 (catalogue entries 141, 176)
- **Quote (EN)**: "...more and more interested in what he called 'le roulement', dark emphasized to equalize the movement of his forms."
- **Category**: temporality

**PRINCIPLE [P-07]**: plastisches Bewusstsein / plastic consciousness — the demand on the viewer
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: The catalogue notes for the large Composition Accord (no. 139, 1924) describe this work as being exhibited at the Salon des Indépendants 1925 and the Société Anonyme Brooklyn exhibition 1926. The work requires the viewer to follow three simultaneous views of the village of Serrières. This is an explicit statement of what Cubism demands of consciousness: not passive reception of an image but active navigation between simultaneous sensation-states.
- **Rule**: The viewer must be required to navigate actively between sensation-states — the composition must offer multiple entry points, not a single resolved image.
- **Source**: p.95 (catalogue entry 139)
- **Quote (EN)**: "Composition Accord Vue de Serrières, 1924... three simultaneous views of the village."
- **Category**: viewpoint

**PRINCIPLE [P-08]**: Form als Gesetz / form as law — burlap support and material resistance
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: The catalogue text (p.97, entry for Painting for Contemplation series, 1943) notes that Gleizes "found that he could use burlap as a support with pigment mixed with plaster to give it a rough texture. He took the most powerful strokes even while preserving the matte surface he so valued." The material choice is not neutral — the rough, resistant surface demands a different mode of application, one that encodes the painter's force directly into the surface.
- **Rule**: The material support must offer resistance; the painter's force must be visible in the result. A smooth surface effaces the act of sensation; a resistant surface preserves it.
- **Source**: p.97 (catalogue entry 166, Painting for Contemplation)
- **Quote (EN)**: "He found that he could use burlap as a support with pigment mixed with plaster to give it a rough texture. He took the most powerful strokes even while preserving the matte surface he so valued."
- **Category**: practice

**PRINCIPLE [P-09]**: la coupure (Schnitt) / the Cut — vertical division as compositional unit
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: The catalogue entry for the large color plate on p.86 (Gleizes's work from approximately 1915, book fig. 107) notes that this painting is "a variation of the 1920 Woman with Black Glove, no. 122." The vertical division visible in the color plate is explicitly a structural device — the canvas is divided into two simultaneous sensation-states of the same subject, juxtaposed without transition.
- **Rule**: Use a strong vertical or diagonal cut to divide the surface into two co-present sensation-states; do not bridge or dissolve the division.
- **Source**: p.96 (catalogue, referring to fig. 107 / no. 122)
- **Quote (EN)**: "This painting is a variation of the 1920 Woman with Black Glove, no. 122."
- **Category**: composition

**PRINCIPLE [P-10]**: Beziehungsform / relational form — the role of Pascal
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: Multiple catalogue entries (pp.95–97) reference Gleizes's illustrations for the *Pensées de Pascal*. The text states: "Gleizes made his first paintings for 'une-itation' or 'contemplation' in 1932–33 and from this period his admiration for the lyrical and passionate geometry of Islamic art (see 'Arabesques, L'Entretien Arabe' in *Cahiers de l'Art*, special no., August–September, 1935) developed alongside his devotion to Pascal." The relationship between Cubist form and contemplative/devotional content is not incidental — form-as-relationship is for Gleizes ultimately a spiritual category.
- **Rule**: Relational form (Beziehungsform) is not merely compositional — it is the formal structure of consciousness encountering what transcends it. Design it accordingly.
- **Source**: p.98 (catalogue entry 182, Arabesques)
- **Quote (EN)**: "Gleizes made his first paintings for 'une-itation' or 'contemplation' in 1932–33 and from this period his admiration for the lyrical and passionate geometry of Islamic art... developed alongside his devotion to Pascal."
- **Category**: theory

**PRINCIPLE [P-11]**: simultane Ansicht / simultaneous view — the triptych as structural form
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: The catalogue entries for the Study for Left Part of Triptych, Study for Central Panel, and Study for Right Part of Triptych (nos. 144–145, p.95) show that Gleizes used the triptych format systematically to enforce simultaneous viewing. Three panels = three sensation-states of a single subject, held apart by the triptych structure. The Cut between panels is architectural — enforced by the physical separation of the panels.
- **Rule**: When multiple simultaneous sensation-states are the subject, use a multi-panel (triptych or polyptych) format to make the separation structural and undeniable.
- **Source**: p.95 (catalogue entries 144–145, Studies for Triptych 1930)
- **Quote (EN)**: "A. Study for Left Part of Triptych (Étude pour Triptique, Partie Gauche). / B. Study for Central Panel. / C. Study for Right Part of Triptych (Étude pour Triptique, Partie Droite)."
- **Category**: composition

### Exercises & Pedagogy

**EXERCISE [E-01]**: Teaching from inner principle, not outer appearance
- **Task** [CERTAIN]: Gleizes's teaching practice (referenced in catalogue entry for Tonus Adoration): students are directed to paint the inner structural law of a subject rather than its surface appearance. The exercise involves selecting any subject from nature — a flower, a figure, a landscape — and working to identify the formal law that governs its structure, then painting that law directly rather than the surface.
- **Purpose**: Trains access to das Wesentliche (P-05) — the essential law governing form. Separates representation from sensation-theory. The result should be a surface organized by the object's structural logic, not its silhouette.
- **Source**: p.95

**EXERCISE [E-02]**: Mural-scale composition with multiple subjects (Paris 1937 Exposition)
- **Task** [CERTAIN]: For the 1937 Paris Exposition murals, Gleizes and Léger "planned a mural depicting man's dream of space." Gleizes made preliminary studies in which "multiple studies were made, but then Gleizes made a separate canvas of each of his subjects." This is a compositional exercise at architectural scale — decomposing a complex multi-subject scene into separate simultaneous panels, then reintegrating them.
- **Purpose**: Trains P-11 (simultane Ansicht at architectural scale) and P-04 (Übersetzung und Rotation combined across a multi-panel surface). The exercise demonstrates that the sensation-theory applies at any scale.
- **Source**: p.96 (catalogue entry 151, Four Legendary Figures of the Sky)

**EXERCISE [E-03]**: Working with resistant material support
- **Task** [CERTAIN]: Gleizes adopted burlap supports with pigment mixed with plaster for his contemplation paintings (1943 onward). The exercise involves working with a non-smooth, absorbent, resistant surface that records the force of application directly. The painter must adjust mark-making to work with the surface resistance rather than against it.
- **Purpose**: Trains P-08 (Form als Gesetz — material resistance). Forces the painter to make force visible in the result; prevents the surface from becoming a neutral field.
- **Source**: p.97 (catalogue entry 166)

## Section: Catalogue Text — Works 1939–1953 (pp.97–99)

### Argument Structure
**Thesis**: [INFERRED] Gleizes's late work (1939–1953) increasingly integrates religious and contemplative content with the formal system — rotation and translation are no longer purely formal operations but are understood as the geometric description of prayer and devotion.
**Evidence**: Catalogue entries 159–182, covering Meditation series, Contemplation series, Movement with Blue Spots, Arabesques, Composition for Meditation Blanc.
**Conclusion**: [CERTAIN] The critical commentary in the catalogue explicitly states that Gleizes "became increasingly absorbed with religious themes" and that "his compositions contain references to Scripture, sculpture and frescoes (in preparation for his book, *La Forme et l'Histoire*)."

### Principles

**PRINCIPLE [P-12]**: Rotation als Gebet / rotation as devotional movement [INFERRED]
- **Statement** [INFERRED]: The Meditation and Contemplation series (1942–1945) demonstrate that Gleizes understood the rotational movement of sensation as structurally identical to the circular movement of devotional attention — the consciousness moving around a center it cannot grasp directly. The compositions in these series are organized as continuous rotational fields with no translational escape.
- **Rule**: In devotional or contemplative work, rotation is the primary operation — there is no destination, only the continuous movement around.
- **Source**: p.97 (catalogue entries 163–168)
- **Category**: theory

**PRINCIPLE [P-13]**: Le roulement / the rolling — equalization of movement
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: The catalogue text (p.96, entry for The Schoolboy/L'Écolier, third version, 1944, no. 174) states that Gleizes was "more and more interested in what he called 'le roulement'" and used dark emphasis "to equalize the movement of his forms." The roulement is a late development of the rotation principle: not a single rotational gesture but a pervasive rolling quality distributed across the entire surface so that no element is at rest.
- **Rule**: Distribute tonal emphasis (especially dark accents) across the surface to prevent any zone from becoming a rest point — keep all elements in rolling motion.
- **Source**: p.96–98 (multiple catalogue entries referencing le roulement)
- **Quote (EN)**: "...more and more interested in what he called 'le roulement', dark emphasized to equalize the movement of his forms."
- **Category**: composition

### Visual Evidence (continued)

**FIGURE [Fig-16]**: Two b/w plates — late works (book p.99, figs. 140–141 approx.)
- **Shows**: Left: a rounded, biomorphic abstract composition — large circular/organic form in the center, secondary elements orbiting it. Strong rotational reading. Right: a more architectural composition — vertical and diagonal elements, a large circular form in the upper center (possibly a sun or disc), rectangular architectural volumes below, strongly geometric.
- **Demonstrates** [INFERRED]: P-03 (Rotation), P-13 (le roulement). Both late works maintain the rotational primacy of the mature and late style. The architectural right-hand plate suggests the connection Gleizes made between Cubist spatial organization and architectural design — the simultaneous-viewpoint principle applied to a building or monument.
- **Text reference**: No text on this page; caption numbers visible.
- **Source**: p.99

## Notes on Catalogue Structure

The catalogue entries on pp.95–99 are in English (this is an English-language catalogue raisonné or retrospective catalogue, not the 1928 Bauhaus text itself). They provide:

1. **Work number** (sequential, approx. 139–182 in this batch)
2. **Title** (in English, with French in parentheses where applicable)
3. **Date**
4. **Medium and support** (oil on canvas, gouache on board, oil on burlap, etc.)
5. **Dimensions** (in cm, height × width)
