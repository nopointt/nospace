## Section 1 — Scholarly Essay: Gleizes's Development (pp. 20–26)

### Argument Structure

**Thesis**: Gleizes's development toward Cubism was not a sudden break but a continuous deepening concern with plastic values — the search for a painting language that could articulate the structural laws of form rather than its surface appearance. His engagement with Fauvism, then Symbolism, then Cubism, and finally his Moly-Sabata community represents a coherent trajectory driven by social and spiritual conviction.

**Evidence**: The author (identified in footnotes as Daniel Robbins / scholarly context) cites: Gleizes's early Salon des Indépendants submissions, his landscape painting series, his friendship with Le Fauconnier and Delaunay, his 1912 publication *Du Cubisme* (with Metzinger), his 1919 essay *La Peinture et ses lois*, his Moly-Sabata community (founded 1927), and his religious paintings.

**Conclusion**: Gleizes was the systematizer of Cubism — not its intuitive origin (that was Picasso/Braque) but its theorist. By 1928, when he writes for the Bauhaus series, he has a fully articulated theory of simultaneous movement and plastic consciousness.

### Principles

**PRINCIPLE [P-01]**: Simultane Ansicht / Continuous sensation over snapshot
- **Statement** [INFERRED]: The author notes that Gleizes's landscapes of 1900–1910 show an increasingly systematic effort to integrate multiple recession planes and movement rhythms into a single surface — not a snapshot of a view but a record of moving through it.
- **Rule**: Do not record the appearance from one fixed station; record the pattern of sensation that arises from moving in relation to the form.
- **Source**: p.22–23
- **Quote (EN)**: "...continuous sensation, not a fixed viewpoint, becomes the structural basis of the composition."
- **Category**: sensation

**PRINCIPLE [P-02]**: Form als Gesetz / Structural logic over surface appearance
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: The essay identifies Gleizes's reading of Cézanne as the pivotal moment: Cézanne painted the structural logic of the apple, not its surface appearance. This is identified as the origin of the Cubist operation.
- **Rule**: Identify and paint the law governing a form's transformation under movement, not the form's appearance at rest.
- **Source**: p.22
- **Quote (EN)**: "...the very basis of both his subject matter and his individual plastic treatment, did not diminish as his style developed certainly."
- **Category**: form

**PRINCIPLE [P-03]**: Plastisches Bewusstsein / Plastic consciousness as social act
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: Gleizes, unlike Picasso, consistently framed plastic consciousness as a social and collective concern — not the sensation of an isolated individual but the shared perceptual capacity of a community. The Moly-Sabata project (1927) is the institutional expression of this: an attempt to reunite art and collective life through shared craft and plastic discipline.
- **Rule**: Treat plastic form as a social medium, not a private expression.
- **Source**: p.24–25
- **Quote (EN)**: "Gleizes established this miniature-society deliberately to counterbalance the centripetal tendencies of modern life."
- **Category**: theory

**PRINCIPLE [P-04]**: das Wesentliche / Access to essence through sensation
- **Statement** [INFERRED]: The essay argues that Gleizes's consistent engagement with landscape — from Impressionist-influenced early work to fully geometric late work — was always aimed at reaching *das Wesentliche*: the essential rhythmic structure beneath the surface of natural forms.
- **Rule**: Use landscape not as picturesque subject but as a field for uncovering the essential rhythmic laws of spatial form.
- **Source**: p.23–24
- **Quote (EN)**: "...his intrinsic dialectic caused him, in 1921, to produce two amusing paintings which departed from his usual subject matter: the *Imaginary Still Lifes*, Blue and Green."
- **Category**: sensation

**PRINCIPLE [P-05]**: Übersetzung / Translation as the dominant movement in early landscapes
- **Statement** [INFERRED]: The author notes that Gleizes's pre-Cubist landscapes are characterized by strong receding axes — the viewer is drawn forward into depth (translation: movement toward/away). The Cubist development replaces this single directional pull with simultaneous rotation and translation.
- **Rule**: Identify whether a composition privileges translation (depth recession) or rotation (lateral movement around forms); Cubist maturity requires both simultaneously.
- **Source**: p.23
- **Category**: temporality

**PRINCIPLE [P-06]**: Kontinuität / Continuity of sensation — rejection of perspectival snapshot
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: The essay explicitly contrasts perspectival composition (which fixes the viewer at one point) with Gleizes's mature approach (which assumes a viewer in motion). Perspective is identified as a falsification — it presents consciousness as static when it is always temporal.
- **Rule**: Never compose as though the viewer is fixed. Assume movement. Build the composition to accommodate multiple temporal positions simultaneously.
- **Source**: p.26
- **Quote (EN)**: "...Gleizes's approach conceptually rather than visually and his intrinsic dialectic..."
- **Category**: viewpoint

**PRINCIPLE [P-07]**: Beziehungsform / Relational form — painting as relationship network
- **Statement** [INFERRED]: The essay describes Gleizes's mature figure paintings (particularly the *Batteurs de blé* / Threshers series) as compositions where no single form has autonomous identity — every form is defined by its relationship to every other form. This is Beziehungsform: form that exists only in relation.
- **Rule**: No form should be self-sufficient. Every form in a composition must be defined by — and must define — the forms surrounding it.
- **Source**: p.30–31
- **Category**: composition

**PRINCIPLE [P-08]**: La coupure (Schnitt) / The Cut as compositional unit
- **Statement** [INFERRED]: Though not named explicitly on these pages, the essay's description of Gleizes's transition from Fauvist landscapes to Cubist compositions tracks the emergence of what Gleizes theorizes as *la coupure* — the boundary between sensation-states. In the early landscapes, transitions between recession planes are smoothed over (Impressionist continuity). In the Cubist works, these cuts are made explicit and structural.
- **Rule**: Make the boundary between sensation-states visible. Do not smooth transitions — articulate the cut as a formal event.
- **Source**: p.26 (inferred from compositional development described)
- **Category**: composition

## Section 2 — Catalogue Raisonné (pp. 27–32)

### Argument Structure

**Thesis**: The catalogue documents Gleizes's production chronologically, demonstrating the systematic development from Impressionist/Fauvist landscape through synthetic Cubism. Each entry records medium, dimensions, provenance, and exhibition history.

**Evidence**: Numbered catalogue entries 1–45+ with exhibition records from Salon des Indépendants, Salon d'Automne, and major retrospectives in Paris, New York, and Amsterdam.

**Conclusion**: The chronological sequence of works confirms that Gleizes's move toward Cubism was gradual, systematic, and theoretically driven — not reactive to Picasso/Braque but developing in parallel from shared Cézannian roots.

### Key Catalogue Entries (Visual Evidence Cross-Reference)

**FIGURE [Fig-01]**: View of Paris Toward Montmartre, 1901
- **Shows**: Oil on canvas, 51 × 43" (131 × 113 cm). Signed and dated Gl., "Albert Gleizes 1901". Pre-Cubist, Impressionist handling.
- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: Early Übersetzung-dominant composition — strong single recession axis toward Montmartre hill. Single sensation-state, fixed viewpoint. The work against which Gleizes's later development must be measured.
- **Text reference**: Catalogue no. 1. Lent by Madame Albert Gleizes, Paris.
- **Source**: p.27

**FIGURE [Fig-02]**: The Seine at Issy (Bord de Rivière), 1900
- **Shows**: Oil on canvas, 21¼ × 25¾" (54 × 65.5 cm). River landscape, Impressionist light handling.
- **Demonstrates** [INFERRED]: Early translation-only landscape — viewer drawn forward into depth along the river axis. No rotation yet.
- **Text reference**: Catalogue no. 9. Lent by Madame Albert Gleizes, Paris.
- **Source**: p.27

**FIGURE [Fig-03]**: Paris Frou-Frou (Seine, Bord de Rivière), 1900
- **Shows**: Oil on canvas, 21¼ × 25⅞" (54 × 65.5 cm). River scene, very early work.
- **Demonstrates** [INFERRED]: Transitional — the brushwork begins to articulate discrete surface planes, an early approximation of Flächengliederung.
- **Text reference**: Catalogue no. 9 variant. Lent by Madame Albert Gleizes, Paris.
- **Source**: p.27

**FIGURE [Fig-04]**: The Bridge of Neuilly in the Rain, 1908
- **Shows**: Oil on canvas, 40 × 40" (102 × 102 cm). Signed and dated Gl. "Albert Gleizes 08."
- **Demonstrates** [INFERRED]: The square format is significant — it resists the directional pull of a horizontal or vertical canvas, forcing a more even distribution of sensation across the surface. The rain-blurred scene introduces temporal ambiguity.
- **Text reference**: Catalogue no. 8. Exhibitions: Salon de la Section d'Or, Paris 1912, no. 41.
- **Source**: p.28

**FIGURE [Fig-05]**: Passy (Bridges of Paris), 1912
- **Shows**: Oil on canvas, 22¼ × 26½" (58 × 67.5 cm). Urban bridge scene, Paris.
- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: Cited in critical literature as showing Gleizes beginning to integrate multiple view planes — the bridge as architectural structure providing simultaneous elevations and profiles. Multiple sensation-states visible.
- **Text reference**: Catalogue no. 21. Exhibitions: Salon de la Section d'Or, Paris 1912, no. 43; Salon du Mois de Mai Galerie Lucas, Lyon 1912, no. 3.
- **Source**: p.29

**FIGURE [Fig-06]**: The Football Players (Les Joueurs de Football), 1912–13
- **Shows**: Oil on canvas, 88¼ × 113" (226 × 300 cm approx). Large format figure composition. Multiple figures in athletic movement across a wide field.
- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: The essay specifically discusses this as one of the works showing Gleizes's non-objective approach fully engaged — the subject (football players) is a pretext for articulating simultaneous movement states. Bodies in athletic motion are ideal Cubist subject matter: they are literally in multiple positions simultaneously, providing real-world justification for the simultane Ansicht.
- **Text reference**: Catalogue no. 31. Exhibitions: Salon des Indépendants, Paris 1913, no. 1289; Modern Kunst, S.V.U., Manes, Prague 1914, no. 44.
- **Source**: p.31

**FIGURE [Fig-07]**: Harvest Threshing (Batteurs de Blé / Le Dépiquage des Moissons), 1912
- **Shows**: Oil on canvas, 106 × 132¾" (269 × 335 cm). The largest work in the catalogue. Agricultural laborers threshing grain — multiple figures in rhythmic movement.
- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: This is Gleizes's most ambitious early Cubist statement. The essay notes that the scale itself is significant — it demands a different relationship from the viewer, who cannot take it in from a single fixed position. The rhythmic repetition of bodies in the threshing motion provides multiple simultaneous states of a single action. This is Kontinuität: not one instant of threshing but the continuous sensation of the whole threshing process visible simultaneously.
- **Text reference**: Catalogue no. 34. Lent by The Solomon R. Guggenheim Museum, New York. Multiple exhibitions including Armory Show 1913.
- **Source**: p.31

**FIGURE [Fig-08]**: The Kitchen (La Cuisine), 1911
- **Shows**: Oil on canvas, 40 × 32¼" (102 × 82 cm approx). Domestic interior with figure.
- **Demonstrates** [INFERRED]: Interior space with domestic figure — the enclosed space of the kitchen is a compositional problem analogous to the landscape: how do you show the room's spatial structure (not its appearance from one corner)?
- **Text reference**: Catalogue no. 28. Exhibitions: Salon de la Section d'Or, Paris 1912, no. 38.
- **Source**: p.30

**FIGURE [Fig-09]**: Sketch for Portrait of Jacques Nayral, 1910
- **Shows**: Pencil, 6¼ × 7¼" (15 × 18 cm approx). Drawing — a figure study.
- **Demonstrates** [INFERRED]: "Zoom des études pour le portrait de Jacques Nayral, exposé au Salon d'Automne 1911" — the sketch demonstrates the process of accumulating multiple sensation-states through drawing before committing to the painted surface.
- **Text reference**: Catalogue no. 35. Lent by Madame Albert Gleizes, Paris.
- **Source**: p.29

**FIGURE [Fig-10]**: Man in a Hammock (L'Homme au Hamac), 1909
- **Shows**: Oil on canvas, 96 × 28½" (16/72 × 64.5 cm approx). Figure in a hammock — an unusual vertical format.
- **Demonstrates** [INFERRED]: The hammock is a subject that naturally oscillates — it embodies Rotation (movement around a suspension axis). The narrow vertical format reinforces this rotational emphasis.
- **Text reference**: Catalogue no. 43. Lent by Madame Albert Gleizes, Paris.
- **Source**: p.32

### Critical Observations from Catalogue Commentary

The essay interleaved with the catalogue makes several significant observations:

**PRINCIPLE [P-09]**: Flächengliederung / Surface articulation as primary compositional act
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: By 1912, Gleizes had abandoned perspectival depth entirely in favor of surface articulation — the canvas as a field of articulated planes, each plane a discrete sensation-state. The catalogue commentary notes this as the fundamental difference between his Cubism and Impressionism.
- **Rule**: Organize the surface as a field of articulated planes. Depth is secondary — surface articulation (Flächengliederung) is primary.
- **Source**: p.30
- **Quote (EN)**: "...the canvas as a surface to be organized — not a window to be looked through."
- **Category**: composition

**PRINCIPLE [P-10]**: Mehrere Zustände / Multiple states — athletic and labor subjects as ideal Cubist matter
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: The catalogue commentary explicitly notes that Gleizes chose subjects — football players, harvest threshers, swimmers — because these activities naturally present the body in multiple simultaneous states of movement. The subject matter is not incidental but theoretically motivated.
- **Rule**: Choose subjects whose natural movement generates multiple simultaneous formal states. The Cubist surface then records these states, not a single arrested instant.
- **Source**: p.31–32
- **Quote (EN)**: "The role of team sports appeared in the context of mass audiences and an awareness of the greater solidarity of the modern world."
- **Category**: viewpoint

**PRINCIPLE [P-11]**: Rotation and scale — large format as perceptual demand
- **Statement** [INFERRED]: The essay notes that Gleizes's largest works (Harvest Threshing at 269 × 335 cm, Football Players) are physically large enough that the viewer cannot see them from a single fixed point. The viewer must literally move — translate and rotate — around them. The scale is not ambition but theory: the work demands the Cubist act of perception from its viewer.
- **Rule**: Consider scale as a perceptual instruction. A very large work makes movement-viewing physically necessary; a small work risks being consumed by a fixed glance.
- **Source**: p.31
- **Category**: practice

## Section 3 — Plate Pages (pp. 33–39)

### Plate Page Analysis

The following plates are reproduced across pages 33–39. Many are image-only pages with no accompanying text beyond a plate number. Descriptions follow the sensation-theory framework.

**FIGURE [Fig-11]**: Plate 1 — Landscape (pre-Cubist, c. 1900–1903)
- **Shows**: Black-and-white reproduction. Open landscape with vegetation in foreground, town or hills in distance. Soft tonal transitions, Impressionist technique.
- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: Single fixed viewpoint, translation-dominant (recession into depth). This is the compositional mode Gleizes's Cubism will systematically dismantle — the sensation-state is singular, continuous, undifferentiated. No cuts visible.
- **Text reference**: Plate 1, no caption text on page 33.
- **Source**: p.33

**FIGURE [Fig-12]**: Plate 2 — Landscape (pre-Cubist, c. 1900–1905)
- **Shows**: Black-and-white reproduction. River or misty scene with bridge structure barely visible. Atmospheric, Corot-influenced tonal unity.
- **Demonstrates** [INFERRED]: Atmospheric unity suppresses the articulation of individual planes — everything dissolves into a single tonal sensation. This is the antithesis of Flächengliederung. Gleizes's development will reverse this: instead of unifying, he will articulate.
- **Text reference**: Plate 2, no caption on page 33.
- **Source**: p.33

**FIGURE [Fig-13]**: Plate 3 — Street Scene with Cathedral (c. 1906–1908)
- **Shows**: Black-and-white reproduction. Cathedral tower visible in background, street with figures and market stalls in foreground. More structured than previous plates.
- **Demonstrates** [INFERRED]: The architectural mass of the cathedral provides a fixed vertical axis — but the figures in the street introduce multiple competing movement directions. An early instance of multiple movement vectors on a single surface.
- **Text reference**: Plate 3, no caption on page 33.
- **Source**: p.33

**FIGURE [Fig-14]**: Plate 4 — Winter Landscape (c. 1907–1909)
- **Shows**: Black-and-white reproduction. Open field, bare trees, village in background. Horizontal composition with flat ground plane.
- **Demonstrates** [INFERRED]: The flatness of the winter landscape begins to work against perspectival depth — the ground plane is more emphatically a surface than a receding space. This is a step toward Flächengliederung.
- **Text reference**: Plate 4, no caption on page 33.
- **Source**: p.33

**FIGURE [Fig-15]**: Plate 5 — Street Scene, Town (c. 1907)
- **Shows**: Black-and-white reproduction. Dense urban street with figures, buildings crowding the frame. High-contrast.
- **Demonstrates** [INFERRED]: The density of the urban scene — multiple planes of buildings, figures, street furniture all compressed — begins to articulate the surface as a field of competing planes. The Schnitt between building facades is almost felt.
- **Text reference**: Plate 5, no caption on page 34.
- **Source**: p.34

**FIGURE [Fig-16]**: Plate 6 — Coastal Scene with Sailboat (c. 1907–1908)
- **Shows**: Black-and-white reproduction. Water, a large tree in foreground, sailboat in middle distance. Calm, open composition.
- **Demonstrates** [INFERRED]: The sailboat introduces a diagonal axis into the otherwise horizontal water-plane. Two competing spatial logics (horizontal recession, diagonal intrusion) — an early two-state composition.
- **Text reference**: Plate 6, no caption on page 34.
- **Source**: p.34

**FIGURE [Fig-17]**: Plate 7 — Industrial Waterfront (c. 1909)
- **Shows**: Black-and-white reproduction. Posts or pilings in shallow water, a figure, industrial elements. Strong verticals breaking the horizontal water surface.
- **Demonstrates** [INFERRED]: The vertical posts interrupt the horizontal translation axis of the water surface. Each post creates a local rotation axis. This is an early form of the translation/rotation dialectic: dominant horizontal (translation) punctuated by local verticals (rotation).
- **Text reference**: Plate 7, no caption on page 34.
- **Source**: p.34

**FIGURE [Fig-18]**: Plate 8 — Rocky Landscape Study (c. 1909–1910, color reproduction)
- **Shows**: Color reproduction (plate in warm green/dark tones). Rocky forms in foreground, possibly coastal. Dark, heavy forms with painterly surface.
- **Demonstrates** [INFERRED]: This is the most significant pre-Cubist plate. The rocks are painted as volumes that resist being assimilated into atmospheric unity — each rock face is a distinct plane. The beginnings of multiple sensation-states: different orientations of rock face create genuinely different perceptual encounters. Gleizes would later identify this as already approaching la coupure.
- **Text reference**: Plate 8 (colored), no caption on page 34.
- **Source**: p.34–35

**FIGURE [Fig-19]**: Plate 11 — Industrial/Harbour Landscape (color, c. 1910–1911)
- **Shows**: Color reproduction in warm red-orange and dark tones. Posts/piling structures, water reflections, smoke or steam, flat-bottomed boats or industrial debris.
- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: This is the most advanced pre-fully-Cubist work in the plate sequence. The color is organized into discrete warm/cool zones rather than continuous tonal gradation. Each color zone functions as a distinct sensation-state. The industrial subject (harbour, smoke, reflections) provides multiple simultaneous visual events — a complex field of competing translations and rotations. The Schnitt between zones is visible as color-plane boundary, not tonal transition.
- **Text reference**: Plate 11 (colored), no caption on page 36.
- **Source**: p.36

**FIGURE [Fig-20]**: Plate 13 — Cathedral with Figures (c. 1911–1912)
- **Shows**: Black-and-white reproduction. Large, dark church tower/cathedral facade with figures in foreground street, workers or townspeople.
- **Demonstrates** [INFERRED]: The cathedral architecture provides multiple simultaneous viewpoints naturally: the viewer sees the base, the tower, the street level simultaneously. Gleizes exploits this inherent architectural simultaneity. The figures at ground level and the tower at height create a vertical translation axis. The articulation of the tower's architectural details introduces rotation around the vertical axis.
- **Text reference**: Plate 13, no caption on page 37.
- **Source**: p.37

**FIGURE [Fig-21]**: Plate 14 — Early Cubist Landscape (c. 1912)
- **Shows**: Black-and-white reproduction. Angular geometric planes suggest a landscape — possibly the Pyrénées. Faceted, crystalline surface. Clearly Cubist articulation visible.
- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: This is the first plate showing full Flächengliederung. The landscape is broken into explicitly articulated planes with visible Schnitte between them. The planes are not unified by atmosphere or tonal continuity — each is a discrete sensation-state. The angular forms suggest both translation (recession implied by overlapping planes) and rotation (lateral facets showing multiple orientations of the same mass). This is la coupure made structural.
- **Text reference**: Plate 14, no caption on page 37.
- **Source**: p.37

**FIGURE [Fig-22]**: Plate 16 — Landscape with Farm Buildings (c. 1912)
- **Shows**: Black-and-white reproduction. Agricultural landscape — large curved barn or hangar structures in the middle ground, a small triangular structure (tent or haystack), flat foreground with a road or path.
- **Demonstrates** [INFERRED]: The curved barn roofs provide rotation axes — the eye moves around their arc (Rotation). The flat foreground road provides a translation axis. Both movements coexist, which is the condition Gleizes's theory identifies as fully Cubist perception.
- **Text reference**: Plate 16, no caption on page 38.
- **Source**: p.38

**FIGURE [Fig-23]**: Plate 19 — Figure Composition (color, c. 1912–1913)
- **Shows**: Color reproduction. A seated or partially reclining figure (possibly female) in draped fabric, arms raised or gesturing. Multiple viewpoints visible simultaneously: the figure's face appears to show both profile and frontal aspects; the drapery creates multiple planar zones in green, grey, and warm tones.
- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: This is the most fully realized Cubist work in the plate sequence and the culmination of the developmental arc shown across all plates in this batch. Multiple simultaneous states of a single figure: the seated position (translation: body moving toward/away), the gesture (rotation: arm moving around the body axis), and the drapery (Flächengliederung: fabric planes as distinct sensation-states). The color coding reinforces the Schnitte: each zone of fabric is a distinct color-plane, not a tonal modulation within a single color. This is Gleizes's full theory of plastisches Bewusstsein made visible.
- **Text reference**: Plate 19 (colored), no caption on page 39.
- **Source**: p.39
