## III. Der weitere Weg des Materials: Das Volumen (Plastik)
### Beziehung zur Plastik — Die fünf Entwicklungsstadien

---

### Argument Structure

**Thesis**: The encounter with volume (Plastik) is the next necessary step after material. Sculpture is not the goal — volume-comprehension (Volumenerfassung) is. The student who has learned to feel material through the hands now confronts the three-dimensional whole: mass, proportion, hollow, penetration, movement. Five developmental stages define this progression from the raw block to kinetic form.

**Evidence**: Historical and contemporary sculpture across all cultures — Egyptian pyramids, the Kaaba, prehistoric idols, African sculpture, Archipenko, Brancusi, Belling, Schlemmer, Vantongerloo, Servranckx, Picasso, Gutfreund, Barlach — assembled as evidence that all sculptural development worldwide has passed through these five stages, independent of culture, period, or material. Student work from the Bauhaus Preliminary Course (Gerhard Spitzer, Bauhaus 1924, first semester) also cited.

**Conclusion**: The five Stadien are not historical curiosity — they are a pedagogical map. The student must traverse each stage consciously, from the inert block to the sculpted surface to the perforated form to the hovering transparent construction to the kinetic object. Only by traversing this sequence does volume-comprehension become a living capacity rather than a borrowed style.

### Principles

**PRINCIPLE [P-01]**: Volumengestaltung / Volume design
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: Plastik is the best form — the Urform — for the comprehension of volume (Volumenerfassung). Everything else — weight, structure, consistency, color — belongs to the second order; the essential and primary thing for the plastic arts is volume-comprehension (Volumenerfassung). This is also the indispensable foundation for the treatment of kinetic sculpture.
- **Rule**: Approach every sculptural form first as a volume problem — weight, proportion, mass-relationship — before considering surface, material, or content.
- **Source**: p.96 (book) / PDF p.101
- **Quote (DE)**: "die plastik ist die beste form — die urform — für die besitznahme des volumens."
- **Quote (EN)**: "Sculpture is the best form — the primal form — for the taking possession of volume."
- **Category**: volume

**PRINCIPLE [P-02]**: Beziehung zur Malerei — Sculpting vs. Painting
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: While people have slowly come to associate painting with sculpture as a connecting relationship, most people stand before it as foreigners and do not know what it shows them — nor what to expect from it. This position comes from the last decades where "art" was used for decoration — as an embellishing element — so that image and sculpture were experienced as decoration. If this attitude prevailed, when image and sculpture were divorced from life, reduced to exhibition-place and table-picture-hostile, honor-price for sporting achievements — this comes from the fact that our existence-forms of the last years, to which our intellectual and unwilled forms have not yet arrived — when nothing more is necessary than a once-necessary thing — then art as decoration: this is what comes back to us as a block.
- **Rule**: Never treat sculpture as decoration — treat it as a space-organizing, volume-defining act.
- **Source**: p.93 (book) / PDF p.98
- **Category**: volume | pedagogy

**PRINCIPLE [P-03]**: Grundhaltungen bei der Bearbeitung von Materialien / Fundamental attitudes in material handling
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: Giving a single person the opportunity to work with a single material block — e.g. a block of wood — without having any sculptural intentions, without ever having had them — the person will show in all their actions a basic tendency in the way they relate to the material and to the abstracting idea. First the person respects the homogeneity of the block: they go carefully and fully over it from all sides, weigh the heavier, consider its constitution, carefully work it through, find more heavy things, consider its constitution more carefully through, cautiously work through; then they begin — according to their inclination: beschaulich-passiv or experimentativ-aktiv — to work the block with a tool, at first imperceptibly, later with clearer planning.
- **Rule**: Begin any volume exercise by handling the unworked block in its entirety — circling it, weighing it mentally, registering its full mass before removing anything.
- **Source**: p.94 (book) / PDF p.99
- **Quote (DE)**: "zunächst respektiert der mensch die homogenität des blocks. er geht damit über- aus behutsam, fast ehrfurchtsvoll um. er besieht, betastet ihn von allen seiten, wägt seine schwere, schätzt seine beschaffenheit, ausdehnung vorsichtig ab und findet ihn in seinem lebendigen umfang, schon im urzustand ausdrucksvoll."
- **Quote (EN)**: "First the person respects the homogeneity of the block. They handle it with extreme caution, almost with reverence. They look at it, touch it from all sides, weigh its heaviness, assess its constitution, extension carefully — and find it in its living fullness, even in its primal state, already full of expression."
- **Category**: material | tactile | volume

**PRINCIPLE [P-04]**: Aktiver und vegetativer Typ / Active and vegetative type
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: After a short or longer time the person with material increases in astonishing ability: they notice connections between mass and figure, between round and edged, between stumpy and pointed; they slowly learn to better know material and tools; they find methods, find more tools; sink increasingly deeper into the block — always from more positively driven movements. At depth in the hollowed volume often appears the unprecise expression: "hollow spaces" (Hohlräume). This example is distant from the perforation stage, but through a child's play it finds this relationship in the purest form — before the decomposition of plastic by adult concerns.
- **Rule**: Observe whether a student approaches material beschaulich-passiv (contemplative-passive) or experimentativ-aktiv (experimental-active) — both types reach the same formal results by different paths, but must be supported differently in teaching.
- **Source**: p.94–95 (book) / PDF p.99–100
- **Category**: pedagogy | volume

**PRINCIPLE [P-05]**: Plastik ist Volumengestaltung / Sculpture is volume organization
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: So it stands before the person as living and evaluating/examining material: that one of the wesentlichsten expressions of their moments is their volume (Volumen), their mass (Masse), their volume-relations (Volumenrelationen). Plastik is not form, not surface — it is the taking possession of the three-dimensional whole. All else — weight, structure, consistency, colour — belongs to the secondary features; the essential and primary thing for the plastic arts is Volumenerfassung. This is also the indispensable basis for the treatment of kinetic Plastik.
- **Rule**: The sculptor's primary material is not stone, wood, or metal — it is the three-dimensional void that the work occupies and transforms.
- **Source**: p.96 (book) / PDF p.101
- **Category**: volume

**PRINCIPLE [P-06]**: Die fünf Entwicklungsstadien / The five developmental stages
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: From the overwhelming of materials and in the discovery of plastic one can — not only in individual human beings, but in the entire history of plastic — discern developmental stages of plastic by fixing on the clearest manifestations: these developmental stages can be summarized. The five stages are: 1. die blockhafte (the block-like), 2. die modellierte (ausgehöhlte) (the modelled/hollowed), 3. die perforierte (durchlöcherte) (the perforated/pierced), 4. die schwebende (the hovering), 5. die kinetische (bewegliche) (the kinetic/moveable).
- **Rule**: When analyzing any sculpture or three-dimensional form, locate it within the five-stage sequence — this reveals its developmental position and the problems it has and has not yet solved.
- **Source**: p.96 (book) / PDF p.101
- **Quote (DE)**: "die fünf stadien sind: 1. die blockhafte, 2. die modellierte (ausgehöhlte), 3. die perforierte (durchlöcherte), 4. die schwebende, 5. die kinetische (bewegliche) | plastik."
- **Quote (EN)**: "The five stages are: 1. the block-like, 2. the modelled (hollowed), 3. the perforated (pierced), 4. the hovering, 5. the kinetic (moveable) | sculpture."
- **Category**: volume | pedagogy

**PRINCIPLE [P-07]**: Stadium 1 — Der Block / Stage 1 — The Block
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: The material block, whose mass in clear, untouched volume — pyramids (abb. 67), dolmens in Karnak; natural monuments: meteorites; the Kaaba in Mecca (abb. 68); crystalline blocks (abb. 69–71). The block is pure mass assertion without manipulation. It can primitively appear that the origination of such a form could be traced to the fact that it represents the intuitive volume-comprehension because of the oversimplification of the concept in the plastic developmental stages — but one must here still also give up the analysis of its possible other components.
- **Rule**: The pure block — uncarved, unmodelled, unperforated — is not a failure of sculpture but the first and most honest assertion of volume; treat it as a complete statement, not a raw material awaiting transformation.
- **Source**: p.97–98 (book) / PDF p.102–103
- **Category**: volume

**PRINCIPLE [P-08]**: Stadium 2 — Der modellierte Block / Stage 2 — The Modelled Block
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: Small and large masses (volume) relationships become elevated and deepened, round and edged, pointed and blunt. The typical of the second stadium in the plastic development: the block is carefully modelled — not in the sense of a nature-faithful representation, but in the sense of the tools, materials and the abstracting idea. It would be wrong — as still often happens — to point to this work as helpless. It shows here a high intuitive ability, which requires spiritual relations to grasp.
- **Rule**: The modelled block does not imitate nature — it abstracts the essential volume-relationships of round/edged, small/large, pointed/blunt into a three-dimensional statement; judge it by its mastery of these relationships, not by likeness.
- **Source**: p.101 (book) / PDF p.106
- **Quote (DE)**: "der modellierte (ausgehöhlte) block, kleine und große massen (volumen) -beziehungen von erhöht und vertieft, rund und eckig, spitz und stumpf."
- **Quote (EN)**: "The modelled (hollowed) block: small and large mass (volume) relationships of raised and deepened, round and edged, pointed and blunt."
- **Category**: volume

**PRINCIPLE [P-09]**: Negative Volumenwerte / Negative volume values
- **Statement** [INFERRED]: Both of the sculptural works shown (Pevsner's abstract portrait of Duchamp, abb. 81, and Belling's head in mahogany, abb. 82) rest on the effectiveness of negative volume values (negativen Volumenwerte). Within a system everything depends on the interconnectedness of the individual elements — the concavities, hollows, and recessed surfaces are not absence of form but active form-elements equal in weight to the projecting masses.
- **Rule**: Treat the hollow, concave, and recessed areas of any three-dimensional form as positive design decisions carrying equal volumetric weight to the projecting masses.
- **Source**: p.107 (book) / PDF p.112
- **Quote (DE)**: "beide plastiken beruhen auf der wirksamkeit der negativen volumenwerte. innerhalb eines systems hängt alles von der bezogenheit der einzelelemente aufeinander ab."
- **Quote (EN)**: "Both sculptures rest on the effectiveness of the negative volume values. Within a system, everything depends on the interconnectedness of the individual elements."
- **Category**: volume

**PRINCIPLE [P-10]**: Stadium 3 — Der perforierte Block / Stage 3 — The Perforated Block
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: With the mastering of all degrees of the positive and negative volumes: intensive penetration into the material and emerging from it — polar contrasts of hollow and fullness. The fully perforated plastic: a reaching to the limits of the material, a heightening in the penetration of emptiness and fullness (abb. 92–133). One can in most plastic-examples conduct an analysis of the material-treatment, the proportions, the psychic, the compositional etc. but limits itself here — for the time being — to the determination of the respective degree of the volume-comprehension.
- **Rule**: In Stage 3, the form must demonstrate mastery of both solid and void as equal formal elements — the holes are not accidents but necessary to the volume argument.
- **Source**: p.113 (book) / PDF p.118
- **Quote (DE)**: "der perforierte (durchlöcherte) block: mit den beherrschten beziehungen aller grade des positiven und negativen volumens: intensives eindringen in das material und heraus- holen polarer kontraste."
- **Quote (EN)**: "The perforated (pierced) block: with the mastered relationships of all degrees of positive and negative volume: intensive penetration into the material and drawing out of polar contrasts."
- **Category**: volume

**PRINCIPLE [P-11]**: Stadien 4 und 5 — Das vierte und fünfte Stadium / Stages 4 and 5
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: In the kinetic (moveable) "Plastik": here the material is not merely raised up — it is nearly overcome (überwunden). Through these movements, virtual (movement-created) volumes are used to create volumes. To the three dimensions of volume a fourth here comes: movement (Bewegung). What lies beyond complete perforation is a dream we still cannot fully grasp — a daring spiritualization (Vergeistigung) of material, a triumph of the pure-relations.
- **Rule**: Movement is the fourth dimension of volume — a form that moves through space does not occupy a fixed volume but generates a virtual volume through its trajectory; design for kinetic forms must account for this temporal-spatial dimension.
- **Source**: p.115 (book) / PDF p.120
- **Quote (DE)**: "die bewegliche (kinetische) »plastik«: hier wird das material nicht allein aufgelockt; es wird beinahe überwunden. bewegungen, der durch diese bewegungen zu schaffenden virtuellen (volumen-) beziehungen verwendet wird. zu den drei dimensionen des volumens kommt hier als vierte die bewegung hinzu."
- **Quote (EN)**: "The moveable (kinetic) 'sculpture': here the material is not merely loosened up; it is nearly overcome. Movements are used for the virtual (volume-) relationships to be created through these movements. To the three dimensions of volume, movement here comes as a fourth."
- **Category**: volume | movement

**PRINCIPLE [P-12]**: Denkmalkultur / Monument culture — critique
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: Exhibition space and plastic form — the game of political parties. The deep sense of pictorial works: the possible richness of experiential condensation (Erlebniskondensation), their biological and social contexts are remnants of previous periods' true intuitions, though the "art" was viewed entirely externally as the pinnacle of human achievement (not actually considering its technique). Behind it all there is often nothing — a demand for "art" equal to the anecdotal soap-opera needs of the people. This tatbestand provoked rejection of art — whose sharpest words against every art were found in the Futurist Manifesto. In the overthrow it focused itself on the justification of visual art proper, after one recognizes that this attitude against them has been mistaken — that this bearing could not place the existence of the creative itself in question; that neither the old forms of painting nor of plastic were able to be established.
- **Rule**: Reject sculptural form that serves as decoration or political monument — every volume decision must emerge from the structural logic of the work itself, not from cultural or commemorative function.
- **Source**: p.93 (book) / PDF p.98
- **Category**: volume | pedagogy

**PRINCIPLE [P-13]**: Intensität des Ausdrucks / Intensity of expression across all stages
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: At all developmental stages it depends on the intensity of the expression, with which a plastic form has been created. With the recognition of a sculptor up to the fifth stage reaching, one can be — despite all — in works of far lesser intensity as the works of a sculptor of the first stage. Naturally it would be desirable that a modern person with today's means should simultaneously master the geistige and technische — equal to Brancusi's processing method (Paris) being influenced. They show a clear crossing between "handcraft" and "industrial" mentality.
- **Rule**: A work at Stage 5 is not automatically superior to a work at Stage 1 — intensity and formal mastery at any stage outweigh mere developmental advancement; do not mistake complexity for quality.
- **Source**: p.104 (book) / PDF p.109
- **Category**: volume | pedagogy

**PRINCIPLE [P-14]**: Gleichgewichtsproblem / Equilibrium problem
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: What lies beyond complete perforation: the large mass without fixed anchoring (Fixierung) — must balance in itself. The da Vinci sketch "zum grabmal trivulzio's" (abb. 95): an equilibrium problem — the great mass without fixed anchorage (Fixierung) balances in itself as it rears up.
- **Rule**: The hovering or balanced form demonstrates mastery of the equilibrium problem — visual and physical mass-distribution must resolve into apparent weightlessness or poise without mechanical support.
- **Source**: p.115 (book) / PDF p.120
- **Category**: volume | space

### Exercises & Pedagogy

**EXERCISE [E-01]**: Freie Materialbearbeitung (Free material handling)
- **Task** [CERTAIN]: Give a single person a material block — e.g. a block of wood — to work with, without sculptural intentions. The person first respects the homogeneity of the block, examines it from all sides, weighs its heaviness, assesses its constitution. Then, according to inclination (beschaulich-passiv or experimentativ-aktiv), begins to work the block with a tool — at first imperceptibly, later with clearer planning. After shorter or longer time increases in astonishing ability: notices connections between mass and figure, round and edged, stumpy and pointed; learns to better know material and tools; finds methods; sinks increasingly deeper into the block.
- **Purpose**: Trains Volumenerfassung (volume-comprehension) directly through material encounter; reveals the student's fundamental handling type (passive/contemplative vs. active/experimental) and develops tactile-spatial intelligence without the interference of representational ambition. Connects directly to P-03 and P-04.
- **Source**: p.94 (book) / PDF p.99

**EXERCISE [E-02]**: Analyse von Plastiken nach Stadien (Analysis of sculptures by stages)
- **Task** [INFERRED]: Students analyze historical and contemporary sculptures — from collections, books, or originals — and locate each work within the five-stage developmental schema. For each work: identify which stage it occupies; identify which volume-relationships (positive/negative, large/small, round/edged) are active; assess the intensity of the expression independent of the stage.
- **Purpose**: Trains the analytical eye to see volume-structure rather than content or style; demonstrates that the five Stadien are universal across cultures, periods, and materials; develops the vocabulary of Volumenerfassung as an active critical tool. Connects to P-06.
- **Source**: p.96–115 (book) / PDF p.101–120

**EXERCISE [E-03]**: Volumenbeziehungen in plastischen Studien (Volume-relations in sculptural studies)
- **Task** [CERTAIN]: Work a material block through successive stages — from pure block-like mass (Stadium 1) through modelling of raised/deepened, round/edged relations (Stadium 2) into controlled positive-negative perforation (Stadium 3). Document at each stage the volume-relationships achieved before proceeding.
- **Purpose**: Makes the abstract developmental schema physically real; trains students to identify at each step what new formal problems have become available and what problems remain unresolved. Grounds theoretical progression in direct material experience. Connects to P-06, P-07, P-08, P-10.
- **Source**: p.96–113 (book) / PDF p.101–118

- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: "die urelemente der plastischen volumengestaltung" ("the primal elements of plastic volume design"). These are the fundamental volumes from which all sculptural forms derive — the student must master their proportional relationships before proceeding to complex form.

**FIGURE [Fig-04]**: abb. 70 — Constantin Brancusi: Plastik für Blinde, 1926
- **Shows**: Photograph of Brancusi's polished egg/oval form sculpture — a seamlessly curved, highly reflective volume. (nicht anders als architepkos torso [abb. 79] mit der äußerst feinen gewölbten linie der hüften.)" Caption further notes: "Plastik für Blinde" — sculpture for the blind.
- **Demonstrates** [INFERRED]: The bridge between Stadium 1 (block) and Stadium 2 (modelled): the surface is perfectly resolved, reducing the form to a single continuous volume-statement accessible to the Tastsinn (P-03 connection). The designation "für Blinde" is a direct invocation of tactile epistemology — the work's validity lies in its haptic completeness, not its visual appearance.
- **Text reference**: Caption references abb. 79 (Archipenko torso) as comparable treatment of a single continuous curved volume.

The reflective surface shows room reflections, collapsing the boundary between object and environment. The form is entirely abstract, a pure volume-statement of aquatic movement frozen in bronze.
- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: Stadium 1–2 transition, or the resolution of block-thinking into extreme formal economy. The polished surface makes the volume simultaneously assert itself and dissolve into its surroundings — an optisch aktiv surface condition at the service of volumetric clarity.
- **Text reference**: No direct text citation on this page; continuation of the Brancusi discussion begun on abb. 70.

- **Text reference**: "es wäre verfehlt — wie das noch öfters geschieht —, diese plastik als unbeholfene arbeit hinzustellen. im gegenteil, es äußert sich hier eine hohe intuitive begabung, die wesentliche geistige relationen zu erfassen wußte."

**FIGURE [Fig-13]**: abb. 79 — Alexander Archipenko, 1920: Torso

Cross-referenced by Moholy-Nagy with Brancusi's "Plastik für Blinde" (abb. 70) as comparable treatment of a single continuous curved surface: "mit der äußerst feinen gewölbten linie der hüften."
- **Text reference**: Referenced in caption of abb. 70 as a parallel formal achievement.
- **Source**: p.106 (book) / PDF p.111

**FIGURE [Fig-14]**: abb. 80 — A. Archipenko, 1922: Weibliche Figur (Metallrelief)
- **Shows**: Archipenko's metal relief "Weibliche Figur" — a highly abstracted female figure in metal, using punched, raised and recessed areas to create a complex positive-negative surface. The relief condition means the work occupies a zone between surface (Fläche) and volume (Volumen).
- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: The transition from Stadium 2 to Stadium 3 — negative volume values become explicit formal elements. This is the first appearance of deliberately worked-in negative volumes (negative Volumenwerte) as primary form elements, per P-09.
- **Text reference**: "beide plastiken beruhen auf der wirksamkeit der negativen volumenwerte. innerhalb eines systems hängt alles von der bezogenheit der einzelelemente aufeinander ab." (The "beide" refers to abb. 81 and abb. 82, but the argument is established here.)
- **Source**: p.106 (book) / PDF p.111

**FIGURE [Fig-15]**: abb. 81 — Pevsner, 1922: Abstraktes Porträt des Malers Marcel Duchamp
- **Shows**: Pevsner's abstract portrait of Duchamp — a complex relief/construction in which the face is constituted entirely from negative volume values: concave planes, hollows, recessed surfaces. No positive projecting element attempts naturalistic likeness. The composition creates a face-reading through negative form.
- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: Negative Volumenwerte as primary formal language (P-09). "beide plastiken beruhen auf der wirksamkeit der negativen volumenwerte."
- **Text reference**: Directly cited with abb. 82 (Belling) as the two key examples of negative volume value mastery.
- **Source**: p.107 (book) / PDF p.112

**FIGURE [Fig-16]**: abb. 82 — Rudolf Belling, 1923: Kopf aus Mahagoni
- **Shows**: Belling's head in mahogany — an abstracted human head in which the facial features are created through a dialogue of positive projections and negative concavities. The mahogany grain is highly visible and participates in the volume reading.
- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: Negative Volumenwerte working with Factur of mahogany (P-09). The grain-direction of the wood is integrated into the formal argument.
- **Text reference**: Cited alongside Pevsner (abb. 81) as key instance of negative volume values.
- **Source**: p.107 (book) / PDF p.112

**FIGURE [Fig-17]**: abb. 83 — Oskar Schlemmer, 1923: Rundfigur (Raumplastik)
- **Shows**: Schlemmer's "Rundfigur" — a multi-component sculptural arrangement photographed from above against a dark background. The work shows multiple separate, simplified volumetric elements (ovoids, cylinders, curved planes) arranged in spatial relationships. The photograph emphasizes the spatial intervals BETWEEN the elements as much as the elements themselves.
- **Demonstrates** [INFERRED]: The transition from Stage 2 to Stage 3 — the intervals between the components become negative volumes participating in the composition. The overhead view (Neue Sehen) reveals spatial relationships invisible from a conventional frontal viewpoint.
- **Text reference**: No facing text; image-sequence positioning within Stadium 2–3 discussion.
- **Source**: p.108 (book) / PDF p.113

**FIGURE [Fig-18]**: abb. 84 — Oskar Schlemmer, 1923: Relief
- **Shows**: Schlemmer's relief — a flat relief panel showing a simplified, geometrically abstracted human figure in a frontal pose. The figure is constituted from circular, cylindrical, and linear elements. The relief is photographed obliquely (Foto: Lucia Moholy), emphasizing the depth and shadow of the relief elements.
- **Demonstrates** [INFERRED]: Relief as a transition form between Flächengestaltung (surface design) and Volumengestaltung (volume design) — the same compositional logic applies at any depth. The photograph credit "Foto: Lucia Moholy" identifies this as a Bauhaus-context work, photographed by Moholy-Nagy's wife with the Neue Sehen methodology.
- **Text reference**: No direct citation; positioned alongside abb. 85 in Stadium 2–3 sequence.
- **Source**: p.109 (book) / PDF p.114

**FIGURE [Fig-19]**: abb. 85 — Czáky: Komposition, 1924
- **Shows**: Czáky's "Komposition" — an abstract sculptural form combining geometric volumes (sphere, cone, rectangular elements) in a vertical composition. Clearly derived from geometric primaries. Form sits between Stadium 1 (block primaries) and Stadium 2 (modelled relationships).
- **Demonstrates** [INFERRED]: The geometric primary volumes as building blocks for composition — the formal vocabulary derived directly from abb. 69 (stereometrische Figuren).
- **Text reference**: No direct citation on facing page.
- **Source**: p.109 (book) / PDF p.114

**FIGURE [Fig-20]**: abb. 86 — Pablo Picasso, 1914: Das Absintglas
- **Shows**: Picasso's "Das Absintglas" — a cubist sculptural construction of the absinthe glass, combining modelled bronze with a real spoon, creating a dialogue between constructed representation and found object. The fragmented planes suggest the glass simultaneously from multiple viewpoints.
- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: "es ist gleichgültig, was zum vorwand der plastik genommen wird. die entwicklungsstadien der material- bzw. volumenbeherrschung sind überall klar ablesbar. hier die zweite stufe: der block wird modelliert. in diesem vorgang liegt die erkenntnisstufe, nicht in dem darstellungs-wunsch der in der plastischen gestaltung bis jetzt ungewohnten objekte."
- **Text reference**: Directly cited as Stadium 2 example — the Cubist object-content is irrelevant to the formal developmental question; the stage is readable regardless.
- **Source**: p.110 (book) / PDF p.115

**FIGURE [Fig-21]**: abb. 87 — Otto Gutfreund, 1925: Plastik
- **Shows**: Gutfreund's abstract plastic — a roughly modelled, dynamic form with protrusions, hollows, and torn surfaces. The work appears to be in plaster or clay. The handling is energetic, immediate, non-finalized.
- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: Stadium 2 alongside Picasso (abb. 86) — the text notes that regardless of content or medium, the developmental stage is readable. This is positioned to show Stadium 2 in both Cubist construction and direct-modelling approaches simultaneously.
- **Text reference**: Positioned alongside abb. 86 with the direct citation on p.110.
- **Source**: p.110 (book) / PDF p.115

**FIGURE [Fig-22]**: abb. 88 — Georges Vantongerloo, 1924: Konstruktion von Volumenverhältnissen
- **Shows**: Vantongerloo's construction — a carefully articulated abstract sculptural work using only prismatic (rectangular, cubic) elements in layered, stepped configuration. Caption: "Konstruktion von Volumenverhältnissen auf Grund eines um ein Quadrat und eines in ein Quadrat geschriebenen Kreises." White material, studio photography, high clarity.
- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: Stadium 2 at the boundary of Stage 3. The caption text directly connects to the governing-law principle: the work is governed by a geometric rule (a square and a circle inscribed in/around a square) — this is Konstruktion before Komposition. However: "trotz der weitgehenden abstraktion, die durch die verwendung von nur prismatischen figuren erreicht wird, gehört diese plastik in das zweite stadium der plastischen entwicklung, weil sie noch nicht in den zustand der durchlöcherung gelangte."
- **Text reference**: "trotz der weitgehenden abstraktion … gehört diese plastik in das zweite stadium … weil sie noch nicht in den zustand der durchlöcherung gelangte (s. abb. 100)."
- **Source**: p.111 (book) / PDF p.116

**FIGURE [Fig-23]**: abb. 89 — Constantin Brancusi, 1914–23: Plastik (Holz)
- **Shows**: Brancusi wooden sculpture — a vertical, totemic form in wood combining cylindrical and bulbous masses, stacked vertically. The grain of the wood is prominent. The form reads as both abstract and vaguely figural.
- **Demonstrates** [INFERRED]: Stadium 2 working with the Factur of wood — the vertical stacking creates clear volume-relationships of large/small, round/edged within the block. Positioned in the sequence alongside Victor Servranckx (abb. 90).
- **Text reference**: No direct citation on facing page.
- **Source**: p.112 (book) / PDF p.117

**FIGURE [Fig-24]**: abb. 90 — Victor Servranckx, 1921: Plastik
- **Shows**: Servranckx's sculpture — a circular/spherical form with a curved void carved through it. Caption: "Verhältnis des Positiven und Negativen." The sphere has been penetrated by a smooth curved channel, creating a dialogue between the remaining mass and the void.
- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: "Verhältnis des Positiven und Negativen" — this work bridges Stadium 2 and Stadium 3. The penetration is controlled and formal, not accidental. This is the beginning of deliberate positive/negative volume dialogue.
- **Text reference**: Caption: "Verhältnis des Positiven und Negativen."
- **Source**: p.112 (book) / PDF p.117

**FIGURE [Fig-25]**: abb. 91 — Gerhard Spitzer (Bauhaus, 1924, erstes Semester): Durchdringungen, Plastik (Holz)
- **Shows**: Bauhaus student work — Gerhard Spitzer, first semester, 1924. A wooden sculptural construction in which flat, rectangular planes intersect and penetrate one another at angles. The work explores the formal consequences of planar interpenetration (Durchdringungen).
- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: Student work at the Bauhaus directly applying the volume-stage pedagogy. Foto: Eckner/Weimar. The work demonstrates transition from Stage 2 to Stage 3 — the penetrations are the primary formal event.
- **Text reference**: No direct text citation on facing page; positioned as pedagogical illustration of the approach to Stage 3.
- **Source**: p.112 (book) / PDF p.117

**FIGURE [Fig-26]**: abb. 92 — Auguste Rodin: Im Tegefeuer (In the Fires of Purgatory)
- **Shows**: Rodin's work — a group of interlocked figures emerging from and submerging into a mass of roughly modelled material. The deeply worked surface creates extreme positive-negative contrasts of projecting limbs and hollowed-out negative spaces between bodies.
- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: Stadium 3 — der perforierte Block — at high expressive intensity. "man könnte bei den meisten plastik-beispielen eine analyse der materialbehandlung, der proportionen, des psychischen, des kompositorischen usw. durchführen. man begnügt sich aber hier — vorläufig — mit der feststellung des jeweiligen grades der volumenerfassung."
- **Text reference**: Cited at the opening of Stadium 3 discussion as the first example.
- **Source**: p.113 (book) / PDF p.118

**FIGURE [Fig-27]**: abb. 93 — Negerplastik
- **Shows**: African sculptural figure — a standing figure in wood. The work integrates functional elements (the figure appears to be part of a vessel or support). Simple, clearly defined volume masses.
- **Demonstrates** [INFERRED]: Cross-cultural evidence for the universality of the five stages — African sculptural tradition reaches Stadium 2–3 by independent development.
- **Text reference**: No direct text citation; positioned alongside abb. 94 in Stage 3 illustration sequence.
- **Source**: p.114 (book) / PDF p.119

**FIGURE [Fig-28]**: abb. 94 — Negerplastik: Holzgeschnitzte Schüssel aus Hawai
- **Shows**: Hawaiian carved wooden bowl supported by inverted human figures — the figures form the legs of the bowl. The human bodies are hollowed out (their torsos become the hollow legs of the bowl), integrating negative volume with function.
- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: Stage 3 in functional/applied form — the perforation of the body-form is structurally motivated, not decorative. The negative volume (the hollow interior of the bowl) is the functional purpose of the object. Cross-cultural universality of the stage sequence.
- **Text reference**: No direct text citation; image-sequence positioning within Stage 3 illustration.
- **Source**: p.114 (book) / PDF p.119

**FIGURE [Fig-29]**: abb. 95 — Leonardo da Vinci: Skizze zum Grabmal Trivulzio's (Foto: Alexander, Budapest)
- **Shows**: Da Vinci's sketch for the Trivulzio tomb monument — a rearing horse with rider, a sketch showing dynamic movement. The large mass of the horse rears upward, creating an extreme equilibrium challenge.
- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: The equilibrium problem (Gleichgewichtsproblem, P-14) — "ein gleichgewichtsproblem: die große masse ohne fixierung muß in sich ausbalancieren." ("An equilibrium problem: the great mass without fixed anchoring must balance within itself.") This is the opening of the Stage 4–5 discussion.
- **Text reference**: Caption and footnote directly connect this to P-14 and the transition to Stadium 4.
- **Source**: p.115 (book) / PDF p.120

**FIGURE [Fig-30]**: abb. 96 — Giovanni da Bologna: Herkules und Antäus
- **Shows**: Giovanni da Bologna's "Herkules und Antäus" — a bronze sculptural group of two interlocked male figures in violent struggle. Herkules lifts Antäus off the ground; the mass of both bodies creates a complex of interpenetrating volumes balanced at the tipping point.
- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: The two-figure group as a resolution of the equilibrium problem — mass distributed and balanced between two interacting volumes. The raised figure creates a virtual upward movement anticipating Stage 4 (schwebend/hovering).
- **Text reference**: Positioned in the Stage 4–5 opening sequence alongside Da Vinci sketch.
- **Source**: p.116 (book) / PDF p.121

**FIGURE [Fig-31]**: abb. 97 — J. B. Carpeaux: Die vier Weltteile
- **Shows**: Carpeaux's "Die vier Weltteile" — a large bronze sculptural group of four female figures supporting a globe, arranged in a dynamic rotational composition. The figures are active, spiraling masses; the globe is an exact sphere above them.
- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: The transition to kinetic Stage 5 — the figures are not frozen but imply rotational movement. The formal arrangement of the four figures creates a virtual rotational movement around the central sphere. This directly anticipates Stage 5 (kinetische Plastik).
- **Text reference**: Positioned at the close of the Stage 4–5 opening in the narrative sequence leading to kinetic form.
- **Source**: p.116 (book) / PDF p.121
