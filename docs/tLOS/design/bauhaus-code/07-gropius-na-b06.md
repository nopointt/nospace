## Weberei (Weaving Workshop) — continued from b05

### Argument Structure

**Thesis**: The weaving workshop produces objects that apply the Bauhaus grid and color reduction principles to textile form — knotted carpets and wall hangings are not decorative supplements but rigorous spatial compositions governed by the same Gestaltungsgesetze that govern the furniture and metalwork workshops.

**Evidence**: Two large-format textile pieces are shown — a monumental 400×400 cm Knüpfteppich (knotted carpet) by M. Erps (1923) and a Wandbehang (wall hanging) in cotton by B. Otte (1924). Both were made at the Bauhaus Weimar.

**Conclusion**: The grid logic native to weaving — the warp-weft matrix — is not a constraint to work around but the precise structural condition that makes Bauhaus textile design possible. The carpet's composition of intersecting geometric zones demonstrates that reduction does not mean simplicity of form but discipline of organizational principle.

### Principles

**PRINCIPLE [P-01]**: Gestaltungsgesetz im Gewebe / Design law in weaving
- **Statement** [INFERRED]: The warp-weft structure of woven and knotted textiles is itself a grid — the most literal possible realization of the theoretical grid from the Vorkurs. To design in weaving is to design within the grid, not to impose a grid upon material. The Gestaltungsgesetz here is the material's own structural nature made visible.
- **Rule**: Design the textile from its structural logic — warp and weft define the minimum unit; all composition is built from multiples of this unit.
- **Source**: p.91–92
- **Category**: material-honesty | form-economy

**PRINCIPLE [P-02]**: Reduktion in der Textilkomposition / Reduction in textile composition
- **Statement** [INFERRED]: The Erps carpet, despite its large scale (400×400 cm), achieves visual organization not through decorative elaboration but through a restricted palette (grün-braun-rosa-rot-weiß-schwarz) and a rigorous geometric zoning — rectangular fields, checkerboard zones, stripe sequences, curved interlocking bands. Complexity of composition, not ornamental addition.
- **Rule**: Restrict the color palette to demonstrate compositional logic — color variety conceals structural thinking; color discipline reveals it.
- **Source**: p.91
- **Category**: form-economy | material-honesty

**PRINCIPLE [P-03]**: Typus im Wandbehang / Type in the wall hanging
- **Statement** [INFERRED]: The Otte Wandbehang (1924) demonstrates the wall hanging as a typological form — a vertical hanging textile with fringe border, organized in horizontal band sequences. The banded structure is not a random compositional choice but the natural type form for a hanging textile: gravity is a design force, the bands read sequentially from top to bottom as the eye descends.
- **Rule**: Design the wall hanging in accordance with its orientation — gravity and the hanging condition define the compositional axis; work with it, not across it.
- **Source**: p.92
- **Category**: material-honesty | function

## Töpferei (Pottery Workshop)

### Argument Structure

**Thesis**: The pottery workshop demonstrates the most direct path from Handwerk to Industrieform: the wheel-thrown and cast ceramic vessel is already structured by the logic of rotation — the simplest volumetric form, reducible to a profile geometry. The workshop's output moves systematically from individual thrown pieces (gedreht) toward cast multiples (gegossen) and finally to industrial prototypes (Bauhausmodelle) manufactured at external porcelain factories — the complete arc of the Bauhaus methodology.

**Evidence**: Pages 93–110 document the full output of the Töpferei, organized by designer (O. Lindig, Th. Bogler, M. Friedländer, G. Marcks) and technique (gedreht = wheel-thrown, gegossen = slip-cast). The section culminates in pages 109–110 where Bauhaus models are shown as industrially produced at the Staatliche Porzellanmanufaktur Berlin and the Älteste Volkstedter Porzellanfabrik — external manufacturers executing Bauhaus designs.

**Conclusion**: The pottery workshop proves the prototype argument completely: a Bauhaus-trained designer produces a formal Typus; that Typus is then manufactured industrially. The workshop is not the endpoint — it is the prototype laboratory.

### Workshop Structure

**CERTAIN** — p.93 caption block identifies the workshop personnel:
- **Formmeister**: G. Marcks (master of form — the artist/designer role)
- **Technischer Meister**: M. Krehan (master of craft — the production technician role)
- **Leitung der Modellwerkstatt 1924**: O. Lindig und Th. Bogler (leadership of the model workshop from 1924 — the shift toward prototype/model production for industry)

This dual-master structure (Formmeister + Technischer Meister) is the Bauhaus institutional design: artist and craftsman in parallel, neither subordinate to the other. The Modellwerkstatt designation (from 1924) signals the deliberate reorientation toward industrial prototype production.

### Principles

**PRINCIPLE [P-04]**: Drehsymmetrie als Gestaltungsgesetz / Rotational symmetry as design law
- **Statement** [INFERRED]: The wheel-thrown ceramic vessel is produced by rotation — the form is inherently rotationally symmetric. This is not a stylistic choice but the technical condition of the process. The Gestaltungsgesetz of thrown ceramics is therefore: the form must be readable as a profile, because it IS a profile rotated. Design begins with the profile, not with the surface.
- **Rule**: Design the thrown vessel as a profile problem — define the silhouette completely before throwing; the three-dimensional form is the profile rotated.
- **Source**: p.93–98
- **Category**: material-honesty | function | form-economy

**PRINCIPLE [P-05]**: Guss als Serienfertigung-Methode / Casting as serial production method
- **Statement** [CERTAIN/INFERRED]: The repeated notation "gegossen" (cast) throughout the pottery captions is not incidental — it marks the technically significant distinction. Slip-casting (Guss) allows exact reproduction of a form; wheel-throwing does not. The move from gedreht (thrown) to gegossen (cast) is the move from craft prototype to serial production readiness. The Bauhaus pottery workshop explicitly documents both techniques to show the progression.
- **Rule**: Use casting (Guss) to indicate serial production readiness — a design that exists only as a thrown piece remains a craft object; a design developed for casting is a Typus.
- **Source**: p.94, 96, 97, 98, 99, 100, 101, 103, 104, 105, 106, 107, 108, 109, 110
- **Category**: craft-industry | standardization

**PRINCIPLE [P-06]**: Bauhaus-Modell als Industrieform / Bauhaus model as industrial form
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: Page 106 explicitly labels pieces as "Bauhausmodelle; industriell vervielfältigt" — Bauhaus models, industrially reproduced. This is the categorical statement of the workshop's purpose: the model is not the product. The industrially reproduced version is the product. The workshop produces the model.
- **Rule**: The workshop prototype is complete only when it can be handed to an industrial manufacturer for reproduction — the model is evidence of design, not the design's final form.
- **Source**: p.106
- **Quote (DE)**: "Bauhausmodelle; industriell vervielfältigt"
- **Quote (EN)**: "Bauhaus models; industrially reproduced"
- **Category**: craft-industry | standardization

**PRINCIPLE [P-07]**: Externe Fertigung als Validierung / External manufacture as validation
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: Pages 109–110 show pieces manufactured externally: p.109 credits "Ausführung: Staatl. Porzellanmanufaktur, Berlin"; p.110 credits "Ausführung: Älteste Volkstedter Porzellanfabrik." The workshop design, when transferred to an established industrial manufacturer, holds its formal integrity — this is validation. If a design cannot survive the transition to industrial execution, it was not yet a true Typus.
- **Rule**: Test the design by transferring it to external manufacture — if the form survives the industrial process without modification, the design is complete.
- **Source**: p.109, 110
- **Quote (DE)**: "Ausführung: Staatl. Porzellanmanufaktur, Berlin" / "Ausführung: Älteste Volkstedter Porzellanfabrik"
- **Quote (EN)**: "Execution: State Porcelain Manufactory, Berlin" / "Execution: Oldest Volkstedt Porcelain Factory"
- **Category**: craft-industry | standardization

**PRINCIPLE [P-08]**: Typus-System im Küchengeschirr / Type-system in kitchen ware
- **Statement** [INFERRED]: Bogler's kitchen vessel series (p.106) and Lindig's related pieces demonstrate the type-system logic for functional vessels: the lidded storage jar, the coffee pot, the teapot, the cup — these are not individual objects but a family of types defined by functional differentiation. Each type solves one specific functional problem; the system is coherent because all types share a common formal vocabulary (proportion, profile geometry, surface treatment).
- **Rule**: Design functional vessels as a system of types — define the common formal vocabulary first, then differentiate by function; the family relationship must be visible.
- **Source**: p.101, 105, 106, 107, 110
- **Category**: standardization | function

**PRINCIPLE [P-09]**: Reduktion der Keramikform / Reduction of ceramic form
- **Statement** [INFERRED]: The progression from the Lindig Kakaokanne (1923, p.94 — a low, wide-bellied form with spout and handle) through the Großen Deckelvase (p.95 — a tall amphora-like form with twin handles) to the Deckelvase (p.99, 100 — reduced lid-vase without handles) shows a systematic reduction of additive elements. Each piece removes an element that is not structurally necessary to the vessel's function. The lid-vase without handles is the most reduced; it has only what it must have.
- **Rule**: Remove from the vessel form every element that is not required by function — handle only if grip is functionally necessary; lid only if closure is functionally necessary; foot only if structural stability requires it.
- **Source**: p.94–100
- **Category**: form-economy | function

## Cross-Batch Notes

- The component-assembly principle (E-12, Fig-12: 6 Teekannen aus gleichen Formteilen) is the pottery workshop's equivalent of the modular furniture logic seen in Breuer's workshop (b04–b05) — both use standardized components recombined to produce formal variety.
- The dual external manufacturer credits (Staatl. Porzellanmanufaktur Berlin + Älteste Volkstedter Porzellanfabrik) are the book's strongest argument for the industrial prototype thesis — these are not aspirational claims but documented facts of production.
- M. Friedländer appears here as a student/workshop participant whose Becher represent the most radical reduction in the entire book — worth noting in the final synthesis as an example of the student embodying the Gestaltungsgesetz more completely than the masters.
- The Erps Knüpfteppich (p.91) was installed in the Versuchshaus des Bauhauses in Weimar — the textile workshop output was architecturally integrated, not displayed as craft object. This supports the reading of all Bauhaus workshop output as architecturally-scaled, spatially-functional production.
