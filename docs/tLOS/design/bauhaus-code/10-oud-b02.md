## ÜBER DIE ZUKÜNFTIGE BAUKUNST (continued from b01) — pp. 19–21

### Argument Structure (conclusion)
**Thesis**: Color (Farbe) is the final and most neglected factor in the renewal of architecture. In current building practice it is treated with wretched indifference (jämmerliche Gleichgültigkeit).
**Evidence**: In Holland, with its dominant brick construction, color is always suppressed. Brick's color varies with weathering — beginning as a harmony, it becomes a disharmony within weeks. Light-colored brick is even worse: it jumps to the eye more sharply. The development of plaster (Putz) and smooth surface finishes opens entirely new possibilities for color in architecture.
**Conclusion**: A rationally grounded architecture will be the sharpest possible contrast to previous architecture — sachlich (objective/matter-of-fact), surrendered completely to the goal of creating technically perfectly formed organisms of clear form and pure proportion. In place of the naturalness of unprocessed material, the rawness of glass, the weathering of the wall, the schmelzen of glaze — the clarity of the blinking, the gleaming, the glittering of steel will appear.

### Principles

**PRINCIPLE [P-18]**: Farbe als letzter Faktor / Color as the final factor
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: Color is named explicitly as the last important direct factor for the renewal of architecture. In current practice it is treated with wretched indifference (jämmerliche Gleichgültigkeit).
- **Rule**: Treat color as a primary architectural element, not a finishing decision. The color of the wall surface is as architecturally consequential as its proportion or material.
- **Source**: p. 20
- **Quote (DE)**: "Als letzter wichtiger Faktor direkter Art für die Erneuerung der Architektur ist zum Schluß noch die Farbe zu nennen. Das Farbelement ist in der heutigen Baukunst in der Regel einer jämmerlichen Gleichgültigkeit ausgeliefert."
- **Quote (EN)**: "As the final important direct factor for the renewal of architecture, color must be mentioned last. The color element in today's architecture is as a rule delivered over to wretched indifference."
- **Category**: surface

**PRINCIPLE [P-19]**: Mauermaterial bestimmt Farbstimmung / Wall material determines color atmosphere
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: At almost every contemporary building, masonry material dominates over color through its material presence. The malerkünstlerische Akzent (painterly accent) of a building is already decided by the color of its wall material. If the total color character of the wall material dominates, then the color composition of the whole building is directed toward nuance, toward mood — not toward positive color statement.
- **Rule**: Do not rely on masonry material color as the color of the building. The material's color suppresses positive color articulation. Use surface finishes (Putz, plaster) to enable genuine color composition.
- **Source**: p. 20
- **Quote (DE)**: "An nahezu jedem gegenwärtigen Bauwerk überwiegt das Mauermaterial durch seine Masse auch durch Farbe. Für den malerkünstlerischen Akzent eines Bauwerks ist darum — bei gleichgewichtiger Farbgestaltung — die Wahl des Mauermaterials schon sofort entscheidend."
- **Quote (EN)**: "In almost every contemporary building, the wall material dominates through its mass, and also through its color. For the painterly accent of a building, therefore — in balanced color composition — the choice of wall material is immediately decisive."
- **Category**: surface

**PRINCIPLE [P-20]**: Backstein als Farbhindernis / Brick as color obstacle
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: In Holland, with its predominant brick construction, this is always the case. The malerkünstlerische Wert (painterly value) of brick lies not in its color as such — not in what can be named as its natural color — but in the tone of its color, in the nuance. Pure, bright, luminous color remains without effect against such a background; it is either overshadowed or suppressed by the prevailing grayness.
- **Rule**: Do not attempt primary color application on brick surfaces. The material's tonal complexity defeats pure color. Use smooth surfaces (plaster, rendered finish) for genuine Farbe als Raumstruktur.
- **Source**: p. 20–21
- **Quote (DE)**: "Der malerkünstlerische Wert des Backsteines liegt — gleich wie der von den meisten nach handwerklicher Technik produzierten Materialien — nicht in seiner Farbe als solche, welche als Gesamtheit braun-grau zu nennen ist, sondern in der Tönung seiner Farbe, in der Nuance. Reine, leuchtende Farbe bleibt einem derartigen Hintergrunde gegenüber ohne Wirkung."
- **Quote (EN)**: "The painterly value of brick lies — like that of most materials produced by craft technique — not in its color as such, which as a whole may be called brown-gray, but in the tone of its color, in the nuance. Pure, luminous color remains without effect against such a background."
- **Category**: surface

**PRINCIPLE [P-21]**: Putz als Chance / Plaster as opportunity
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: The inventions appearing in shorter and shorter intervals for smooth and brightly colored treatment of plaster and rendered surfaces open such significant perspectives for the development of color in architecture that they can completely transform the overall appearance of architecture in combination with the new formal possibilities.
- **Rule**: Smooth rendered surface finishes (Putz) enable genuine color architecture. Prioritize these over exposed masonry when color is architecturally intended.
- **Source**: p. 21
- **Quote (DE)**: "Besonders aber die in immer kürzeren Zwischenpausen auftauchenden Erfindungen zur glatten und hellfarbigen Bearbeitung von Putz- und Betonoberflächen eröffnen so bedeutende Aussichten für die Entwicklung der Farbe in der Baukunst, daß sie in Zusammenwirkung mit den dargelegten neuen Formmöglichkeiten den Gesamtaspekt der Architektur vollständig verändern können."
- **Quote (EN)**: "But especially the inventions appearing in ever shorter intervals for smooth and bright-colored treatment of plaster and concrete surfaces open such significant prospects for the development of color in architecture that, in combination with the new formal possibilities outlined, they can completely transform the overall appearance of architecture."
- **Category**: surface

**PRINCIPLE [P-22]**: Sachlichkeit als Ziel / Objectivity as goal
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: The tendency of architectural development points toward a Baukunst that is more bound to material (Stoff) than before — bound in appearance to surface — free from all impressionistic mood-making, developed in fullness of light to a purity of proportion, blank of color (Blankheit der Farbe), and organic clarity of form.
- **Rule**: Achieve sachlich clarity — surface-bound, light-full, pure in proportion, blank in color. Not mood, not expression: clarity.
- **Source**: p. 22
- **Quote (DE)**: "So weist die Tendenz der architektonischen Entwicklung auf eine Baukunst, welche im Wesen mehr als früher an das Stoffliche gebunden, in der Erscheinung darüber hinaus sein wird; welche sich, frei von aller impressionistischen Stimmungsgestaltung, in der Fülle des Lichtes entwickelt zu einer Reinheit der Verhältnisse, einer Blankheit der Farbe und einer organischen Klarheit der Form, welche durch das Fehlen jedes Nebensächlichen die klassische Reinheit wird übertreten können."
- **Quote (EN)**: "Thus the tendency of architectural development points toward an architecture that in its essence will be more bound to material than before, but in appearance will surpass it; which, free from all impressionistic mood-making, develops in the fullness of light toward a purity of proportions, a blankness of color, and an organic clarity of form, which through the absence of everything secondary will be able to surpass classical purity."
- **Category**: form

## DIE ENTWICKLUNG DER MODERNEN BAUKUNST IN HOLLAND: VERGANGENHEIT, GEGENWART, ZUKUNFT — pp. 22 ff.

> Section title page: bold black typography on white — "DIE ENTWICKLUNG / DER MODERNEN / BAUKUNST IN HOLLAND / VERGANGENHEIT GEGENWART ZUKUNFT"

### Argument Structure
**Thesis**: The modern Baukunst of Holland can only be understood against the trajectory of Modernität itself — defined not as timely fashion but as "das Wechselnde" (the changing), the individually becoming as distinguished from the collectively becoming (das Neue).
**Evidence**: The trajectory runs: Cuijpers (Stil-Architektur, historicism, ornamental compilation) → Berlage (rationalism, structural honesty, but still entangled in the Stil problem) → the generation after Berlage (expressionism of the Amsterdam School vs. the rationalist line leading to De Stijl).
**Conclusion**: Berlage's work opened a perspective of endless developmental possibilities for Dutch architecture — even if his own work never fully resolved the Stil problem. The developmental line continues past him.

### Principles

**PRINCIPLE [P-23]**: Modernität als Wechsel / Modernity as change
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: "Das Moderne" is defined as "das Wechselnde" (the changing) in the sense of "das Sich-Entwickelnde" (the self-developing). Modernity does not need to be at the same time "das Neue" (the new). Modernity is the individually-becoming; the New is the collectively-becoming — a distinction of fundamental importance.
- **Rule**: Do not confuse the fashionably modern with the genuinely developmental. The developmental line is what matters; individual novelty is secondary.
- **Source**: p. 24
- **Quote (DE)**: "Es läßt sich das 'Moderne' definieren als das 'Wechselnde' im Sinne des 'Werdenden', d.h. des 'Sich-Entwickelnden'. Demnach braucht das 'Moderne' nicht immer zu gleicher Zeit auch das 'Neue' zu sein. Das 'Moderne' ist das Individuell-Werdende, das 'Neue' ist das Kollektiv-Werdende, ein Unter-"
- **Quote (EN)**: "One can define 'the Modern' as 'the changing' in the sense of 'the becoming', i.e. of 'the self-developing'. Accordingly, 'the Modern' does not always need to be at the same time 'the New'. 'The Modern' is the individually-becoming, 'the New' is the collectively-becoming — a distinction—"
- **Category**: form

**PRINCIPLE [P-24]**: Destruktion und Aufbau / Destruction and construction
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: In Dutch architecture of the previous century, this process of destruction (Zerstörung) and construction (Aufbau) began without which no primitivity of creative work can arrive — essentially with Berlage.
- **Rule**: Creative development requires both destruction of inherited form and construction of new form. A purely conservative architecture cannot develop; a purely destructive one cannot build.
- **Source**: p. 33
- **Quote (DE)**: "In der niederländischen Architektur des vorigen Jahrhunderts begann dieser Prozeß von Zerstörung und Aufbau, ohne welchen also eine Primitività des künstlerischen Schaffens nie nahe zu kommen ist, erst wesentlich nach Cuijpers."
- **Quote (EN)**: "In Dutch architecture of the previous century, this process of destruction and construction, without which one can never approach a primitivity of creative work, began only essentially after Cuijpers."
- **Category**: form

**PRINCIPLE [P-25]**: Berlage — Rationalismus als Haltung / Berlage — rationalism as posture
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: Berlage's rationalism was truer to the concept in a far broader sense than Cuijpers' had been. It related to all life-manifestations that stood in any direct or indirect connection with the building art. His rationalism — one could say — was at bottom a rational Gesinnung (disposition/attitude).
- **Rule**: Rationalism in architecture is not merely a formal language — it is a fundamental orientation of the architect's attitude toward every element, every decision, every component.
- **Source**: p. 36
- **Quote (DE)**: "Der Rationalismus Berlages hingegen war dem Begriffe mehr in der Tat gemäß, weshalb er ein viel breiteres Gebiet umspannte. Er bezog sich nämlich auf alle Lebenserscheinungen, welche in irgendeinem direkten oder indirekten Zusammenhang zur Baukunst stehen; der Rationalismus — könnte man sagen — wurde bei ihm zur rationellen Gesinnung."
- **Quote (EN)**: "Berlage's rationalism, however, was truer to the concept and therefore encompassed a far broader field. It related to all life-manifestations standing in any direct or indirect connection with building art; his rationalism — one could say — became a rational disposition."
- **Category**: construction

**PRINCIPLE [P-26]**: Stil-Problem als Sackgasse / The Style problem as dead end
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: The Stil-Architektur (style architecture) of Cuijpers represents the apex of academic formal compilation — the point where the accumulation of historical form-vocabularies is most "accomplished" and simultaneously most artistically bankrupt. It is the Gipfelpunkt (peak) of academic formal compilation, showing how the dead end of architectural development works.
- **Rule**: Do not attempt to solve architectural problems by assembling historical style elements. This is form-compilation, not architecture. The problem to be solved is compositional, not stylistic.
- **Source**: pp. 34–35
- **Quote (DE)**: "Im letztgenannten Entwurfe scheint sich tatsächlich der Gipfel, damit aber auch der Wendepunkt einer ganzen Epoche zu verkörpern. Ein Höhepunkt akademischer Formkompilation, zeigt er zu gleicher Zeit trefflich die Sacksasse baukünstlerischer Entwicklungsmöglichkeiten."
- **Quote (EN)**: "In the last-named design, the peak, and also the turning point, of an entire epoch seems to actually embody itself. A high point of academic formal compilation, it simultaneously demonstrates perfectly the dead end of architectural developmental possibilities."
- **Category**: form

**PRINCIPLE [P-27]**: Technik als Ansporn, nicht Vorbild / Technology as spur, not model
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: Technology has achieved new forms (automobiles, electrical and sanitary articles, medical instruments, etc.) because it surrendered to the practical demands of life without ulterior thoughts. It follows from this that there is no equivalence between Baukunst and Technik — technology cannot serve as a model for architecture, only as a spur and illumination. Because architecture, unlike technology, must also consider organic formal relations in its appearance.
- **Rule**: Do not copy the formal language of machines or industrial objects into architecture. Technology demonstrates that function-driven form can achieve new beauty — use this as inspiration, not as direct model.
- **Source**: p. 37
- **Quote (DE)**: "Es folgt daraus nicht eine Gleichstellung von Baukunst und Technik, wie eine gewisse Art mechanischer Ästhetik dieses gern tut. Das ist u.a. schon deshalb nicht möglich, weil die Baukunst selbst in ihrer organischen Form noch optische Beziehungen auf Grund der Situation zu berücksichtigen hat, die Technik hingegen funktionell ohne weiteres gestalten kann... Zwar darf sie darin der Baukunst nicht zum Vorbild dienen, wohl aber kann sie ihr Ansporn und Belehrung sein."
- **Quote (EN)**: "From this there does not follow an equation of architecture with technology, as a certain kind of mechanical aesthetics likes to do. This is, among other reasons, not possible because architecture itself in its organic form must still consider optical relations on the basis of situation, while technology can simply form functionally... Technology may certainly not serve as a model for architecture, but it can well be its spur and illumination."
- **Category**: construction

### Visual Evidence

**FIGURE [Fig-02]**: P.J.H. Cuijpers, Reichsmuseum Amsterdam, 1876 (Abb. 1, p. 24)
- **Shows**: Photograph of the Rijksmuseum — elaborate historicist facade combining Gothic towers, Renaissance dormers, arched central entrance, ornate brick detailing. Highly complex silhouette.
- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: The Stil-Architektur Oud criticizes — exhaustive compilation of historical formal elements without compositional unity. Referenced in text as point of departure (pp. 26–28).
- **Text reference**: "besonders da an das Reichsmuseum (Abb. 1) und an den Hauptbahnhof (Abb. 2) in Amsterdam zu denken."
- **Source**: p. 24

**FIGURE [Fig-03]**: P.J.H. Cuijpers, Hauptbahnhof Amsterdam, 1880 (Abb. 2, p. 25)
- **Shows**: Photograph of Amsterdam Central Station — wide symmetrical facade with twin towers, brick construction, elaborate applied ornament across all surfaces, Gothic and Renaissance vocabulary mixed.
- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: Further example of Cuijpers' Stil-Architektur; Oud uses it alongside the Rijksmuseum as evidence of the Stil dead end.
- **Text reference**: Same reference as Abb. 1 above.
- **Source**: p. 25

**FIGURE [Fig-04]**: H.P. Berlage, Erster Entwurf für die Börse in Amsterdam, 1897 (Abb. 3, p. 28)
- **Shows**: Drawing/rendering of first design for the Amsterdam Stock Exchange — still contains historicist tower, Gothic tracery, elaborate ornamental composition. Comparable in approach to Cuijpers.
- **Demonstrates** [INFERRED]: The starting point of Berlage's development — before his rationalist breakthrough. Shows the Stil-Architektur entanglement from which he would free himself.
- **Text reference**: Referenced in discussion of Berlage's Stil problem (p. 28).
- **Source**: p. 28

**FIGURE [Fig-05]**: H.P. Berlage, Zweiter Entwurf für die Börse in Amsterdam, 1897 (Abb. 4, p. 29)
- **Shows**: Drawing of second Börse design — tower retained but simplified; ornament reduced; masonry surface begins to appear more continuous; less complex silhouette.
- **Demonstrates** [INFERRED]: Transition in Berlage's development toward rationalism — ornamental compilation giving way to surface continuity.
- **Text reference**: Referenced in Berlage developmental sequence discussion.
- **Source**: p. 29

**FIGURE [Fig-06]**: H.P. Berlage, Dritter Entwurf für die Börse in Amsterdam, 1897 (Abb. 5, p. 30)
- **Shows**: Drawing of third Börse design — further simplification; tower becomes more austere; masonry surface more unified; ornament largely suppressed.
- **Demonstrates** [INFERRED]: Berlage's progressive rationalization — each iteration strips more ornament. The developmental line made visible in sequential designs.
- **Text reference**: Sequential with Abb. 3 and 4.
- **Source**: p. 30

**FIGURE [Fig-07]**: H.P. Berlage, Börse in Amsterdam, 1898 (Abb. 6, p. 31)
- **Shows**: Photograph of built Amsterdam Stock Exchange — large horizontal brick building with prominent square clock tower, round-arched windows, continuous brick masonry surface. Ornament minimal; surface reading is dominant. Street-level perspective shows human scale against the long horizontal wall.
- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: Berlage's achieved rationalism — the brick wall as continuous Fläche, the window as arch-framed opening in mass. The corner tower marks the civic importance without historical ornament. Oud cites this as the key Berlage achievement.
- **Text reference**: Discussed as the major work of Dutch rationalism (pp. 33–34).
- **Source**: p. 31

**FIGURE [Fig-08]**: H.P. Berlage, Entwurf für ein Mausoleum, 1889 (Abb. 7, p. 34)
- **Shows**: Drawing of mausoleum project — massively eclectic, combining Romanesque/Byzantine dome, Gothic pinnacles, Renaissance colonnades, elaborate spatial composition. The most historicist of all Berlage's shown designs.
- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: The Gipfelpunkt (peak) of academic formal compilation. Oud identifies this explicitly as both the apex and the dead end of the Stil-Architektur direction.
- **Text reference**: "Im letztgenannten Entwurfe scheint sich tatsächlich der Gipfel, damit aber auch der Wendepunkt einer ganzen Epoche zu verkörpern."
- **Source**: p. 34

**FIGURE [Fig-09]**: H.P. Berlage, Entwurf für ein "Beethovenhuis" in Bloemendaal, 1908 (Abb. 8, p. 37)
- **Shows**: Drawing of Beethoven House — centralized domed building, massive cylindrical form, very simplified exterior. Roman/Byzantine influence but drastically reduced ornament. Heavy and monumental.
- **Demonstrates** [INFERRED]: Late Berlage — the rationalist tendency carried to monumental form. Simplified mass but still bound to historical typology (the centralized dome). Demonstrates Berlage's limitation: rational disposition but not yet free of historical form references.
- **Text reference**: Referenced in context of Berlage's continued Stil problem (p. 37–38).
- **Source**: p. 37

**FIGURE [Fig-10]**: H.P. Berlage, Kontorgebäude in London, 1914 (Abb. 9, p. 38)
- **Shows**: Photograph of London office building — regular grid of large windows in a continuous facade; brick piers between windows; highly repetitive bay rhythm; minimal ornament; strong horizontal reading. Street-level view through an arched frame (possibly camera framing device).
- **Demonstrates** [INFERRED]: Berlage's late rationalism approaching a more purely compositional approach — repetitive window grid as Masse und Öffnung rhythm, horizontal extension, suppressed ornament. Closest Berlage comes to the new architecture Oud advocates.
- **Text reference**: Referenced as late Berlage example in context of his development.
- **Source**: p. 38
