## DIE ENTWICKLUNG DER MODERNEN BAUKUNST IN HOLLAND (continued) — pp. 59–76

> This batch covers the critical transition: from critique of the Amsterdam School and the two currents of the new movement, through Oud's own Wohnbau projects (Tusschendiepen, Hoek van Holland), to the international context (Le Corbusier, Gropius, Lönberg-Holm), the conclusion of the main essay at "Wende 1922–1923", and the NACHWORT (1929).

### Argument Structure
**Thesis**: The second current of the new Dutch architectural movement — the one that leads toward genuine organic architecture — finds its clearest expression in works that embody ornament-free composition, sachlich material use, and the principle of form generated from inner necessity. This current connects with international developments (Le Corbusier, Gropius, the new movement in Germany) and represents the genuine new style. The Dutch achievement, evaluated without chauvinism, is significant.
**Evidence**: The sequence of images from Rietveld (jewelry shop, doctor's consulting room, Utrecht house), to Klaarhamer (Utrecht apartment design), to Oud's own Spangen and Tusschendiepen blocks, to the Hoek van Holland Arbeiterwohnhäuser, to Le Corbusier and Gropius, demonstrates the range of the international movement.
**Conclusion**: The developmental line leads from Cuijpers through Berlage, through the Amsterdam School (as a necessary destructive detour), to the rationalist current, to the international style. The Dutch architectural situation (Wende 1922–1923) represents a genuine turning point. The Nachwort (1929) acknowledges that the tendency became an "Internationale Architektur" of worldwide spread — but warns against premature celebration.

### Principles

**PRINCIPLE [P-35]**: Ornament als Natur-Objekt / Ornament as nature-object
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: Ornament is for architecture what the nature-object is for painting — an inappropriate means and a secondary matter (Nebensächlichkeit). Just as the nature-object on a building is not an organ of the overall organism but merely an organism-for-itself beside the whole, next to the composition and not in it — so ornament has only eccentric relations to the whole, standing in no necessary connection with it.
- **Rule**: Remove ornament from architecture as the painter must remove the nature-object from his composition. Both are external additions that prevent the work from becoming an integrated formal organism.
- **Source**: p. 64
- **Quote (DE)**: "Das Ornament ist für die Baukunst, kann man vergleichsweise sagen, was das Natur-Objekt ist für die Malerei, d.h. ungeeignetes Mittel und Nebensächlichkeit. Das Ornament am Gebäude ist — wie das Natur-Objekt im Staffeleibilde — nicht nur Organ des Gesamtorganismus, des Kunstwerkes, sondern zu gleicher Zeit Organismus an und für sich."
- **Quote (EN)**: "Ornament is for architecture, one can say by comparison, what the nature-object is for painting — an inappropriate means and a secondary matter. Ornament on a building is — like the nature-object in the easel picture — not only an organ of the overall organism, of the artwork, but simultaneously an organism in and for itself."
- **Category**: form

**PRINCIPLE [P-36]**: Realität als Ausgangspunkt / Reality as starting point
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: The fundamental principle is that reality forms the starting point of aesthetic creation (ästhetischen Schaffens). Every fixing of any formal schema from the outset — for example the cubic formal vocabulary in which the new Dutch architecture threatens to harden into a new formal dogma — is therefore to be rejected, because it does not correspond to the inner (cleansing) tendencies of function.
- **Rule**: Begin from reality — from function, material, construction — not from a predetermined formal schema. Even "new" forms can become a new Stil-Architektur if applied as external formula.
- **Source**: p. 71
- **Quote (DE)**: "Fundamental dabei bleibt, daß die Realität nicht entspricht — und ich denke dabei jetzt auch an die vorher genannte kubische Formgebung, in welcher nun wieder die holländische Baukunst in einem neuen Formdogma zu erstarren droht, setzt auf dessen inneren (reinigenden) Tendenzen also eine Funktion nicht entsprechen, eine Formauflösung ist schon deshalb verwerflich, weil die unumgänglichen Notwendigkeiten des Seins gewalttätig unterdrückt und den Zwiespalt inneren und äußeren Lebens vergrößert."
- **Quote (EN)**: "Fundamental is that reality does not correspond — and I am thinking here also of the aforementioned cubic form-giving, in which Dutch architecture now threatens to harden again into a new formal dogma — a formal dissolution that does not correspond to the inner (cleansing) tendencies of function is already therefore to be rejected, because it violently suppresses the unavoidable necessities of being and enlarges the split between inner and outer life."
- **Category**: construction

**PRINCIPLE [P-37]**: Maschine als Möglichkeit / Machine as possibility
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: In the machine we have possibilities for a strictness and precision of form, a firmness (Straffheit) and precision (Genauigkeit) of form, such as we have never known before. In chemistry we have today means and methods for enameling (Emaillieren) and glazing (Glasieren) of unheard-of kind; in iron construction methods for bridging and for lifting gravity (Aufhebung der Schwere); in glass technology methods for floating (zum Fliegen) and spreading (Ausbreiten); in concrete construction for seamlessness and projecting (Auskragen). All of these are far from exhausting their use — which always again fails through the sentimentality of a self-satisfied, outdated artistic worldview.
- **Rule**: Exploit the full technical possibilities of modern materials and processes: enameling, glazing, cantilevering, seamless concrete surfaces. These are not yet fully used in architecture.
- **Source**: p. 71
- **Quote (DE)**: "In der Maschine besitzen wir Möglichkeiten zu einer Straffheit und Genauigkeit der Form, wie sie bisher unerhört waren. In der Chemie besitzen wir heute Mittel und Verfahren ungeahnter Art zum Emaillieren und zum Glasieren; in der Eisenkonstruktion solche zur Überbrückung und zur Aufhebung der Schwere; in der Glastechnik solche zum Fliegen und zum Ausbreiten; im Betonbau solche zur Nahtlosigkeit und zum Auskragen, usw."
- **Quote (EN)**: "In the machine we possess possibilities for a strictness and precision of form such as have never been known before. In chemistry we today have means and methods of unheard-of kind for enameling and glazing; in iron construction those for bridging and for lifting gravity; in glass technology those for floating and spreading; in concrete construction those for seamlessness and cantilevering, etc."
- **Category**: material

**PRINCIPLE [P-38]**: Wille zum Stil / The will toward style
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: The will toward Style (Wille zum Stil) always loses itself again in the love of art (Liebe zur Kunst). The collective becoming — the New, das Neue — that emerges from the convergence of destroying and building forces represents the genuinely new, while the individually motivated (das Moderne) remains individualistic artwork. Only when individual will aligns with collective tendency does genuine style emerge.
- **Rule**: Do not impose a personal formal will as style. Align individual creative work with the collective developmental tendency. The will toward style must dissolve into love of the thing itself.
- **Source**: p. 76
- **Quote (DE)**: "Der Wille zum Stil verliert sich immer wieder in der Liebe zur Kunst."
- **Quote (EN)**: "The will toward style always loses itself again in the love of art."
- **Category**: form

**PRINCIPLE [P-39]**: Internationale Architektur als Wende / International architecture as turning point
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: The tendency toward "Internationale Architektur" — already latent in 1922–1923 — spread worldwide with unexpected speed: from Japan to Denmark, from Russia to America. Holland included.
- **Rule**: The new architecture is not nationally specific — it is an international tendency rooted in the same material and technical conditions worldwide. Do not interpret it as a local Dutch or European phenomenon.
- **Source**: p. 78
- **Quote (DE)**: "Die Tendenz zu einer 'Internationalen Architektur', welche — wie angedeutet — in Holland schon früh anwesend war, verbreitete sich mit unerwarteter Schnelligkeit über die ganze Welt: von Japan bis Dänemark, von Rußland bis Amerika. Holland nicht währenddessen ein."
- **Quote (EN)**: "The tendency toward an 'International Architecture', which — as indicated — was already present early in Holland, spread with unexpected speed across the whole world: from Japan to Denmark, from Russia to America. Holland included."
- **Category**: urbanism

**PRINCIPLE [P-40]**: Romantiker im Blute / Romantic in the blood
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: The Dutch architect is — despite all legend — a Romantic in the blood. How it would succeed in him, one can scarcely prophesy today; but also the healthy reason could grow over his head again. In other words: the risk of Romanticism remains a permanent temptation for Dutch architecture — even within the rationalist movement.
- **Rule**: Be alert to the Romantic temptation — even in seemingly rational architecture. The tendency to individual expressiveness runs deep in Dutch artistic culture and must be actively resisted.
- **Source**: p. 78–79
- **Quote (DE)**: "— trotz aller Legende! — der Romantiker im Blute. Wie es ihm gelingen würde, läßt sich heute noch kaum prophezieren, doch auch das gesunde Raison könnte ihm wieder über den Kopf wachsen."
- **Quote (EN)**: "— despite all legend! — the Romantic in the blood. How it would succeed in him, one can scarcely prophesy today; yet also healthy reason could again grow over his head."
- **Category**: form

### Visual Evidence

**FIGURE [Fig-26]**: G. Rietveld, Juweliersladen Kalverstraat in Amsterdam, 1922 (Abb. 25, p. 59)
- **Shows**: Photograph of jewelry shop facade — narrow storefront with large plate glass display window in the center, clean rectilinear frame, dark frame against light interior, minimal lettering. Completely ornament-free; the display case itself is the entire facade composition.
- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: Rietveld's sachlich commercial design — the plate glass opening as primary architectural element. The Masse und Öffnung principle at shop scale: the surround frame as mass, the glazed opening as void. No ornamental supplement.
- **Text reference**: Listed among Abb. 25–29 as examples of the "wesentlich" current (genuinely organic).
- **Source**: p. 59

**FIGURE [Fig-27]**: G. Rietveld, Innenraum, Juweliersladen Kalverstraat in Amsterdam, 1922 (Abb. 26, p. 60)
- **Shows**: Interior photograph — glass display cases in orthogonal arrangement, structural elements clearly expressed as separate pieces (horizontal planes, vertical supports as distinct elements), ceiling lamp as geometric object. De Stijl spatial organization: planes and lines defining space without enclosing it.
- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: Rietveld's De Stijl interior — color as spatial structure (the different planes carry different values), the corner problem resolved by allowing planes to float past each other, structural elements as distinct formal objects rather than continuous walls. The first fully realized De Stijl interior in the book.
- **Text reference**: Part of Rietveld sequence Abb. 25–29.
- **Source**: p. 60

**FIGURE [Fig-28]**: G. Rietveld, Sprechzimmer eines Arztes in Maarssen, 1922 (Abb. 27, p. 61)
- **Shows**: Interior photograph of doctor's consulting room — white/light walls with a large dark circle on the upper wall (possibly a blackboard or decorative element), orthogonal furniture (desk, chair with visible frame structure), open shelving system on left. Strong primary color accents implied. De Stijl furniture arrangement.
- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: De Stijl principles in interior space — the circular element on the wall is the sole "irrational" element and immediately stands out precisely because everything else is orthogonal. Color as spatial instruction: the dark circle makes the wall recede at that point. The Rietveld chair shows structural honesty (each member distinct).
- **Text reference**: Part of Rietveld sequence.
- **Source**: p. 61

**FIGURE [Fig-29]**: G. Rietveld, Wohnhaus in Utrecht, 1924 (Abb. 28, p. 62)
- **Shows**: Exterior photograph of the Schröder House, Utrecht — pure De Stijl composition: white/light rendered surfaces, strong horizontal and vertical plane articulation, balconies as flat horizontal slabs, windows as rectilinear openings, corner resolved by overlapping planes, colored accent elements (black window frames, primary color panels implied). Flat roof. No ornament of any kind.
- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: The most fully realized De Stijl building in the book — the Eckproblem resolved by dissolution: the corner is treated as void, planes appear to float past each other. Color differentiates planes structurally (warm planes advance, cool recede). The building embodies Farbe als Raumstruktur in three dimensions. Oud identifies this as the leading example of the "wesentlich" current.
- **Text reference**: "In dem Strom verdienstvollen Bemühungen und der Bewegung anschließt, lehrreich (Abb. 20–21–25–26–27–28–29 mehr wesentlich)."
- **Source**: p. 62

**FIGURE [Fig-30]**: G. Rietveld, Innenraum, Wohnhaus in Utrecht, 1924 (Abb. 29, p. 63)
- **Shows**: Interior photograph (rotated) of the Schröder House — open-plan interior space with sliding partitions, structural columns as isolated elements, large glass surfaces admitting full daylight, horizontal planes of floor/ceiling extending to the exterior through glass, orthogonal furniture, color-differentiated surfaces.
- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: De Stijl interior at full scale — the floor plan as an open field defined by planes rather than enclosed by walls. The window as large glazed opening that equalizes interior and exterior. Standardized components (windows, partitions) in a non-standardized spatial configuration.
- **Text reference**: Part of Rietveld sequence.
- **Source**: p. 63

**FIGURE [Fig-31]**: P.J.C. Klaarhamer, Entwurf für Etagenhäuser in Utrecht, 1919 (Abb. 30, p. 65)
- **Shows**: Drawing of apartment building design — large-scale mixed-use block, regular window grid in large bays, horizontal banding at floor levels, projecting balconies creating strong horizontal shadow lines, minimal ornament, corner treatment with slight setback. Highly regular facade grid.
- **Demonstrates** [INFERRED]: Dutch rationalist housing in the Wright-influenced current — large horizontal window bands, regular grid, projection and recession as the primary compositional tools. Compare to Oud's Tusschendiepen which achieves a purer surface.
- **Text reference**: Listed among Abb. 30–36 as examples of the current Oud is tracing.
- **Source**: p. 65

**FIGURE [Fig-32]**: J.J.P. Oud, Etagenhäuser "Spangen" in Rotterdam, Pieter Langendijkstraat, 1919 (Abb. 31, p. 66)
- **Shows**: Photograph of Spangen housing — long brick housing block, corner view; the corner building has a distinctly more elaborate treatment with slightly projecting upper story and rounded corner element. Long horizontal run of regular windows in the street facade, but still with brick articulation at vertical piers. Ground level commercial units.
- **Demonstrates** [INFERRED]: Oud's own early housing — still partially Amsterdam School influenced (the corner treatment) but moving toward rationalist surface. The street facade already shows the horizontal window-band-in-brick approach that he later simplifies. The corner here is the problem Oud is working to solve.
- **Text reference**: Referenced in Oud's own developmental sequence.
- **Source**: p. 66

**FIGURE [Fig-33]**: J.J.P. Oud, Etagenhäuser "Tusschendiepen" in Rotterdam, Gijsingstraat, 1920 (Abb. 32, p. 67)
- **Shows**: Photograph (rotated) of Tusschendiepen housing — very long horizontal facade with strong horizontal emphasis; regular window openings in continuous rows; rendered (plastered) white surfaces with dark window frames; horizontal band at each floor level; flat roof. Extremely pure surface compared to Spangen.
- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: A major step in Oud's development — the rendered surface now fully replaces brick as the primary Fläche. The horizontal window rhythm creates the Masse und Öffnung logic at housing scale: the wall as continuous white plane interrupted by regular dark openings. Color (white surface) is now a spatial statement, not just material.
- **Text reference**: Referenced as key development point in Oud's progression.
- **Source**: p. 67

**FIGURE [Fig-34]**: J.J.P. Oud, Etagenhäuser "Tusschendiepen" in Rotterdam, Taandersstraat, 1920 (Abb. 33, p. 68)
- **Shows**: Photograph (rotated) showing a different view of Tusschendiepen — the underside of the continuous balcony slab visible overhead, the street-level facade with regular window openings below. The cantilevered balcony slab creates a deep horizontal shadow line dividing the facade horizontally.
- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: The cantilever (Auskragen) as architectural principle — Eisenbeton enables the projecting floor plane that creates spatial depth without physical ornament. The shadow cast by the projecting slab structures the facade through light and shadow, not through applied decoration.
- **Source**: p. 68

**FIGURE [Fig-35]**: J.J.P. Oud, Hof Etagenhäuser "Tusschendiepen" in Rotterdam, 1920 (Abb. 34, p. 69)
- **Shows**: Photograph (rotated) of the Tusschendiepen courtyard — view along the inner court facade showing both flanking walls in perspective; strong triangular shadow elements (possibly protective awnings) at regular intervals creating a rhythmic pattern; the court space framed by the continuous housing facades.
- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: The housing court as spatial enclosure defined by the repetition of identical facades. The triangular shadow elements create a syncopated rhythm against the regular window grid. Human scale established by the court width relative to the facade height.
- **Source**: p. 69

**FIGURE [Fig-36]**: Van der Vlug und Wiebenga (Abb. 35, p. 70)
- **Shows**: Photograph (small, landscape) — a low industrial or institutional building with very large horizontal window bands, minimal facade articulation, flat roof, white rendered surface. Strong horizontal emphasis. [Caption partially illegible: "Van der Vlug und Wiebenga" + date 1922 + location unclear]
- **Demonstrates** [INFERRED]: A further example of the rationalist current — large-scale glazing as primary facade element, construction directly expressed. The extreme of Masse und Öffnung: the glass band dominates over the wall band.
- **Source**: p. 70

**FIGURE [Fig-37]**: J.J.P. Oud, Arbeiterwohnhäuser in Hoek van Holland (am Meer), 1924 (Abb. 36, p. 72)
- **Shows**: Photograph of workers' housing at Hoek van Holland — long, extremely horizontal white-rendered facade; flat roof with narrow continuous band; regular window openings in rows; curved corner treatment at the building's end (a cylindrical corner volume); ground-floor commercial strip with horizontal window band. Strikingly pure and modern in appearance.
- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: The Hoek van Holland project is the primary example of Oud's fully achieved De Stijl Wohnbau. Key observations: (1) the rendered white surface enables Blankheit der Farbe; (2) the horizontal window bands read as continuous openings in the wall mass; (3) the curved corner — notable — is a soft resolution of the Eckproblem: the corner is rounded rather than broken or dissolved. This is Oud's own corner solution: not the De Stijl planar dissolution of Rietveld, but a cylindrical transition that maintains surface continuity. (4) The ground-floor commercial strip creates a datum line that separates the housing above from the street below — a standardization strategy.
- **Text reference**: "Erzielen einer organischen Baukunst, sich verkörpernd in einer der Ornamentlosigkeit und dem Fortschritte der Technik sowie der Lebenspraxis entsprechenden einfachen, klaren und straffen Formgebung zum ersten Male wieder ein gediegenes Fundament zu der Erlangung einer allgemeinen Schönheit, eines Stiles, zu ergeben. (Abb. 37–39)"
- **Source**: p. 72

**FIGURE [Fig-38]**: Le Corbusier-Saugnier, Landhaus in Vaucresson, 1923 (Abb. 37, p. 73)
- **Shows**: Photograph of Villa Besnus, Vaucresson — white rendered exterior, flat roof, simple cubic volume, large window openings, garden context. Strikingly similar in vocabulary to Oud's Hoek van Holland — white surface, horizontal emphasis, orthogonal geometry, ornament-free.
- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: Oud places Le Corbusier in the same developmental line as his own work — both represent the international expression of the same organic rationalist principle. The similarity between Oud's rendered surfaces and Le Corbusier's purism is not coincidence but convergence from the same formal logic.
- **Text reference**: Abb. 37–39 grouped together as examples of the international movement achieving "ein gediegenes Fundament... einer allgemeinen Schönheit, eines Stiles."
- **Source**: p. 73

**FIGURE [Fig-39]**: W. Gropius mit A. Meyer, Theater in Jena, 1922 (Abb. 38, p. 74)
- **Shows**: Photograph of the Jena theatre addition — white rendered cubic volumes, flat roofs at different levels, corner windows at roof level, strong horizontal banding of window strip, pure orthogonal composition. No ornament.
- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: Gropius placed alongside Le Corbusier and Oud as evidence of the international convergence. The Jena theatre uses the same formal vocabulary — rendered surface, cubic massing, horizontal window bands — reached from the German side of the Bauhaus/Dessau context.
- **Text reference**: Part of Abb. 37–39 sequence.
- **Source**: p. 74

**FIGURE [Fig-40]**: K. Lönberg-Holm, Entwurf für das Hochhaus der "Chicago Tribune" in Chicago, 1922 (Abb. 39, p. 75)
- **Shows**: Drawing of a Chicago Tribune tower competition entry — extremely vertical tower, pure glass curtain wall from top to bottom, regular grid of floor plates and structural columns, minimal setbacks, flat top. The most radically transparent and structural of all tall-building proposals.
- **Demonstrates** [INFERRED]: The extreme end of the rationalist direction applied to the skyscraper typology — pure Masse und Öffnung where glass dominates, structure is the ornament, no applied decoration whatsoever. Oud includes this as the outer limit of the formal direction he is advocating.
- **Text reference**: Abb. 37–39 sequence extended to include this example.
- **Source**: p. 75

### NACHWORT (Postscript, 1929) — pp. 77–79

**Argument Structure**
**Thesis**: Since the main essay was written, Dutch architecture has lost its vitality as a whole — absorbed into the "Internationale Architektur" that has spread worldwide, it settled into self-satisfied complacency and declined to a "Niveau" that only day-trippers can enjoy.
**Evidence**: The "Internationale Architektur" spread from Japan to Denmark to Russia to America. Holland included. But in Holland it degenerated because architects embraced technology one-sidedly and its aesthetic aspect completely neglected — imitating the healthy substance rather than creating from it.
**Conclusion**: The international striving now offers — already free from a greater degree of presupposed aestheticism than before — more security for steady further development. But the Dutch architect remains a Romantic in the blood. Hope for the best.
