## Bauhausgebäude — Interior Spaces: Workshop Wing, Technical School, Communal Facilities, Dormitory

### Argument Structure
**Thesis**: The interior of the Dessau Bauhaus building is not decoration applied to structure — it is the continuation of the same logic that governs the exterior. Light, material, and spatial organization are chosen to support the specific work performed in each room. The building does not merely house the Bauhaus; it is the Bauhaus made physical.

**Evidence**: Photographs of the workshop wing at night (Abb. 49), iron windows in the workshop (Abb. 50), iron windows in the swimming bath and Speisesaal (Abb. 51–52), corridor and staircase of the technical school (Abb. 53–54), entrance vestibule (Abb. 55), Aula (Abb. 56), teacher's room (Abb. 57), director's office (Abb. 58), weaving workshop (Abb. 59), metal workshop (Abb. 60–63), carpentry workshop (Abb. 64), wall-painting workshop (Abb. 65), Vorlehre drawing room (Abb. 66), architecture drawing room (Abb. 67), canteen food-counter and Speisesaal (Abb. 68–69), dormitory tea kitchen (Abb. 70).

**Conclusion**: Every room in the building is designed from the inside out — the program determines the section, the section determines the facade, the facade expresses the structure. Interior furnishings and color are executed by Bauhaus workshops, closing the loop between pedagogy and built environment.

## Section 1 — Night View: The Structural Frame Revealed by Light

### Principles

**PRINCIPLE [P-01]**: Konstruktives Gerüst / Structural Frame as Night Composition
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: At night, artificial interior light transforms the glass curtain wall into a luminous display of the building's structural skeleton. The concrete frame, normally read as the boundary between glass and solid, becomes the figure against the glowing ground of the workshop windows. The building communicates its construction logic most clearly when darkness removes all material ambiguity.
- **Rule**: Design the structural frame so it remains legible in all lighting conditions — the constructive logic must be readable by day through opacity contrasts and by night through interior illumination.

## Section 2 — Workshop Wing: Iron Windows and the Curtain Wall System

### Argument Structure
**Thesis**: The iron window system of the workshop wing is not merely a technical choice — it is the material embodiment of the Vorhangfassade principle. The column (Pfeiler) stands behind the glass skin, not in front of it; the window frames are iron because iron spans further and profiles thinner than wood; the cantilevered slabs and heating radiators along the glass wall are the mechanical consequence of this structural decision.

**Evidence**: Abb. 50 shows the iron window of a workshop space from inside, revealing the column behind the glass skin, the cantilevered ceilings, and the radiators running along the glass wall. Abb. 51–52 show iron windows of the Aula/Speisesaal and the swimming bath (Badeanstalt), with details of hinged/tilting casements (gekuppelte Fensterdrehflügel, Kippflügel).

**Conclusion**: The iron window system is a system — not a series of individual windows but a continuous glazed wall with integrated thermal management (radiators at the glass plane), ventilation (tilting/coupled casements), and structural expression (column behind glass, not in front).

### Principles

**PRINCIPLE [P-02]**: Vorhangfassade / Curtain Wall — Column Behind the Glass
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: The structural column (Pfeiler) stands behind the glass outer skin. The glass is hung from the concrete frame, not supported by masonry between the columns. This is the essential inversion that defines the Vorhangfassade: the structural element and the enclosing element are separated, each performing its own function without compromise.
- **Rule**: Place the structural column behind the glass plane — never allow the column to interrupt the glazing. The structural grid and the enclosing skin are two independent systems.
- **Source**: p.61 (abb. 50)
- **Quote (DE)**: "pfeiler hinter der glasaußenhaut"
- **Quote (EN)**: "column behind the glass outer skin"
- **Category**: transparency

**PRINCIPLE [P-03]**: Dachentwässerung / Roof Drainage at Interior Columns
- **Statement** [INFERRED]: In the workshop wing, roof drainage (Dachentwässerung) runs down the interior columns rather than through the external wall. This is a direct consequence of the Vorhangfassade system: since the glass skin is not load-bearing and cannot receive drainage downpipes, drainage is internalized and routed through the structural elements.
- **Rule**: When the facade is a non-load-bearing glass curtain, internalize the drainage through the structural frame. The exterior skin remains uninterrupted.
- **Source**: p.69 (abb. 59, caption)
- **Quote (DE)**: "dachentwässerung an den inneren pfeilern"
- **Quote (EN)**: "roof drainage at the interior columns"
- **Category**: function

**PRINCIPLE [P-04]**: Eisernes Fenster / Iron Window — Thin Profile, Maximum Light
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: Iron window frames are chosen over wood because iron sections are thinner for the same structural span, maximizing the glazed area and the light admitted to the workshops. The lesser column spacing (geringere Pfeilerstützweiten) and cantilevered slabs (ausgekragte Decken) are architectural consequences of choosing iron over wood.
- **Rule**: Choose window frame material based on its structural performance relative to section depth — the thinner the frame relative to its span, the more light enters the workspace.
- **Source**: p.61 (abb. 50)
- **Quote (DE)**: "eisernes fenster eines werkstatttraumes. pfeiler hinter der glasaußenhaut, daher geringere pfeilerstützweiten und ausgekragte decken. radiatoren längs der glaswand. gekuppelte fensterdrehflügel"
- **Quote (EN)**: "iron window of a workshop space. Column behind the glass outer skin, therefore lesser column spacings and cantilevered slabs. Radiators along the glass wall. Coupled rotating window casements."
- **Category**: material

**PRINCIPLE [P-05]**: Radiator an der Glaswand / Thermal Compensation at the Glass Plane
- **Statement** [INFERRED]: Heating radiators are placed along the glass wall (längs der Glaswand), not against the interior solid walls. This placement compensates for the thermal loss through the large glass surfaces and creates a warm air curtain rising along the cold glass plane — a functional response to the thermal consequence of the curtain wall.
- **Rule**: When glazing is continuous and floor-to-ceiling, place heating elements at the glass plane, not at the solid walls. The thermal strategy must match the enclosure strategy.
- **Source**: p.61 (abb. 50)
- **Category**: function

**PRINCIPLE [P-06]**: Kippflügel / Tilting Casements — Ventilation Without Draught
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: The iron windows of the Aula and Speisesaal operate with tilting casements (Kippflügel) — hinged at the top, opening inward at the bottom — that allow ventilation without direct draught at occupant level. One wheel mechanism operates three to four coupled casements simultaneously (gleichzeitig drei bis vier fenster mit je vier gekuppelten kippflügeln).
- **Rule**: Design ventilation into the window system itself — individual casements operated by single mechanisms, allowing controlled air exchange without disrupting work or comfort. Ventilation is not residual; it is designed.
- **Source**: p.62 (abb. 51)
- **Quote (DE)**: "das rad rechts unten bedient gleichzeitig drei bis vier fenster mit je vier gekuppelten kippflügeln"
- **Quote (EN)**: "the wheel at lower right operates simultaneously three to four windows each with four coupled tilting casements"
- **Category**: function

- **Source**: p.62

## Section 3 — Technical School (Technische Lehranstalten): Corridor, Staircase, Spatial Economy

### Argument Structure
**Thesis**: The corridor and staircase of the technical school wing demonstrate the principle of ökonomische Lösung (economical solution) — two-sided daylit corridor with acoustic glazing above the lockers (Reißbrettschränke) and cross-ventilation through opposite classrooms. The staircase is material: white and black terrazzo on steps and balustrade coping.

**Evidence**: Abb. 53 shows the double-loaded corridor of the technical school with staircase at left and view toward windows at far end. Abb. 54 shows the main staircase (Haupttreppenhaus) with terrazzo steps.

**Conclusion**: The section of the corridor is an acoustic and lighting problem solved simultaneously: the clerestory glazing above the lockers (doppelt verglas te Oberlichter) brings daylight into both sides of the corridor while providing acoustic insulation from the classrooms. Cross-ventilation (Querlüftung) comes from the windows of the classrooms on both sides.

### Principles

**PRINCIPLE [P-07]**: Ökonomische Lösung des Schulflurs / Economical School Corridor Section
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: A two-sided (zweiseitig) school corridor is the most efficient spatial organization. Light reaches the corridor through acoustically double-glazed clerestory windows (doppelt verglaste Oberlichter) above the lockers/drawing-board cabinets that line the corridor walls. Cross-ventilation (Querlüftung) is provided through the windows of the opposite classrooms. The staircase window at the end provides the borrowed view and terminates the axis.
- **Rule**: In a double-loaded corridor school, bring light to the corridor through high-level clerestory glazing above built-in furniture, and ventilate through borrowed air from the flanking rooms. No corridor space is wasted or dark.
- **Source**: p.63 (abb. 53)
- **Quote (DE)**: "ökonomische lösung eines zweiseitig bebauten schulflurs. belichtung der längsflurwände durch gegen schallwirkung doppelt verglaste oberlichter über den reißbrettschränken. querlüftung durch die gegenüberliegenden klassen und durch die fenster des treppenhauses."
- **Quote (EN)**: "Economical solution of a double-sided school corridor. Lighting of the longitudinal corridor walls through acoustically double-glazed clerestory windows above the drawing-board cabinets. Cross-ventilation through the opposing classrooms and through the windows of the staircase."
- **Category**: function

**PRINCIPLE [P-08]**: Terrazzo / Material Specification for Circulation Surfaces
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: Steps and balustrade coping in the main staircase are surfaced with white and black terrazzo plates (weiße und schwarze Terrazzo-Platten). Terrazzo is chosen for its durability in high-traffic circulation space and for its clarity of tone — white treads, dark risers or borders — which makes the stair legible as a sequence of horizontal planes.
- **Rule**: Specify terrazzo for heavy-use circulation surfaces — steps, balustrade cappings — in black/white combinations that reinforce the spatial reading of ascending planes.
- **Source**: p.64 (abb. 54)
- **Quote (DE)**: "stufen und brüstungsauflagen aus weißen und schwarzen terrazzo-platten"
- **Quote (EN)**: "steps and balustrade coping of white and black terrazzo plates"
- **Category**: material

### Visual Evidence

**FIGURE [Fig-05]**: Bauhausgebäude — Corridor and staircase, technical school (abb. 53)
- **Shows**: Interior photograph looking along the double-loaded corridor of the technical school. On the left, a straight-run staircase rises with clean white plaster walls. The corridor stretches to the right toward glazed windows at the far end. The ceiling is plain concrete. The floor is light-toned and continuous. No ornament. The spatial sequence — stair, corridor, window — is entirely composed of structural and functional elements.
- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: The ökonomische Lösung of the school corridor (P-07). The termination of the corridor axis with a window is a deliberate compositional decision — the view outward is the reward of the corridor.
- **Text reference**: The text block on this page provides the full description of the section logic (quote above).
- **Source**: p.63

**FIGURE [Fig-06]**: Bauhausgebäude — Main staircase, Haupttreppenhaus (abb. 54)
- **Shows**: Photograph of the main staircase from below looking up. Steps are wide, generous, with white/black terrazzo surfaces. The balustrade is a solid panel with a flat terrazzo coping. The staircase composition is purely geometric: horizontal planes of the steps, diagonal plane of the underside of the stair, vertical planes of the walls. No railing spindles — the balustrade is a plane, not a series of elements.
- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: Material specification (P-08) and the treatment of the staircase as a composed spatial element — each step is a horizontal plane, the underside of the flight is a sloping ceiling. Circulation as designed space (Zirkulationsraum).

## Section 4 — Entrance Vestibule: Workshop-Produced Fittings

### Argument Structure
**Thesis**: The entrance vestibule of the Bauhausgebäude is furnished and lit by products made in the Bauhaus workshops. The building's interior is not merely designed in the Bauhaus style — it is literally equipped with objects produced in the Bauhaus's own workshops, making the building a demonstration of the school's productive capacity.

**Evidence**: Abb. 55 shows the entrance vestibule with three doors to the Aula (stage/hall), overhead lighting infrastructure visible, and dark-colored furniture/fittings along the wall.

### Principles

**PRINCIPLE [P-09]**: Workshop-Integration in der Ausstattung / Workshop Integration in Fittings
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: The lighting bodies (Beleuchtungskörper) and color design (farbige Gestaltung) of the entrance vestibule were executed by Moholy-Nagy with the Bauhaus workshops (mit den Werkstätten des Bauhauses). The building is the workshop's showroom — every fitting is a demonstration of the school's capacity to produce functional objects.
- **Rule**: Equip the building with objects produced in its own workshops. The relationship between the school and its building is circular: the school produces objects for the building; the building demonstrates what the school can produce.
- **Source**: p.65 (abb. 55)
- **Quote (DE)**: "beleuchtungskörper und farbige gestaltung: moholy-nagy mit den werkstätten des bauhauses"

## Section 5 — Aula: Multi-Purpose Hall, Workshop-Produced Seating

### Argument Structure
**Thesis**: The Aula (assembly hall with stage) is the social heart of the Bauhausgebäude. Its back wall opens to the Speisesaal (dining room), making the combined space suitable for assemblies, lectures, and theatrical performances. The seating (Stahlrohrgestühl) was designed by Marcel Breuer and produced in the Bauhaus metal workshop. Lighting by Moholy-Nagy. Color design by the wall-painting workshop (Scheper). The hall is a complete Bauhaus interior.

**Evidence**: Abb. 56 shows the Aula in full seating configuration with stage opening visible at rear.

### Principles

**PRINCIPLE [P-10]**: Aula als flexibler Mehrzweckraum / Assembly Hall as Flexible Multi-Purpose Space
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: The Aula with its attached stage (anschließende Bühne) opens at the rear wall to the Speisesaal. This allows the combined space to serve assemblies (Versammlungen), lectures (Vorträge), and theatrical performances (Bühnenaufführungen). The folding wall (Harmonikätüren) between Aula and Speisesaal is the spatial mechanism of this flexibility.
- **Rule**: Design large communal spaces with operable walls to adjacent spaces, enabling programmatic combination. The single-purpose hall is a luxury — the multi-purpose hall achieves the same uses with less floor area.
- **Source**: p.66–67 (abb. 56, abb. 69)
- **Quote (DE)**: "die aula mit anschließender bühne, deren rückwand sich auch nach dem speisesaal öffnen läßt, dient für versammlungen, vorträge und bühnenaufführungen. diffuse, nicht blendende beleuchtung mit röhrenlampen. oben rechts im bild schlitz für film- und projektionsapparate."
- **Quote (EN)**: "The Aula with attached stage, whose rear wall can also open to the Speisesaal, serves for assemblies, lectures, and theatrical performances. Diffuse, non-glare lighting with tubular lamps. Upper right in the image: slot for film and projection apparatus."
- **Category**: function

**PRINCIPLE [P-11]**: Diffuse Beleuchtung / Non-Glare Diffuse Lighting for Assembly Spaces
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: The Aula uses diffuse, non-glare (nicht blendend) lighting with tubular lamps (Röhrenlampen). This is not merely an aesthetic choice — it is a functional requirement. In a space used for lectures and film projection, glare from direct light sources disrupts visibility and concentration. Diffuse light serves the program.
- **Rule**: In assembly, lecture, and performance spaces, specify diffuse non-glare lighting distributed across the ceiling plane. Light intensity and distribution follow the visual task, not decorative intent.
- **Source**: p.66 (abb. 56)
- **Category**: function

**PRINCIPLE [P-12]**: Stahlrohrgestühl / Steel Tube Seating
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: The seating of the Aula is Stahlrohrgestühl designed by Marcel Breuer (Tischlerei des Bauhauses — carpentry workshop). Steel tube chairs — thin-section structural members achieving a seat with minimal material. The same material logic as the iron window frames: thin profile, high performance, honest structure.
- **Rule**: Specify steel tube furniture for assembly seating — the structural logic of the thin tube applies at furniture scale as it does at building scale.
- **Source**: p.66 (abb. 56)
- **Quote (DE)**: "stahlrohrgestühl: marcel breuer. beleuchtung: metallwerkstatt des bauhauses (moholy-nagy). farbige gestaltung: wandmalerei-abteilung des bauhauses (scheper)"
- **Quote (EN)**: "Steel tube seating: Marcel Breuer. Lighting: metal workshop of the Bauhaus (Moholy-Nagy). Color design: wall-painting department of the Bauhaus (Scheper)"
- **Category**: workshop-integration

### Visual Evidence

## Section 7 — Workshop Spaces: Light, Productivity, Pedagogy

### Argument Structure
**Thesis**: The workshops of the Bauhaus are productive spaces first. The slogan that appears on page 68 is the clearest statement of the building's philosophy applied to the workspace: "gesunde, gut belichtete Arbeitsplätze steigern die Leistung!" — healthy, well-lit workplaces increase output. This is not a humanitarian argument alone: it is a functional argument. Better light = better work = better products.

**Evidence**: Abb. 59 (weaving workshop), Abb. 60–63 (metal workshop), Abb. 64 (carpentry workshop/Tischlereiwerkstatt), Abb. 65 (wall-painting workshop).

**Conclusion**: The workshops demonstrate the building's core productive argument. The glass curtain wall exists to deliver maximum even (north) light to the workshop benches. Unplastered ceiling surfaces in the weaving workshop (unverputzte, gewölbte Decken) are an honest expression of structure. Roof drainage at interior columns keeps the glass facade uninterrupted. All mechanical and structural systems serve the worker at the bench.

### Principles

**PRINCIPLE [P-13]**: Gut belichtete Arbeitsplätze / Well-Lit Workplaces as Productivity Principle
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: The quality of the workplace — health and light above all — directly increases productive output. This is stated as a building principle, not merely as welfare. "Gesunde, gut belichtete Arbeitsplätze steigern die Leistung!" The curtain wall glass facade of the workshop wing is the architectural instrument of this principle.
- **Rule**: Maximize natural light at every workstation. The glass wall exists for the worker at the bench — light is a production requirement, not a luxury.
- **Source**: p.69 (abb. 59, emphasized text)
- **Quote (DE)**: "gesunde, gut belichtete arbeitsplätze steigern die leistung!"
- **Quote (EN)**: "Healthy, well-lit workplaces increase output!"
- **Category**: function

**PRINCIPLE [P-14]**: Unverputzte Decken / Unplastered Ceiling as Honest Expression
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: In the weaving workshop, the ceiling is left unplastered (unverputzte, gewölbte Decken — unplastered, vaulted ceilings). The concrete structure is exposed. This is the interior expression of the same logic that produces the Vorhangfassade on the exterior: structure is not hidden behind finish, it is made legible.
- **Rule**: Leave structural ceilings unplastered in workshop and productive spaces. The structural material is the finish — honesty of construction is a design value, not merely a cost reduction.
- **Source**: p.69 (abb. 59, caption)
- **Quote (DE)**: "dachentwässerung an den inneren pfeilern. gesunde, gut belichtete arbeitsplätze steigern die leistung! unverputzte, gewölbte decken"
- **Quote (EN)**: "Roof drainage at the interior columns. Healthy, well-lit workplaces increase output! Unplastered, vaulted ceilings."
- **Category**: material

**PRINCIPLE [P-15]**: Vereinigung von Handwerk und Industrie / Unity of Craft and Industry Training
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: The separation of craft (Handwerk) and industrial (Industrie) training is illogical and outdated (unlogisch und überaltert). The old manual craft curriculum needs supplementation by a technical curriculum that communicates to every apprentice the foundations and interrelationships of modern industrial working methods. The Bauhaus workshops — as shown in the metal workshop photographs — are both craft studios and industrial training environments simultaneously.
- **Rule**: Design workshop spaces that can accommodate both hand tools and machines simultaneously, without hierarchy between the two. The workshop is the place where craft knowledge and industrial method are unified.
- **Source**: p.72 (text block, abb. 63)
- **Quote (DE)**: "die heutige trennung der nachwuchsschulung für handwerk einerseits und industrie andererseits ist unlogisch und überaltert. die alte manuelle handwerkslehre bedarf vielmehr der ergänzung durch eine technische lehre, die jedem lehrling die grundlagen und zusammenhänge der modernen werkmetoden der industrie übermittelt."
- **Quote (EN)**: "The present-day separation of apprentice training for craft on one side and industry on the other is illogical and outdated. The old manual craft curriculum needs rather the supplement of a technical curriculum that communicates to every apprentice the foundations and interrelationships of the modern working methods of industry."
- **Category**: pedagogy

### Visual Evidence

**FIGURE [Fig-11]**: Bauhausgebäude — Weaving workshop (Weberei-Werkstatt) (abb. 59)
- **Shows**: Large interior photograph of the weaving workshop. The space is wide and column-free in the working zone; round concrete columns visible at right. ## Section 8 — Teaching Spaces: Vorlehre and Architecture Drawing Rooms

### Argument Structure
**Thesis**: The teaching spaces of the Bauhausgebäude are differentiated by their functional requirements but unified by their architectural vocabulary. The Vorlehre (preliminary course) drawing room uses the full ceiling height with large arched openings. The architecture drawing room uses a continuous window band (fortlaufendes Fensterband) for even light across all drawing tables.

**Evidence**: Abb. 66 (Vorlehre work and drawing room), Abb. 67 (architecture drawing room, Zeichensaal).

### Principles

**PRINCIPLE [P-16]**: Fortlaufendes Fensterband / Continuous Window Band for Even Illumination
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: The architecture drawing room (Zeichensaal der Architekturabteilung) achieves gleichmäßige Belichtung (even illumination) at all workplaces through a fortlaufendes Fensterband — a continuous horizontal band of windows running the full length of the room. This eliminates contrast between well-lit and poorly-lit positions. Every drawing table is equally illuminated.
- **Rule**: In a drawing studio, use a continuous window band at a consistent sill height rather than individual windows. Continuous glazing produces even illumination across all workplaces; individual windows produce alternating zones of light and shadow.
- **Source**: p.76 (abb. 67)
- **Quote (DE)**: "zeichensaal der architekturabteilung, links trennwand zum flur aus schränken für zeichnungen und baumaterialmuster. gleichmäßige belichtung der arbeitsplätze durch fortlaufendes fensterband."
- **Quote (EN)**: "Drawing room of the architecture department, left: partition wall to the corridor made of cabinets for drawings and building material samples. Even illumination of workplaces through continuous window band."
- **Category**: function

**PRINCIPLE [P-17]**: Trennwand aus Schränken / Partition Wall as Built-In Storage
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: The partition wall separating the architecture drawing room from the corridor (Trennwand zum Flur) is formed entirely from built-in storage cabinets for drawings and building material samples. The wall is not a passive spatial divider — it is active storage. The partition performs two functions simultaneously: spatial separation and organized storage.
- **Rule**: Where a partition wall is required, replace it with built-in storage cabinets. The partition is the furniture; the furniture is the partition. No space is allocated to walls that do not also work as storage.
- **Source**: p.76 (abb. 67)
- **Category**: function

### Visual Evidence

**FIGURE [Fig-18]**: Bauhausgebäude — Vorlehre work and drawing room (abb. 66)
- **Shows**: Interior photograph of the Vorlehre (preliminary course) work and drawing room. The space is high-ceilinged with large arched window openings on two sides — the full glazed facade of the workshop wing is visible. Long trestle tables and stools are the only furniture. Industrial pendant lamps hang from a structural rail. The columns of the workshop wing are visible at the perimeter. The space is generous, neutral, and entirely organized around multiple light sources from large windows.
- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: P-13 (maximum light at workplaces). The Vorlehre room uses the full structural bay of the workshop wing — it is not subdivided. The open plan serves the exploratory nature of the preliminary course.
- **Source**: p.75

**FIGURE [Fig-19]**: Bauhausgebäude — Architecture drawing room (Zeichensaal) (abb. 67)
- **Shows**: Interior photograph of the architecture drawing room taken along its long axis. Drafting tables with T-squares and drawing instruments at regular intervals. The left wall is entirely the built-in cabinet partition to the corridor. The right wall is the continuous window band running the full length — all drawing tables face the window. Pendant lamps supplement the window light. Roller blinds are visible on the windows for glare control.
- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: P-16 (continuous window band for even illumination) and P-17 (partition as storage wall). Every spatial decision in this room serves the draughtsman at the table.
- **Text reference**: Caption gives the full spatial description.
- **Source**: p.76

## Section 9 — Speisesaal (Dining Room): Social Physiology and Workshop Furniture

### Argument Structure
**Thesis**: The design of the Speisesaal is grounded in a physiological and social argument: the person who builds must, beyond technical knowledge, understand the special design questions of space; these arise from the natural physiological facts of the genus human. They are secondary demands of race, nation, and individual, subsumed under these primary physiological facts. The Speisesaal is designed for human physiology — the appropriate scale, the right light, the folding wall to the stage.

**Evidence**: Abb. 68 (food service counter with iron Kippflügel windows visible in background), Abb. 69 (full view of the Speisesaal with Breuer steel tube chairs and tables, Harmonikätüren to stage visible at rear).

### Principles

**PRINCIPLE [P-18]**: Physiologische Grundlage des Raumes / Physiological Foundation of Space
- **Statement** [CERTAIN]: The building designer must know, beyond technical ability, the special design questions of space (die besonderen Gestaltungsfragen des Raumes). These arise from the natural physiological facts of human beings. They are the primary demands; the secondary demands of race, nation, and individual are subordinate to these physiological realities.
- **Rule**: Ground every spatial design decision in the physiological facts of the human body — scale, light, air, acoustic, thermal comfort. The body is the measure of the room, not the other way around.
- **Source**: p.77 (text block, abb. 69)
- **Quote (DE)**: "der bauende mensch muß über technisches können hinaus die besonderen gestaltungsfragen des raumes kennen, deren mittel entspringen den natürlichen fysiologischen tatsachen des genus mensch; sie sind den sekundären forderungen der rasse, der nation, des individuuums übergeordnet."
- **Quote (EN)**: "The building person must know, beyond technical ability, the special design questions of space, whose means arise from the natural physiological facts of the genus human; they are superior to the secondary demands of race, nation, and the individual."
- **Category**: pedagogy

**PRINCIPLE [P-19]**: Harmonikätüren / Folding Partition Wall for Spatial Flexibility
- **Statement** [INFERRED]: The rear wall of the Speisesaal opens to the Aula stage through white and black Harmonikätüren (folding/accordion doors). These allow the Speisesaal and Aula to be combined into a single large space for performances and events. The folding door is the spatial mechanism of programmatic flexibility.
- **Rule**: Use folding partition walls (Harmonikätüren) to connect adjacent large rooms whose programs are sometimes complementary and sometimes independent. The folding wall turns two rooms into one without constructing a third.
- **Source**: p.77 (abb. 69, caption)
- **Quote (DE)**: "im hintergrund weiße und schwarze harmonikätüren zur bühne"
- **Quote (EN)**: "in the background: white and black accordion doors to the stage"
- **Category**: function

### Visual Evidence

**FIGURE [Fig-20]**: Bauhausgebäude — Food service counter, Speisesaal (abb. 68)
- **Shows**: Atmospheric photograph of three students at the food service counter of the Speisesaal. The photograph is taken from the serving side, looking out through the pass-through at the students. In the background: the Kippflügel iron casements of the Speisesaal windows are clearly visible open, admitting air and light. The counter is dark-surfaced wood. The students are informal, animated — the photograph captures the social life of the building.
- **Demonstrates** [INFERRED]: P-06 (Kippflügel ventilation in everyday use — the windows are open, ventilating the dining space during service). The Speisesaal is designed for its social use: the open window behind the counter connects the interior social scene to the exterior.
- **Text reference**: Caption: "speiseausgabe im speisersaal, im hintergrund die kippflügel der eisernen fenster sichtbar"
- **Source**: p.77 (abb. 68)

**FIGURE [Fig-21]**: Bauhausgebäude — Speisesaal (dining room) (abb. 69)
- **Shows**: Interior photograph of the Speisesaal in full layout. Long white-topped tables with Breuer steel tube chairs in rows. The room has generous proportions — double-height or very tall ceiling. At the rear: the folding Harmonikätüren (white and black) to the stage are closed. Lighting by Moholy-Nagy/metal workshop (pendant lamps). Color design by Scheper/wall-painting workshop. The room is austere and social simultaneously: no ornament, but human scale and generous light.
- **Demonstrates** [CERTAIN]: P-10 (Speisesaal opens to Aula stage — the Harmonikätüren are the visible mechanism), P-12 (Breuer Stahlrohrgestühl), P-09 (workshop-produced furniture, lighting, color), P-18 (physiologically grounded space: appropriate scale, light, air).
- **Text reference**: Caption lists all workshop contributors. Text states the physiological principle of spatial design.
- **Source**: p.77
