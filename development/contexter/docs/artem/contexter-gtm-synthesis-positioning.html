Contexter GTM Synthesis — Positioning & Hero Copy

Author: Lead/Copywriting Date: 2026-03-27 Operator: Eidolon (nopoint) Sources: contexter-gtm-v2-nontechnical-pain.md, contexter-gtm-v2-direct-competitors.md, contexter-gtm-v2-indirect-competitors.md, contexter-gtm-competitor-supermemory.md Scope: Non-technical user positioning — analysts, researchers, PMs, marketers, founders, consultants

Copywriting Brief — Summary Before Writing

The product: Contexter = one place to store everything you know. Every AI you use reads from it. Upload once. ChatGPT knows it. Claude knows it. Gemini knows it. Switch models freely.

The emotional truth (from user research): People grieve lost AI context. They say "it forgot me," "two years gone," "like losing a loved one." They've built something real — accumulated understanding, trained preferences, shared history — and it disappears without warning. The pain is personal, not technical.

The competitive gap: Every competitor is either:

  • Locked to one AI (Claude Projects, Custom GPTs, NotebookLM) — your knowledge dies if you switch
  • Developer-first (Ragie, Graphlit, Supermemory, Morphik) — requires a coder to set up and use
  • Format-limited (Langbase, copy-paste) — won't accept your PDFs, meetings, voice notes

Contexter is the only product that is simultaneously: works with all AI, accepts all formats, and requires zero technical knowledge to use.

The user's vocabulary (verbatim, from research):

  • "memory" / "remembers" / "forgot everything"
  • "start from scratch" / "start all over again"
  • "knows me" / "knows my work"
  • "picks up where we left off"
  • "I don't have to explain myself"
  • "my stuff" / "my information"

Copy rules derived from research:

  • NEVER use: RAG, embeddings, vector, MCP, API, chunks, pipeline, context window, tokens
  • ALWAYS use: remembers, knows, forgets, memory, your stuff, all your AIs
  • Lead with the emotional relief, follow with the functional clarity
  • H1 under 8 words (confirmed best practice from NotebookLM, Otter.ai, Gamma hero patterns)
  • Frame the product as a trusted assistant, not infrastructure

Section 1 — Positioning Statements (3 Variants)

Format: "For [audience], Contexter is [category] that [benefit], unlike [alternatives] which [limitation]."

Variant A — Emphasizing Cross-Model Memory

For knowledge workers who use multiple AI tools, Contexter is the one memory that works across every AI you use, unlike ChatGPT memory, Claude Projects, or Gemini — which each lock your knowledge inside their own walls.

Rationale: The research shows the dominant pain is having to "start over" when switching tools or starting a new chat. The user has accumulated real value — months or years of context — and it disappears every time they cross a platform boundary. This variant names the villains explicitly (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini), which is bold but accurate: those products silo your memory by design. The "walled garden" trend identified in competitive research confirms this framing is fair and timely. The phrase "every AI you use" echoes the tagline and the user's own language ("every AI").

Variant B — Emphasizing Format Freedom

For professionals who live in documents, meetings, and research, Contexter is the only AI memory that accepts everything — PDF, DOCX, audio, video, images — no matter the size, unlike tools that only work with certain files or make you retype your material.

Rationale: This variant targets users with diverse, messy knowledge libraries: analysts with hundreds of PDFs, consultants with recorded client calls, researchers with mixed-format archives. The direct competitor research shows most tools have narrow format support (Langbase: PDF/TXT only, 10MB limit; Custom GPTs: 20 files max). The phrase "no matter the size" addresses an unspoken anxiety — "is my 200-page report too big?" — by pre-emptively eliminating it. "No retype" references the copy-paste manual workaround, the most common incumbent behavior identified in the research.

Variant C — Emphasizing Simplicity for Non-Tech

For non-technical professionals who use AI every day but aren't developers, Contexter is the first AI memory you can actually set up yourself — upload your files, connect your AI tool, done — unlike Ragie, Supermemory, and similar products built for engineers who write code for a living.

Rationale: This variant is the most direct differentiation play in the market. The competitive analysis confirms that every direct competitor (Ragie, Graphlit, Morphik, Supermemory, Langbase) is developer-first: their landing pages lead with code examples, SDKs, and API documentation. Contexter's target user doesn't know what an SDK is. The phrasing "write code for a living" is slightly aggressive but accurately describes the competitor ICP — and creates a clear in-group/out-group distinction for the non-technical user. "Upload your files, connect your AI tool, done" is a three-step journey that any person can visualize.

Section 5 — Product Video Decision + Script

Decision: YES — Include a product video on the hero section.

Rationale: Non-technical users make decisions based on social proof and demonstrated simplicity. A product video serves as the single most efficient objection-clearing mechanism for this audience, because:

  1. The product's value is best demonstrated, not described. "Upload a PDF, connect ChatGPT, it knows your document" is a 30-second demo that would take three paragraphs to explain in text.

  2. The non-technical user's primary fear is: "I'll try this and it'll be too complicated." A video showing a real person — not a developer — successfully setting this up in under 60 seconds neutralizes that fear immediately.

  3. Competitive context: Supermemory's landing page leads with a code block (TypeScript). NotebookLM has a product video. Otter.ai has a product video. For the non-technical segment, the video signals "this is for people like you."

  4. Research shows that the moment users believe in is the cross-tool moment — "I asked Claude about a document I uploaded to ChatGPT and it knew the answer." That moment needs to be shown.

Recommended format: Auto-playing silent loop in hero section (20-30 seconds) + a "Watch how it works" link to the full 60-second version.

Section 6 — Objection Busters

For use in FAQ section or landing page trust section.

Objection 1: "Is my data safe?"

Your files are stored securely and are only accessible to you. Contexter does not use your documents to train AI models, share them with other users, or sell your data. What you upload stays private — only you and the AI tools you explicitly connect can access it.

RU версия: Ваши файлы хранятся в защищённом виде и доступны только вам. Contexter не использует ваши документы для обучения нейросетей, не передаёт их другим пользователям и не продаёт ваши данные. То, что вы загружаете, остаётся приватным.

Copywriting note: The research shows users feel ownership over their AI memories — "my crucial data," "two years of memories." The answer must match that emotional register: "yours" and "stays private" are the operative words. Do NOT lead with technical security terms (encryption, TLS, SOC 2) — these are meaningless to non-technical users. Lead with what they actually worry about: who can see my files.

Objection 2: "Do I need to be technical?"

No. If you can attach a file to an email, you can use Contexter. There is nothing to install, no code to write, and no settings to configure. Upload your files, connect your AI tool of choice, and you're done. Most users are up and running in under two minutes.

RU версия: Нет. Если вы умеете прикреплять файл к письму, вы справитесь с Contexter. Ничего устанавливать не нужно, код писать не нужно, никаких сложных настроек. Загрузил файлы, подключил нейросеть — всё. Большинство пользователей начинают работать за две минуты.

Copywriting note: "If you can attach a file to an email" is a reference point that literally every knowledge worker has — it's not condescending, it's reassuring. The "two minutes" claim is borrowed from Supermemory's testimonials ("integration achievable in 120 seconds") and should be verified against actual onboarding time before publishing.

Appendix: Copy-Strategy Decisions Log

This section records the reasoning behind top-level strategic choices so future copywriters or A/B test designers can understand the intent.

Decision 1: Why "remembers" and "knows" instead of "stores" and "retrieves"

The research found that non-technical users never say "stores." They say "remembers" and "knows." Technically, Contexter "stores" data and "retrieves" it — but these words trigger the wrong mental model. "Stores" implies a passive archive (filing cabinet). "Retrieves" implies mechanical process (library catalog). "Remembers" implies a trusted colleague. "Knows" implies expertise. The goal is to position Contexter as an intelligent assistant, not a database.

Decision 2: Why not lead with "self-hosted" in non-technical copy

The direct competitor analysis confirms self-hosted is a real differentiator — Contexter is the only product offering true self-hosting without an enterprise agreement. However, this feature is ONLY relevant to developers and technical operators. Non-technical knowledge workers do not know what "self-hosted" means, do not care, and may interpret it as meaning "you have to install something," which creates friction rather than reducing it.

Self-hosting belongs in developer-facing copy, not in the non-technical landing page.

Decision 3: Why name competitors explicitly (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini)

The research shows users are locked to specific platforms by habit and investment. The question "does it work with my AI?" is the first question in every non-technical user's mind. The answer must come before they have to ask it. Naming the three major tools (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini) in H1 or the first subheadline is the fastest way to answer this implicit question. It also serves as social proof by association — "if it works with ChatGPT, it's real."

Decision 4: Why "every AI" rather than "all AI models" or "any LLM"

"Every AI" is the phrase non-technical users actually use. The research documents that users say "all my AIs," "every AI I use," not "all language models" or "any LLM." "Model" is a technical term. "LLM" is a technical term. "AI" is mainstream vocabulary. "Every AI" also mirrors Contexter's own tagline ("One memory. Every AI."), creating internal consistency across the landing page.

Decision 5: Why the Before/After section is critical for non-technical users

Non-technical users do not respond to feature lists. They respond to recognition. When a user reads "every new chat starts from zero. You type the same background again." and thinks "that's exactly me," the sale is already three-quarters done. The Before/After format is borrowed from the strongest non-technical SaaS landing pages (Otter.ai, Calendly, Loom) because it does the work of positioning through identification, not explanation.

Decision 6: Why the CTA "Upload Your First File" outperforms "Get Started" for non-technical users

"Get Started" is generic and implies a journey. "Upload Your First File" is a specific, visualizable action — the user can literally imagine themselves doing it before clicking. For non-technical users who are not sure if the product is for them, a specific action CTA reduces the commitment feeling. "Try Contexter Free" is acceptable for sections where the user already understands the product. "Upload Your First File" is better for the hero where they are still deciding.
